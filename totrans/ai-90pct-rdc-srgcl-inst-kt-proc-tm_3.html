The Healthcare Landscape

To make an effective entry, Medloaner required an in-depth understanding of the dynamics of the US healthcare system, including organization structures and incentives, attitudes to innovation, and regulatory concerns.

In brief

  • Operational innovations lag behind clinical ones by at least 15-20 years. Data exists but is fragmented, and the vertical structure of hospital departments presents the challenge of organizational silos. Kip found through his user interviews that there is an overwhelming desire for innovation here.
  • Fear of HIPAA (or equivalent) regulations abounds. The high cost of compliance is a significant barrier to AI adoption and innovation in healthcare.
  • The current US healthcare system is too focused on short-term revenue wins. Hospitals are resistant to adopting technologies that improve efficiency without driving revenue directly.
  • There is no data economy, even though the data is available. Kip contrasts this with the advertising technology industry, citing identity vendors like LiveRamp, which claims to be “the industry’s only interoperable platform for data collaboration across all cloud, walled garden, and media platforms”.

We still have those laggards in charge of divisions within organizations.”

– Kip Pogrebenko

The technology adoption lifecycle

According to this model (Everett Rogers, 1962), any new piece of technology is adopted in five stages as time goes on, by:

  • Innovators (2.5%)
  • Early adopters (13.5%)
  • Early majority (34%)
  • Late majority (34%)
  • Laggards (16%)

FIGURE 1: The technology adoption lifecycle.

The rate of adoption is governed by different factors. Rogers identifies five: relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability.

In some formulations, there is a “chasm” — popularized by Geoffrey A. Moore in his book, Crossing the Chasm – depicted between the early adopters and the early majority, reflecting the mentality gap between visionaries and pragmatists, who will only make the leap to adoption once they have seen other organizations and clients doing the same thing.

The problem of HIPAA

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of 1996 is a US law that guards the privacy and security of protected health information (PHI), among other things. The law amounts to a tight lock on the sharing of medical history. Data that contains identifiers that could link it to a specific patient cannot be shared, including names, addresses, and social security numbers.

HIPAA also means that subcontractors like Medloaner share liability for any data leakages, alongside healthcare entities. Healthcare-adjacent products and services carry additional costs because of the heavy regulatory burden needed to deliver them – which means attorneys are expensive. Kip says, “There are companies who work with electronic health records (EHRs), there are a couple of successful startups, but they are always short on cash because of HIPAA.” A 2017 report from Acritas found that legal departments of US companies spend the most in the world relative to revenue, and that “Pharma” and “Healthcare” legal spend rank 7th and 9th respectively out of 22 industries.

The reason why HIPAA is so tricky is that it is effectively use-case based, according to Kip. As a subcontractor, in your risk assessment, you need to be able to identify all possible use cases where potential data leakage can happen, which is a difficult task. Inevitable holes in your strategy can lead to fines and lawsuits, particularly following cyberattacks, which have been responsible for some of the biggest HIPAA violations in history.

The Surgical Instrument Problem

A surgical kit is an essential collection of medical instruments and tools carefully prepared to meet the specific requirements of a surgical procedure.

FIGURE 2: Components of a surgical kit. 1) Auxiliary tray 2) Graphic case 3) Standard plate tray 4) Reduction tray 5) Insertion tray 6) Screw rack.

HHS had identifiable inefficiencies in its Medical Device Reprocessing Department (MDRD) and its Sterile Processing Department (SPD), which rely on manual methods for tracking, cleaning, inspecting, and assembling surgical kits.

HHS hospitals use two different types of kit:

  • In-house kits, which are a permanent part of hospital inventory.
  • Loaner kits, which are vendor-managed and need to be ordered and returned after use.

Loaner kits bring cutting-edge technology to hospitals but have more complex handling requirements and create a higher operational burden: additional inspection steps, double sterilization (48-hour processing time per CSA standards), compliance checks before and after use, meticulous tracking, and expedited processing.

Problem outline

The existing MDRD workflow faces several key problems.

Less than full utilization of surgical kits: Hospitals track kits as a whole and not components. Medloaner discovered that 20% of the instruments in a surgical kit are never used but are still included, transported, and processed unnecessarily, leading to financial waste and storage inefficiencies.

Lost or misplaced instruments: 1-2 incorrect items or discrepancies are common in vendor-provided write-ups. On top of this, there can be a mix-up of tags in kits after the sterilization of instruments. Instruments can also be taken from other kits to supplement missing ones, without the change being recorded. Missing items lead to scavenger hunts, where MDRD staff must search for tools across departments.

STAT: One of Medloaner’s customers loses $11,000 every week due to lost or misplaced inventory.

Manual processes leading to errors and inefficiencies: The lack of digital tracking systems forces MDRD technicians to rely on manual processes for counting, assembling, disassembling, and sterilizing. This is time-consuming, error-prone, and contributes to mental fatigue. Technicians may also have to resterilize instruments that were not properly positioned during the decontamination process, which has a huge impact on workflow.

Operational delays due to different kit types and surgical needs. High-priority kits often need expedited processing, which disrupts planned workflows and strains resources. 9+ loaner trays may arrive for a single hip surgery, overwhelming MDRD staff.

If that department stops, not a single surgery will be performed. Even something as simple as removing stitches. It’s literally the heartbeat of the hospital.”

– Kip Pogrebenko

User interviews

In its quest to balance efficiency with cost effectiveness, Medloaner sought to understand the pain points and KPIs of stakeholders involved in the process.

FIGURE 3: Personas that Medloaner spoke to within HHS hospitals.

They identified 5 customer personas:

  • 3 personas within the MDRD: Technician, Supervisor, Manager. These personas generally cared about metrics like time to the OR (operating room) for surgical kits.
  • 1 persona within Procurement: Supply Chain Coordinator. This persona focused on metrics like kit utilization, instrument utilization, revenue per kit, and revenue per instrument.
  • 1 persona within the Operating Room (OR): Service Registered Nurse (SRN).

Internal champions: Understanding the MDR department

Kip paints us a picture of employees in the MDR department:

  • Essential to hospital function.
  • Technicians are overworked and undervalued. “Generally, it’s a low paying job, and they are asked to do at least twice more than they are supposed to do.”
  • Eager to embrace new technologies. “They kept talking continuously because they finally realized that you are the one who can make their life easier. They all want to innovate, but the resistance is huge because of silos in the organization.”
  • Potentially powerful internal champions.

Medloaner’s Solution

Getting around HIPAA: A novel data layer

Medloaner’s solution avoids using any information that would identify patients personally, and thus avoids having to deal with HIPAA entirely. The only information they collect about a given surgery is:

  • ① Surgery type: For example, orthopedic.
  • ② Surgery subcategory: For example, knee surgery or shoulder surgery.

On top of this, Medloaner’s key insight is to track and collect data about each ③ individual surgical instrument, rather than staying at the higher level of the surgical kit.

The needs of every hospital are different, of course, and they understood from user research that their solution must also be tailored to the ④ data management at each hospital, and the relationships between different software systems that hospitals utilize.

Together with an HHS internal team consisting of consultants, business analysts, data scientists, and data engineers, they designed a novel data layer that would bring all these datasets (① ② ③ ④) together. There is a system of checks in place to ensure that not a single PHI or patient ID, or any patient information, is leaked.

The rest of the technology is fed by this data layer.

A key insight: Instrument-level data

Kip describes a previous attempt at an instrument-level solution:

Someone I know implemented an RFID tracking system. Imagine you have to tag with a specific sticker every single component of the surgical kit, and imagine what will happen to those tags if they go through 120, 150°C decontamination. It’s either steam or, if it’s very delicate, it will be processed by specific chemicals, and after that it also has to go through sterilization. So often those tags get lost. That’s why that solution never took off.”

-- Kip Pogrebenko

It’s easy to see how Medloaner’s computer vision-based tracking model learns from this insight.

Without instrument-level tracking, there are commonly observed failure modes, which demonstrate that the devil is in the detail.

Failure mode #1

Technician 1 comes in and grabs the surgical kit that needs to be decontaminated. They disassemble everything and drop metal tags labeled #25 into all the compartments of that kit, and it goes to the washer. Technician 1 leaves.

Technician 2 comes in and sees the stack of tags labeled #25. They have no idea that this stack was already used. They grab the stack and drop them into another surgical kit.

Once the two kits come back from sterilization, they are all mixed up.

Failure mode #2

A surgical kit is ready to go to the OR. A nurse notices there is a critical instrument missing. They open any case in storage that may contain it. They grab it, pack it, stick it to the surgical kit with sports tape, and it goes to the OR.

The original set was missing that instrument, but it’s now also missing from another kit. But that change was never logged.

The instrument-level data collected continuously by Medloaner’s solution includes:

  • Instrument type
  • Instrument movements
  • Instrument vendor and manufacturer
  • Instrument positioning inside kit (outlines)

These data provide insight into:

  • Instrument utilization rates: Identifying frequently and infrequently used instruments to optimize kit configuration and reduce waste.
  • Inventory management: Improving tracking and reducing the frequency of lost or misplaced instruments.
  • Extent of decontamination and kit completeness: Assessing cleaning efficacy and automating completeness checks improves accuracy, mitigates the risk of human error, and reduces burden on MDRD technicians.
  • Workflow efficiency: Analyzing instrument flow to identify bottlenecks and areas for improvement, optimize inventory levels, and predict high-priority cases.

Medloaner’s proof-of-concept used AI and computer vision to realise these aims. The next few subtopics explore how this was done.

The model: AI-backed automation

Kip describes the two levels of the model that deal with different concepts:

We trained multiple models. We have a general model for just the type of device, and then it’s broken down by vendor and by manufacturer. This way we are able to recognize any single device, doesn’t matter where it sits. But then another higher-level layer of the model understands “a configuration of the set”.

– Kip Pogrebenko

The model is integrated with a perioperative resource (PR) management or inventory management system, so the system understands the shapes of all the specific instruments, puts them together, and helps the technician make sure that they sit on a proper kit tray in a proper location. Kip describes this as “your giant system of equations”.

The tech stack

For the model itself, Medloaner has been through a few refactors, with a core philosophy of avoiding commercial packages. They used:

  • OpenCV for the first proof of concept.
  • Open-source libraries from Meta for the second iteration, which allowed them to tackle the more challenging aspects of image recognition. Kip gives the example of screws on caddies that look the same from above but that are actually different lengths.

Difficult image recognition tasks Medloaner faces

  • Multi-level kits: Hard to see all instruments in a single image.
  • Varying instrument sizes: Hard to distinguish, for example, screws of different diameters.
  • Inconsistent reference images from vendors: Hard to tag instruments correctly.

In parallel, the team are building their own in-house solution based on the internal workings of the Meta libraries, so they can avoid vendor lock-in.

If Meta goes out of business tomorrow, who’s going to maintain those packages?”

– Kip Pogrebenko

For their novel data layer, Medloaner uses Amazon Redshift for data warehousing. They chose Redshift because of its position as an industry standard, but also because “it closely resembles a relational database management structure, but it also allows you to store unstructured data”. Alternatives to Redshift include SQL Server, MongoDB Atlas, Databricks, and Google BigQuery.

When we talk about data, generally speaking, you have structured and unstructured data. You want to have your data warehousing solution house in both types of data, because there are a lot of insights in unstructured data.”

– Kip Pogrebenko

“Now with the advances in AI technologies, it’s much easier to process unstructured data and create a structure from it according to your own requirements.” TechLeader has covered Lucidis AI’s solution to internal data chaos in another report, Generative AI for 80% Reduction in Work Order Processing Time.

Quantifiable improvements

It is early days, but Medloaner has been able to demonstrate some significant operational gains for HHS:

  • Time savings: Reduced kit processing time from 10 minutes to 1 minute per tray.
  • Cost efficiency: Lowered annual inventory verification costs by 90%, from $1.6M to $160K.
  • Reduced errors: Reduced human errors in tray assembly, preventing delays and improving surgical outcomes.
  • Enhanced productivity: Allowed MDRD technicians to focus on high-value tasks, boosting morale and efficiency.

It is important to remember that properly sterilized surgical kits also reduce the risk of infections and procedural complications. The implications of this technology are clear — they extend far beyond pure efficiency gains, into the realm of better patient safety. This is the nature of innovation in the healthcare industry.

Business Challenges

Challenge #1: Reluctant customers

In the early stages, armed with their initial software prototype, Medloaner spoke to a large number of healthcare groups in the US, as well as device manufacturers, device distributors, and group purchasing organizations (GPOs).

The #1 question that we were asked is, ‘How can we drive revenue with your technology?’”

– Kip Pogrebenko

They struggled to articulate a clear value proposition that resonated with these groups, who were primarily focused on revenue generation, and not interested in the more indirect effects implied by cost savings from Medloaner’s tech. They told Kip to come back when he had his first customer.

Solution #1: Reassess target geographies and go-to-market strategy

Medloaner took a pause while Kip hit the books. He took three Harvard Business School courses online and learned more about strategies for specialization.

RESOURCE: What they taught Kip at Harvard Business School (Online), at a cost of $1,850 each:

  • Disruptive Strategy
  • Business Strategy
  • Strategy Execution

Kip decided to shift Medloaner’s first region of focus to Canada rather than the US, because they operate under a publicly funded model and so — in theory — have a weaker emphasis on direct revenue.

“Canada doesn’t care about revenue. What the Canadian hospital system does, they forecast the number of surgeries, in our case per year, and they go to the government. The prices for the surgeries are roughly equal across all healthcare systems because they are government-subsidized. Depending on that forecast, the government will fund the hospital, and at the end of the year it has to file a report.”

Certain provinces use activity-based funding (ABF) models, under which funding may be increased or withdrawn in future budget allocations if a hospital performs more or fewer procedures than projected. Therefore, if a hospital bound by this model can make itself more efficient, it will see direct benefits to revenue. “For the Canadian market, an efficiency play is of the highest priority.”

Challenge #2: Learn the right lessons from competitor failures

Almost all Medloaner’s competitors are out of business. Among the remainder, one example is a UK-based startup, Scalpel AI, which secured funding in October 2024.

One difficulty that Kip rattles off is the expense associated with supporting hardware like cameras, and having to rely on third-party vendors here. Failed competitor solutions also added to the complexity of existing hospital logistics, introducing additional steps to processes rather than taking them away.

Some of them relied on third-party vendors in terms of equipment. All those frames with additional bolt-on cameras and video feed, and the surgical kit was supposed to slide underneath the cameras. They gave too much power to third-party vendors. Power of suppliers, it’s called.”

– Kip Pogrebenko

Porter’s Five Forces (1979)

This seminal framework from Harvard Business School professor and businessman, Michael Porter, defines the five competitive forces that govern dynamics in any industry. These are:

  • Rivalry between existing competitors, which is fed by:
  • Bargaining power of suppliers
  • Bargaining power of buyers
  • Threat of new entrants
  • Threat of substitute products

Undertaking a Five Forces analysis gives companies a firmer idea about their competitive position relative to the rest of the industry.

In particular, Kip highlights his closest competitor ORtelligence, the Lyft to Medloaner’s Uber, who also “ceased to exist” a few months before our interview. Medloaner invested a substantial amount of time into figuring out why, owing to the similarities between them: both companies had computer vision as their backbone, and they had independently built out all the same additional streams of revenue.

They discovered that ORtelligence wanted to charge an expensive onboarding fee, requiring hospitals to pay a $5,000 fee for each surgical kit, to cover the cost of imaging and analyzing each kit and training its model to recognize the instruments inside it. For the average hospital that may circulate hundreds of surgical kits, this proves prohibitive. “It’s not easy to go to the CIO of the healthcare group and justify that cost without [them] knowing if the technology is going to work or not.” This was a custom solution for each hospital, and not optimized for scalability.

Solution #2: Change the funding model

Medloaner took a different path, opting to rely on government grants and fundraising so they could chase an ultimate goal of building a comprehensive surgical kit image library, something that “from a financial point of view, no one [else] wanted to build and work off”. This allowed them to offer hospitals a more attractive value proposition with extremely low upfront costs, “virtually zero”.

Medloaner focused on rolling out pilot schemes to get their foot in the door, laying the foundations for a land and expand strategy, with the US as a slightly more distant goal.

Land and expand

Land and expand is a growth strategy that focuses on securing and delivering on a smaller initial deal with a client (“land”), then using this foothold to cross-sell and up-sell (“expand”).

FIGURE 5: A sketch of how land and expand achieves growth.

Customer retention is a key aspect of this strategy.

Towards a standard taxonomy

For now, Medloaner is focused on addressing the pain points of the HHS, analyzing high-moving and high-discrepancy surgical kits first – those used most frequently, and those most often missing instruments.

However, Kip talks with some zest about the potential of his comprehensive surgical kit image library, once it is built. His stretch goal is “to be able to recognize every single surgical kit eventually”. This is a possibility because every medical device in the world has a unique reference ID.

We are kind of creating a standard taxonomy, if you will. That taxonomy doesn’t exist today, but once you have a standard taxonomy, that’s your network effect.”

– Kip Pogrebenko

Network effects

Network effects come into play when the value of a product depends, usually positively, on how many users of the product there are. They can be direct or indirect.

The more hospitals Medloaner partners with, the more surgical instruments they will need to add to their image library, and the more useful the library will become — both for attracting new clients and for adding value to existing ones.

FIGURE 6: How Medloaner’s image library creates network effects.

Other standard taxonomies include the UN’s Food and Agriculture Organization’s AGRAVOC and the Dewey Decimal Classification (DDC).

Cross-Industry Perspective

Kip makes a range of cross-industry references over the course of our interview. He has many parallels to draw between attitudes to AI and innovation at various points in history, including:

  • The carriage industry’s resistance to automobiles (20th century).
  • Candlemakers’ resistance to electricity (19th century).

He reflects on how the role of bank tellers changed when the ATM came out, from people who “just count the money” to fully fledged customer representatives who can cross-sell. “Exactly the same will happen with AI and it’s going to happen in two years. In two years, there will be a new generation of knowledge workers who will be extremely accustomed to AI technologies, and they will not be able to see a future where these technologies are not in place.”

Those people who will drive the revolution, they don’t have purchasing power yet.”

– Kip Pogrebenko

Salesforce and Epic Systems

Most interesting is Kip’s comparison between Salesforce and Epic, as it pertains to the specifics of the healthcare industry in the US but also to Kip’s ideas about internal champions.

Kip cites how Salesforce achieved widespread adoption in the enterprise world. They did this, in part, by targeting mid-level sales managers with a simpler version of their software instead of only trying to sell directly to top executives. These managers became advocates for the software’s use within their organizations, leading to a bottom-up approach to adoption.

Epic Systems versus Salesforce

Epic is the dominant electronic health record system in US hospitals. According to its website, over 325 million people worldwide have a record with Epic.

Salesforce is the leading customer relationship management (CRM) platform.

Both systems require significant customization and integration effort to implement effectively.

“To give you a good understanding of what Epic is, it’s like a Salesforce. You can’t just deploy Salesforce and start using it. It requires a lot of manual effort, a lot of custom work. That’s why you have a huge economy around Salesforce. All sorts of contractors, subcontractors, companies that simplify your workflow and make your life easy.

“It’s the same with Epic. It’s not that easy to knock on Epic’s door unless someone from the healthcare organization knocks on that door on your behalf. So that was the challenge. We were super happy initially. ‘Oh, Epic. It should be easy.’ But it’s not that easy. It’s a serious gatekeeper to a lot of things.”

Medloaner knew they had to capitalize on their access to employees in the MDRD, technicians and managers alike — their deep, hands-on understanding of the problem of manual processing, their interaction with hospital systems day-to-day, and how automation could solve their problems. “We had to find internal champions and work with them. Once they understood, they became the driving force behind us.”

They had so many ideas! Like, oh, we can do this. We can do that. We can remove this stuff. We could deploy something like that. We could create a simple spreadsheet that would track this and that. It’s just mind-blowing.”

– Kip Pogrebenko

Medloaner’s Next Steps

Medloaner’s immediate focus is to finalize their minimum viable product (MVP), which will include:

  • Digitized instructions for surgical kit assembly, disassembly, and sterilization.
  • Instrument purchase order (PO) tracking.

With their MVP, they will aim to demonstrate five important metrics, including two emotional and social ones. A component of the solution not discussed in this report relates to social proof: to directly address the mental fatigue of technicians, Medloaner introduces gamification with features like leaderboards and rewards.

A short-term roadmap

  • An alpha launch across four HHS hospitals.
  • A beta launch for select healthcare organizations. Kip is currently in conversation with two large healthcare groups in the US, one of them the largest in Michigan State.
  • In parallel, Medloaner is building a comprehensive surgical kit image library, which will play an important role in its long-term strategy.

Disposable surgical kits: A stretch goal

Kip’s stretch goal, which he believes possible in three years, is the production of disposable surgical kits, from biodegradable materials like high-grade plastic.

These kits would target small, localized procedures like ACL replacements and would be offered at a fraction of the cost of traditional surgical kits. Kip sees this as a way to democratize access to surgical care in developing countries like Ukraine, where Kip hails from, and where the high cost of traditional surgical equipment is a major barrier to treatment. He also cites regions like Gaza, where the healthcare ecosystem may have to be re-built.

War-torn Ukraine cannot afford $150,000 per surgery using those standard surgical kits. What if we could offer disposable kits at a fraction of the cost, let’s say, $10,000?”

– Kip Pogrebenko

The majority of surgeries are orthopedic surgeries, according to Kip. They certainly account for a large portion of annual hospital revenue. Massachusetts healthcare analytics platform Definitive Healthcare found that there were over 1.4 million knee and hip arthroplasties — surgeries to restore joint function — performed in 2022 alone.

This means orthopedic surgeries are a prime target for medical device companies.

A Data Economy

In this report, we touch on the expansive topic of data economies.

Kip’s ultimate vision is for there to be an “Amazon of healthcare” — a data-driven marketplace that improves quality while reducing costs, something akin to LiveRamp for adtech. LiveRamp relies on “advanced encryption technology” and multi-party computation (MPC), a cryptographic technique, to protect its data.

For industry stakeholders and policy makers, Kip has these words:

Sharing means caring. If you allow healthcare organizations to share data for good and create necessary guardrails to ensure patient data is safe, it will be a tremendous value-add for society as a whole. So, they need to encourage healthcare businesses to try to deal with regulations – but the only way to encourage them to deal is to loosen them up, right?”

– Kip Pogrebenko

The challenges posed by laws like HIPAA are not easily overcome. Medloaner chose to sidestep these concerns and isolate their technology from all patient data. Despite this, they still had to persevere through many discussions about PHI. Kip does not see Medloaner’s approach as the only path.

The blockchain facilitator

Kip believes that blockchain technologies could be a long-term solution to the information security problem. By using blockchain to anonymize patient data while preserving its granularity, an extremely rich repository of information could be used for R&D without compromising patient privacy.

Think about disease prescription. All sorts of procedures, treatment plans, all that stuff. We are sitting on wells of information in EHR systems. But it’s so heavily guarded...we could have predicted COVID-19.”

– Kip Pogrebenko

Blockchain’s decentralized system would allow patients to control and potentially monetize their data. The financial burden of healthcare can be high, and could be offset by the value that an individual’s data provides to other parts of the healthcare system, for example as a small dividend or a discount on medical insurance. Kip imagines a data economy where researchers, healthcare providers, and patients can share and benefit from data in a secure and ethical manner.

Kip as mentor: A Ukrainian mammogram startup

“I mentor a couple of startups in a government incubator in Ukraine, and we are testing this idea with one company that created a mammogram scanner to identify whether or not a breast image has a benign or malign tumor.

The technology is ready to use, but now we are looking into how to integrate it, to create a marketplace like a matching engine between oncologists and patients. As a patient you can upload your scans, and then based on the result the system will match you with an oncologist based on historical data, the success rate of those doctors — this way, you create this data economy. The regulations are not as stringent in Ukraine. So, there is a way to experiment with that.”

Kip even proposes an actionable way ahead, a phased approach starting with less sensitive data, like for the common cold, bronchitis, or pneumonia, to test effectiveness and build trust.

This type of strategy could pave the way for wider adoption and demonstrate the benefits of a more data-driven healthcare economy to sceptics.

Conclusions

In its journey so far, Medloaner has demonstrated a willingness to understand its business context and adapt its strategies.

Kip’s team developed a technical solution to a regulatory problem that many potential entrants in healthcare see as prohibitive — but there are lessons to take away for tech leaders outside this industry as well.

  • Design around regulatory landscapes: Tech leaders should think carefully about how they want their solutions to interact with existing regulations, and explore workarounds that allow for progress. Medloaner has not yet cracked the US healthcare market, but their foundational work puts them in a position to meet the challenge smoothly when it arrives.
  • Find internal champions: Successfully implementing new technology, particularly in slow-innovating industries, can hinge on empowering internal champions — users at the ground level who can help you advocate for change.
  • Know your strategy: Medloaner pursued a disruptive strategy — building a library of surgical kits using government grants — which gives them a competitive advantage over competitors who may charge for onboarding, and sets them up for scale and network effects.
  • Think about data as a strategic asset: Medloaner’s case underscores the importance of viewing data collection and analysis as an opportunity to identify previously hidden problems and develop innovative solutions.

The business challenges around going to market, balancing short-term gain with long-term planning, approaching regulatory environments, and robust competitor analysis seem to outweigh the technical challenges in the particular case of Medloaner — a likely story for healthcare startups in general.

Current and future tech leaders in this space now have the challenge of unlocking a true data economy for healthcare.
