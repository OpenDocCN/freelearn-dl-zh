Introduction to ontologies and knowledge engineering

When we’ve talked about RAG so far, we’ve relied on vector stores (semantic embeddings) and, at times, keyword (sparse) search to retrieve context for large language models (LLMs). While this hybrid semantic plus keyword approach improves relevance, it can still struggle whenever you need precise reasoning, explainability, or tight factual grounding. The next evolutionary leap is graph-based RAG, which leverages knowledge graphs (KGs) to provide a structured, navigable context that dramatically enhances reliability, factuality, and multi-step inference.

What is an ontology?

An ontology is a formal, explicit representation of domain knowledge structured into defined classes, properties, and relationships. Unlike traditional relational databases or purely vector-based stores, ontologies offer richer semantics and explicit links between concepts, facilitating better information retrieval, inference, and domain-specific reasoning. For example, an ontology can explicitly encode business rules, regulatory constraints, and logical connections between financial entities, making it invaluable for precise knowledge retrieval.

Fundamentally, ontologies in KGs revolve around the same core graph concepts we introduced in Chapter 12: vertices (nodes), edges (relationships), paths, distances, and subgraphs. Nodes represent classes or individual instances, while edges define relationships among these nodes. Understanding paths and hops (connections between multiple nodes) allows agents to navigate and reason across complex networks of knowledge effectively.

The role of ontologies in agent architectures

Agents are inherently versatile and powerful, adept at performing diverse tasks simultaneously. The transformative progression from shallow learning models, once narrowly focused on singular tasks such as translation, named-entity recognition, or classification, to neural networks and ultimately transformers has expanded the capabilities of artificial intelligence significantly. Modern models, especially LLMs, excel at multiple tasks and are even merging previously separate domains, such as vision and language, into integrated multi-modal frameworks.

However, with retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), we revisit specialization. Here, our system will be designed to excel predominantly within a specific domain. Just as individuals specialize and become experts in particular fields, a well-planned AI agent will possess defined domain expertise to operate effectively. This domain expertise is central to an agent’s design, making the role of ontologies critical as they become the foundational backbone of the agent’s knowledge. Your agent has a specific goal and area it needs to be good at. We will step through how to properly plan and implement this domain expertise so that your agent will be set up for success!

Encoding domain expertise

Ontologies explicitly encode detailed domain knowledge, ensuring clarity and precision. To build an ontology, you define classes (such as organizations, financial instruments, and regulatory entities) and establish meaningful relationships and constraints using ontology languages. Capturing industry-specific expertise, such as the nuances of financial regulation or corporate structures, helps agents interpret complex user queries accurately and consistently.

To effectively embed this domain expertise into an agent through a KG, ontologies provide a structured framework that encapsulates both explicit factual information and implicit domain-specific logic. By clearly defining the semantic relationships among domain entities, an ontology facilitates accurate semantic inference, enabling the agent to derive new insights and answer complex queries beyond surface-level facts. For example, in financial compliance, an ontology can explicitly model the hierarchical relationship between regulatory frameworks, jurisdictional rules, and specific compliance obligations. This structured representation allows an agent to automatically infer compliance requirements applicable to different financial products or activities within various jurisdictions.

Ontology modeling languages: RDFS versus OWL

When building KGs, you need a way to define what types of things exist in your domain and how they relate to each other. While you could simply create ad hoc lists of nodes and relationships, standardized ontology languages provide crucial benefits: they enable automated reasoning (discovering implicit facts from explicit ones), ensure interoperability with other systems, and allow validation of data consistency.

Resource Description Framework Schema (RDFS) is a vocabulary modeling language that lets you define classes and properties for your KG. With RDFS, you can specify that Employee is a type of Person, or that worksFor is a relationship between people and companies. It provides basic constructs such as class hierarchies (subClassOf) and property hierarchies (subPropertyOf), making it suitable for straightforward taxonomic structures. When you use RDFS, the systems that use it can understand inheritance. If all employees are people, and all people have names, then all employees have names.

Web Ontology Language (OWL) extends RDFS with powerful logical capabilities. Where RDFS lets you say “employees are people,” OWL lets you express complex constraints such as “managers are employees who manage at least one person” or “no one can be both a company and a person.” OWL can specify that certain relationships are symmetric (if A knows B, then B knows A), transitive (if A supervises B and B supervises C, then A supervises C), or have cardinality restrictions (a person has exactly two biological parents). These features enable sophisticated automated reasoning, for instance, classifying individuals based on their properties or detecting logical inconsistencies in your data.

The choice between RDFS and OWL depends on your needs. RDFS suffices for simple hierarchical vocabularies and basic inference. When you need complex domain modeling, validation rules, or advanced reasoning capabilities, OWL becomes essential. Many projects start with RDFS for its simplicity and add OWL constructs as requirements grow more sophisticated.

In the following code lab, we’ll work hands-on with OWL as our modeling language. We’ll use Protégé, a specialized ontology editor that provides a visual interface for creating OWL ontologies and leverages built-in reasoners to validate our model and infer new knowledge. This practical experience will illuminate the power of OWL’s formal reasoning capabilities in creating robust domain models.

What is Protégé?

Think of Protégé as your workbench for shaping knowledge into something a machine can actually “think” with. It’s a free, open source tool that lets you build ontologies, classes, relationships, and annotations, in a clean, standards-friendly way (like OWL) without getting lost in the weeds. The payoff? When you’re ready to turn your ontology into a KG for Neo4j, everything slots neatly into place, meaning no messy conversions or lost meaning. And for conversational AI, that neatness really matters: it’s what lets your bot understand questions, follow relationships, and serve up sharp, context-aware answers instead of vague guesses.

Step 1 – define domain, scope, purpose, and competency questions

Define the domain: Before specifying your particular domain, start by identifying the core subject area and its boundaries. Ask yourself: “What is the central topic? What related concepts must be included for completeness? What adjacent areas can be excluded?” The domain for this ontology is financial instruments and their relationships within the securities market. This includes publicly traded stocks and bonds, the organizations that issue them, and the regulatory authorities that oversee them. We’re focusing specifically on the core entities that a financial AI assistant would need to understand ticker symbols, security types (equity versus debt instruments), issuing companies, and regulatory bodies, such as the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). This domain is intentionally narrow to keep the lab manageable while still demonstrating key ontology engineering principles.

  • Define the scope: To define scope effectively, consider the depth and breadth of coverage needed for your use cases. List what will be explicitly included and, equally important, what will be excluded. Think about the level of detail required. Will you need granular attributes or just high-level concepts? The scope of this ontology is limited to modeling the essential relationships between financial instruments, their issuers, and regulators. We include only publicly traded securities (stocks such as AAPL and MSFT, bonds such as USTB), major organizations (Apple Inc., Microsoft Corp., U.S. Treasury), and the SEC as our regulatory authority. We explicitly exclude complex financial products such as derivatives, options, or private equity, as well as detailed financial metrics such as prices, volumes, or historical data. This focused scope allows us to build a complete, working ontology that answers specific questions without getting lost in financial complexity. But the key here is to know the scope that you need for your application and to define it as precisely as possible.
  • Identify the purpose and users: Begin by clearly articulating why this ontology exists and who will benefit from it. Interview potential users or stakeholders to understand their needs, technical expertise, and the types of questions they’ll ask. Document both primary and secondary user groups, as this will influence your design choices regarding complexity and terminology. For this financial ontology, we will define the purpose as enabling a conversational AI assistant to accurately answer questions about financial instruments, their types, and their regulatory relationships. The primary users are individual investors conducting research for their investment decisions, who need quick, accurate answers about whether a ticker represents a stock or bond, which companies issue specific securities, and what regulatory oversight applies. By structuring this knowledge in an ontology, we ensure the AI can not only retrieve facts but also reason about relationships, i.e., it can understand, for instance, if something is a Stock, it’s also an Equity and therefore a FinancialInstrument, providing investors with both specific answers and contextual understanding.
  • Determine key use cases: To identify use cases, analyze the tasks users will perform and the questions they’ll ask. Create specific scenarios that demonstrate how the ontology will be used in practice. Group similar use cases together and prioritize them based on frequency and importance to ensure your ontology addresses the most critical needs first. The key use cases we will define for this ontology focus on classification and relationship queries that a financial AI assistant must handle. Users should be able to ask whether a given ticker represents a stock or bond, identify which organization issued a particular security, determine which regulatory authority oversees specific instruments, and retrieve lists of known stocks or bonds. These use cases drive our modeling decisions—ensuring we have clear class hierarchies (FinancialInstrument → Equity → Stock), explicit relationships (issuedBy, isRegulatedBy), and proper data properties (hasTicker) to support natural language queries about the financial domain.
  • Determine competency questions: Competency questions are the litmus test for your ontology – concrete questions that it must be able to answer. Formulate these as natural language queries that reflect real user needs. Make them specific and measurable, covering different types of queries (factual, classification, relationship-based) to ensure comprehensive coverage. These questions will later serve as test cases to validate your ontology’s completeness and correctness. At the heart of ontology engineering is first scoping the project, that is, defining what your agent needs to know and do. You must clearly outline the domain of knowledge and the specific tasks your AI agent will perform. In this lab, the focus is on answering the following:
    • Is AAPL a stock or bond?
    • What type of instrument is USTB?
    • Which authority regulates MSFT?
    • Which equities are regulated by the SEC, and who issues them?
    • What stocks do you know? What bonds?

These competency questions guide us in selecting the classes, relationships, and attributes that our ontology needs to support. Identifying these questions upfront ensures that when you later test the ontology, you’re not just modeling concepts, you’re modeling the behaviors that matter to your AI. You’ll build the structure to support these queries throughout this lab, and, in Chapter 14, you’ll import this ontology into Neo4j and use Cypher queries with a RAG system to actually answer these questions. The final step of the code lab in Chapter 14 demonstrates how all five competency questions are successfully answered using the financial knowledge graph you’re building here.

Step 4 – define properties and relationships

Before you open Protégé, decide on the schema, which refers to the properties that tie classes together and give meaning. This includes the following:

  • Data property: hasTicker—domain: FinancialInstrument, range: xsd:string
  • Object properties:
    1. issuedBy—connects FinancialInstrument → Organization
    2. isRegulatedBy—connects FinancialInstrument → RegulatoryAuthority
    3. ownedBy—links FinancialInstrument → Person or Organization

The domain specifies which classes can have a property (e.g., only FinancialInstruments can have a hasTicker property), while the range specifies what type of values the property can take (e.g., hasTicker must be a string, or issuedBy must point to an Organization).

By spelling out these attributes (domains and ranges), you’re ensuring that Protégé’s built-in reasoners can detect modeling errors whenever someone later creates assertions that conflict with your schema logic. With our classes and properties clearly defined, the next crucial decision is selecting the right ontology modeling language that can express these relationships with the appropriate level of formality and reasoning support.

Step 5 – choose an ontology modeling language

Protégé supports both RDFS and OWL, and each offers different levels of expressivity. For this lab, we select OWL 2 DL. As we discussed earlier, OWL strikes a pragmatic balance, allowing domain/range declarations and basic cardinality, and supports full reasoning, while RDFS is simpler but lacks the inferencing power to enforce the constraints we require for this ontology.

Choosing your ontology language is like picking the grammar for your domain logic. It shapes everything downstream. OWL 2 DL is the most widely compatible and semantically rich choice for our needs.

Basic Protégé terminology

In Protégé, “Individuals by class” refers to browsing or creating real-world examples (individuals, or “instances”) organized under specific classes (types or categories, such as Stock, Organization, or RegulatoryAuthority). The Individuals tab is where you actually see, select, and create these instances for a chosen class. Entities is a more general term covering all the main building blocks of your ontology: classes (types), properties (relationships and attributes), and individuals (instances). Assertions are statements that connect individuals to properties, such as saying AAPL (an individual) hasTicker “AAPL" (a data property assertion) or is issuedBy Apple_Inc (an object property assertion). So, classes are categories, individuals are examples, entities are the parts of your ontology, and assertions are the facts tying it all together.

Step 14 – enrichment and annotation

Adding a label (rdfs:label) and comment (rdfs:comment) to each class, such as Stock, is important for making your ontology both understandable and usable by people (and machines) who might use it in the future. The label provides a human-readable name for the class, which helps users quickly recognize what each class represents—even if the technical class name is less clear or uses code-like formatting. The comment gives a brief description or definition, explaining exactly what the class means in your model. This is especially helpful in larger ontologies, or when sharing your work, because it removes ambiguity and makes sure everyone has the same understanding of what a Stock is. Good labeling and commenting make your ontology self-documenting, easier to maintain, and more accessible for anyone who works with it.

For each class (starting with Stock), we will do the following:

  1. Click on the Entities tab, and then the Classes sub-tab. look for the list of classes down the left side.
  2. Select Stock.
  3. Look for the Annotations panel on the right side, select Click +, and add the following:
    • rdfs:label: Stock
    • definition: A security representing equity ownership in a corporation.
    • scope note: Includes only publicly traded equity securities; excluding private share classes (LP units, founder’s shares).
    • example: AAPL is a Stock concept instance with ticker ‘AAPL’.
    • hidden label: equity share
    • hidden label: common stock (add as a separate entry!)
    • Optional: alternative label: Share

The annotation properties definition, scope note, example, hidden label, and alternative label come from the SKOS vocabulary. Here is your chance to learn how to import a new ontology to help with your ontology development:

  • At the top of the Protégé window, click the Active Ontologies tab.
  • In the sidebar under Direct imports, click the small + plus button. (Yes—it’s in that tab.)
  • Choose Import an ontology from a document on the Web.
  • Enter this URL for SKOS-DL:

http://www.w3.org/TR/skos-reference/skos-owl1-dl.rdf

  • Click Continue.
  • It will check for the ontology, and if found, you can click Finish and continue.
  • Be sure to go back and add this to the Stock class: definition, scope note, example, hidden label, alternative label.

Note, when you add the SKOS ontology, the SKOS top-level classes become visible. SKOS defines these classes as the backbone of controlled vocabularies. We won’t dig too deep into this right now, but these can be helpful as you build out your ontology, so we will leave them and you can research and determine whether you want to use them in the future. Specifically:

  • Concept: Represents any idea or term
  • ConceptScheme: Groups sets of concepts (a vocabulary or thesaurus)
  • Collection: For arbitrary groupings of concepts (e.g., types of equity instruments)

Now do this for Bond:

  • rdfs:label: Bond
  • Definition: A debt security issued by governments or corporations, paying periodic interest and repaying principal at maturity.
  • Scope note: Includes fixed-income securities with defined term and coupon; excludes unsecured debt not structured as bonds.
  • Example: USTB is a Bond individual issued by the US Treasury and regulated by the SEC.
  • Hidden label: fixed income security
  • Hidden label: debt bond (add as a separate entry!)
  • Optional: alternative label: Bond Security

While it would be beneficial to add similar annotations to all classes in our ontology (Account, FinancialInstrument, Debt, Equity, Organization, RegulatoryAuthority, Person, and Transaction), we’ll skip annotating these remaining classes to keep this lab focused and manageable. In a production ontology, you would want to provide comprehensive annotations for every class to ensure clarity and maintainability.

Similarly, while we defined the ownedBy property in Step 10, we haven’t demonstrated its use in this lab. In a production ontology with Person individuals representing investors, you would use ownedBy to link financial instruments to their owners. For instance, asserting that John_Smith is owned by AAPL to represent an investor’s stock holdings. We’ve omitted this to keep the lab focused on the core ontology structure, but ownership relationships are essential in real-world financial knowledge graphs. Annotate individual stocks and bonds:

  • Click on the Individuals sub-tab near where you currently are in the Classes sub-tab.
  • Select the following individuals:
    • Annotate AAPL with rdfs:label = AAPL
    • Annotate MSFT with rdfs:label = MSFT
    • Annotate USTB with rdfs:label = USTB

Finally, save in the Turtle format. Go to File → Save and ensure the format is Turtle (.ttl). Save as FinancialOntology.ttl. We’ll use this exact file in Code Lab 14.1.
