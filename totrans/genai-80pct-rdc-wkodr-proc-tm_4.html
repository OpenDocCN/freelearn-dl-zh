The Data Chaos Problem

The pervasive nature of unstructured data

Unstructured data is a persistent issue across industries and a significant barrier to digital transformation initiatives. Unlike structured data, which resides neatly in databases or spreadsheets, unstructured data arrives in various formats — PDFs, DOCX, PowerPoint presentations, and images – making it difficult to process, analyze, and leverage efficiently.

Even traditionally structured formats like XLS and CSV often contain freeform text, images, and inconsistent formatting that challenge large-scale data processing. An estimated 80% of an organization’s data is unstructured (Automated Intelligence, 2023), making the issue widespread and increasingly complex.

Why this is a problem in the age of AI

A key challenge unstructured data poses is its incompatibility with automated systems and AI processing. Formats like PDFs often include embedded images, charts, and tables that cannot be easily converted into usable, structured data. This forces teams to resort to manual processes, such as copying and pasting data from documents, resulting in inefficiencies and introducing a high risk of human error.

When businesses rely on AI systems to automate processes and generate insights, the quality and structure of input data is critical. AI systems require structured, well-organized data to function effectively. Complex or poorly formatted unstructured data can lead to inaccurate outputs when fed into AI systems.

For example, AI models may misinterpret or fail to process key information, resulting in flawed insights or erroneous recommendations.

The long-term impact

The consequences of failing to manage unstructured data extend beyond immediate operational inefficiencies. Poorly processed data can lead to flawed AI outputs, creating systemic issues across an organization. Over time, this results in the accumulation of technical debt—a growing burden that, like financial debt, compounds and becomes increasingly difficult to address.

Technical debt caused by unstructured data slows innovation, increases costs, and hinders an organization’s ability to scale. Flawed or incomplete data inputs can lead to suboptimal AI-driven decisions, reducing the overall effectiveness of AI initiatives. As organizations increasingly rely on AI for automation and strategic decision-making, addressing unstructured data early in the process is crucial to avoid long-term inefficiencies.

By implementing structured data solutions, AI- powered workflows, and consistent preprocessing strategies, businesses can reduce technical debt and create a foundation for scalable, efficient AI systems.

When the source data is already digital — like spreadsheets or databases — transformation and augmenting processes, reporting, and analytics are relatively manageable.

However, the real challenge comes with unstructured data, which often arrives in various formats and through different delivery methods.

We call this problem data chaos. All these different formats of unstructured data are hindering companies from fully realizing their digital transformation efforts.”

The Black Dome Case Study

Who is Black Dome?

Black Dome Property Services specializes in property preservation for mortgage companies and financial institutions. These institutions often acquire properties through foreclosure, bankruptcy, or default, and are responsible for maintaining them until they can be sold to new owners. Black Dome handles tasks ranging from lawn care and snow removal to more extensive repairs, ensuring that properties remain in good condition throughout the foreclosure process.

What problems were Black Dome facing?

Inconsistent formatting: Black Dome’s primary challenge stemmed from the varied formats in which they received work order data. As Binni explained, “The data they received from various vendors came in different formats, because each vendor and client has their own system for generating housing data.” This inconsistency created a significant hurdle, as Black Dome had to manage multiple formats from different sources, complicating their ability to process the data efficiently.

Manual data entry and its consequences: Due to the lack of uniformity in data formats, Black Dome employees were forced to manually enter information from each document into their property and work order management system. This tedious process had several detrimental effects on the company:

  • Reduced efficiency and throughput: As Binni noted, “Employees were opening documents, cutting and pasting line by line into their application, which manages work orders and communicates with field reps.” This manual approach severely limited the number of work orders Black Dome could process in a given time. The bottleneck made it challenging to keep up with the volume of work, sometimes leading to processing delays.
  • Increased risk of human error: Manually re-entering data always carries a risk of mistakes. Employees could misread data, type incorrectly, or enter information into the wrong fields. This often led to inaccurate work orders, which could cause further delays or even result in errors that damaged properties.
  • Outsourcing costs: To manage the high volume of work orders, Black Dome had to outsource some of the data entry work, incurring additional expenses.

Specific pain points

Multiple work orders per property: Black Dome frequently managed properties that required multiple work orders for different tasks, such as lawn care, repairs, or cleaning. As Binni explained, “A single property might have multiple work orders, and each order could have 40 line items.” This dramatically increased the amount of data that needed to be manually processed, further slowing down operations.

Time-consuming data entry for each work order: Each work order took considerable time to process, with employees spending as much as 40 minutes per order manually inputting the data. “It was a time- consuming process,” said Binni, “Workers had to open the attachment, then open their application on another monitor, often using multiple screens. For each house, they manually copy and paste details like repairs, service categories, and locations from the document into the application across various screens. It’s a tedious, manual process.” This inefficient system placed a burden on Black Dome’s staff, reducing their ability to handle the growing workload.

Data Processing Automation for Your Business

In previous sections, we discussed the problem of unstructured data and its impact on organizational efficiency and AI-driven processes. We demonstrated how solving this problem has led to transformative gains for Black Dome. In this section, we introduce the concepts involved in implementing a similar solution in your own company, and outline some of the common tools used.

Any organization dealing with unstructured data can adopt a structured approach to automate data processing at scale, and ensure that their data pipelines are clean, accurate, and integrated with their existing systems. By utilizing a combination of AI techniques, optical character recognition (OCR), machine vision, and automation, companies can drastically increase efficiency and reduce costs.

The core steps

  1. Document ingestion: The first step is to set up a system that can ingest data in unstructured formats automatically. This can be achieved using a variety of tools or platforms that leverage optical character recognition (OCR), machine vision, and natural language processing (NLP) to capture and interpret content within these diverse file types. Intelligent document processing (IDP) and robotic process automation (RPA) solutions are often used to automate document ingestion and streamline workflows across departments.
  2. Document cleaning and standardization: Once the data is ingested, the next challenge is to clean it. This involves converting various unstructured formats into images and standardized, machine-readable formats. Machine vision can analyze document or image layouts and detect complex elements, such as tables, embedded images, and charts, ensuring they are accurately captured. OCR can then extract the text, images, and tables, converting them into manageable text-based formats. This step reduces noise and ensures that data is usable by downstream systems. Data deduplication may also occur during this stage to remove redundant data, reducing storage and processing costs. In cases where documents are in multiple languages or contain complex formatting, multilingual processing and normalization are used to ensure consistency.
  3. Data extraction and conversion: After standardization, companies should focus on extracting the most relevant data points from the documents. This involves identifying key fields, such as invoice numbers, dates, or specific metrics, and converting them into structured data formats. This ensures that only the essential data is retained for further processing, depending on business needs.

AI-powered extraction tools, often based on NLP and entity recognition models, can be employed to automatically detect and extract key fields. These models can learn and adapt over time, becoming more efficient with use. Custom-built AI models may be required to handle highly specific or complex document types, such as legal contracts or financial statements.

Optical character recognition (OCR) converts text from unstructured documents like PDFs or scanned images into machine-readable formats, making the data searchable and editable.

Machine vision complements OCR by analyzing image or document layouts, recognizing patterns, and identifying components like text, tables, and images.

Together, they enable accurate extraction of data from complex formats, turning unstructured documents into usable, structured information for further processing.

  1. Data structuring and serialization: Once the relevant data has been extracted, it must be structured in a way that allows it to be easily integrated into other systems. This often means serializing the data into formats such as JSON, XML, or YAML, which are widely used in modern APIs and system integrations. These formats ensure the data can seamlessly flow into enterprise systems like ERP or CRM tools. For relational data storage, SQL databases might be used, while NoSQL systems are preferred for flexible, unstructured data. Schema validation during serialization ensures the structured data aligns with the formats required by downstream systems, reducing integration errors.
  2. Data integration and workflow automation: Integrating cleaned and structured data into existing workflows is where real efficiency gains occur. Workflow automation tools, such as RPA platforms or integration Platform-as-a-Service (iPaaS) solutions, can automatically populate structured data into operational systems. This eliminates manual data entry, drastically reduces errors, increases throughput, and leads to significant time and cost savings. Advanced AI-driven automation can further enhance these workflows by adjusting data routing and processing based on real-time analytics and predefined business rules. For organizations with large-scale data needs, event-driven architectures (for example, Kafka or Amazon Kinesis) are used to handle real-time data flows across the enterprise.
  3. AI-powered insights and interactions: Once data is integrated and structured, companies can leverage AI-powered insights to enhance decision-making. AI models can analyze trends, forecast future outcomes, and provide actionable insights based on the extracted data. For example, businesses could deploy AI-powered chatbot interfaces that allow employees to interact with the structured data via natural language queries, making the data more accessible and enabling faster decision-making. Beyond chatbots, predictive analytics, intelligent agents, and automated decision-making tools can further enhance data usability. These tools can trigger actions such as ordering inventory, alerting users to anomalies, or making recommendations based on past data patterns.

Key considerations when solving the data chaos problem

Scalability: Solutions must scale as data volumes grow. Cloud-native architectures are essential for scalability, allowing companies to scale elastically as data demands increase. Microservices, containerization (Docker, Kubernetes), and serverless architectures enable flexible, on-demand scaling. Companies should consider serverless platforms (AWS Lambda, Microsoft Azure Functions, Google Cloud Functions) for event-driven workloads, allowing seamless processing without the overhead of managing infrastructure.

Security and compliance: As companies move toward AI-driven automation, ensuring compliance with regulations like GDPR, HIPAA, and CCPA is crucial. Implementing robust encryption, access control mechanisms, and ensuring data sovereignty (keeping data within specific geographical regions) helps maintain data security. Additionally, data lineage and audit trails allow organizations to track how data is used and processed, ensuring transparency and accountability.

Data governance and quality: A strong data governance framework ensures data consistency, ownership, and quality control across the organization. This includes implementing master data management (MDM) to manage core data entities and using data catalogs for transparency. Without proper governance, data can degrade over time, leading to inefficiencies and creating technical debt, which can hinder future automation and AI efforts.

Vectorization for AI systems

A key aspect of making unstructured data usable for AI-driven workflows is vectorization. Once the data is cleaned and structured, it must be converted into vectors — numerical representations of the data that AI models can process. This transformation enables the AI to understand and work with text or image data by converting it into a form that algorithms can analyze. Vectorization supports tasks such as document classification, semantic search, and natural language understanding. In addition, vectorized data can be stored in vector databases for efficient querying and retrieval, enabling AI models to provide contextually relevant results in real-time interactions.

Vectorization workflow

  1. Input document/text data: Start with a text document, which could be extracted from a PDF, webpage, or raw text file. This text can include paragraphs, sentences, or individual words in their raw, unprocessed form.
  2. Preprocessing: The raw text undergoes preprocessing. This includes:
    • Tokenization: Splitting text into tokens, typically words or subwords.
    • Lowercasing and stopword removal: Converting text to lowercase and removing irrelevant words like “the”, “is”, and “and”.
    • Lemmatization/Stemming: Reducing words to their base/root form (for example, “running” to “run”).
  3. Encoding with vectorization model: The preprocessed tokens are passed through a vectorization model to create numerical representations (vectors):
    • Word embeddings: Models like Word2Vec or GloVe generate dense vectors, capturing word meanings based on context.
    • Sentence embeddings: Models like BERT or Sentence-BERT are used to generate embeddings for entire sentences or phrases, capturing nuanced relationships between words sentences.
    • Vector dimensions: The result is a high- dimensional vector (for example, 300 or 768 dimensions) representing each token or sentence.
  4. Output vectors: The text is now represented as a vector (or series of vectors). Each vector has numerical values that encapsulate the meaning, context, and relationships within the text, allowing similarity comparisons and ML applications.
  5. Application: These vectors can now be used in various applications, like similarity search (comparing vectors to find semantically similar documents or sentences), clustering and classification (using vectors as input for machine learning models to classify or cluster data), or storage in a vector database (vectors can be stored in specialized databases like Pinecone for efficient retrieval and querying).

How this works in the context of Lucidis

Figure 4 illustrates the data processing pipeline for Lucidis’ solution. Here’s what’s happening:

  1. Input phase: Unstructured data is produced and sent to pre-cleaning storage before being ingested by Lucidis.
  2. Process phase: The document is ingested and converted into an image. Then, OCR, machine vision and NLP are used to clean the data and make it more accessible and valuable for downstream applications and AI systems.
    • Preprocessing: Machine vision standardizes documents and enhances image readability. It adjusts brightness, contrast, and alignment and segments text, images, and tables.
    • Text extraction (OCR): OCR converts text regions into digital, adapting to mixed content like faded text or handwriting and ignoring non-text elements identified by machine vision.
    • Post-OCR layout structuring: Machine vision refines text structure to identify paragraph breaks, alignment, and layout cues, helping keep the document’s original organization.
    • Semantic analysis: NLP organizes text by identifying headers, extracting entities, and categorizing content. It detects patterns and analyzes context, which aids in sentiment detection, topic identification, and summarization, helping shape data for different AI models.
    • Feedback loop: NLP flags any nonsensical text for reprocessing by OCR, while machine vision checks and suppresses erroneous text detections, enhancing accuracy.
  3. Output phase: After the data is cleaned, it is directed to different destinations depending on the use case and business need. Cleaned data can be fed into different kinds of databases or post-cleaning storage, or sent to AI applications like RAG models, traditional apps, or knowledge graphs. It can then be used for fine- tuning AI models or enhancing app intelligence.
  4. Linking with APIs: If the cleaned data needs to be integrated with downstream systems, it is converted into standardized formats like JSON, XML, or YAML for use with APIs. This allows the data to be used by enterprise applications like ERP or CRM systems, ensuring smooth interoperability across different software platforms.

Popular tools for every part of the process

By now, you should have a good understanding of the technologies involved in automating data cleaning initiatives. Now, we will discuss some tools used at each stage of the process, giving you a repository of resources should you decide to implement an in-house solution.

Document ingestion and cloud storage

Cloud storage setup: Use cloud storage solutions like AWS S3, Google Cloud Storage, or Azure Blob Storage to store incoming documents. Each document can be uploaded to a specific folder or bucket, triggering the processing pipeline. Folder- based triggers can also help sort by document type or priority level for better organization in larger workflows.

Event-driven triggers: Configure triggers (for example, AWS Lambda, Google Cloud Functions) to initiate processing automatically when new files are uploaded. These triggers start the automation workflow as soon as a document is placed in the designated storage.

Conversion to image and preprocessing

PDF to image conversion: Use libraries like pdf2image or tools like ImageMagick to convert PDFs to image files. These tools are also effective for handling large PDFs or documents with high resolution, allowing OCR and vision models to process complex, highly formatted documents.

Machine vision for document structure and quality enhancement: Machine vision algorithms detect document structure, such as headers, footers, and tables, helping segment the document for more precise OCR. Tools like OpenCV can detect basic document structures (edges, lines) to identify text blocks.

For advanced segmentation and image enhancement, models like UNet or Faster R- CNN can detect specific document zones or extract tables.

Preprocessing images: Clean up images using OpenCV and PIL (Python Imaging Library) to further optimize OCR accuracy. Machine vision models like EAST (Efficient and Accurate Scene Text Detector) can also improve the segmentation of text blocks, enhancing OCR outcomes.

Optical character recognition

OCR engine: Leverage OCR tools like Tesseract, PaddleOCR, or commercial options (Google Vision OCR, AWS Textract) to extract text from images.

Machine vision for region and layout detection: Detectron2 or LayoutLM can be used for layout analysis, identifying document regions (text, tables, images) and allowing OCR to focus on text areas. YOLO (You Only Look Once) can detect specific document elements, such as logos, stamps, or seals, that need separate processing.

Custom OCR models: For complex document layouts, consider training custom OCR models with TensorFlow or PyTorch. Machine vision models like CascadeTabNet can help accurately recognize structured data such as forms, tables, and invoices.

Data cleaning and standardization

Text parsing and cleaning: Use regular expressions (regex) and NLP tools, like spaCy or NLTK, to parse and clean extracted text.

Template-based data structuring: LayoutLM and Detectron2 can recognize specific layouts in frequently encountered document formats. Use layout analysis to match predefined extraction templates that map data to structured formats like JSON.

Template matching: OpenCV’s template-matching functions can automate structuring tasks by identifying visual cues and aligning data fields. Template matching can also detect changes in standard forms, flagging deviations from expected formats for quality checks.

Data validation and quality checks

Automated quality checks: Run quality assurance processes to validate the accuracy of extracted data. OpenCV can verify the presence and clarity of expected visual elements, such as logos or verification markers, by confirming alignment with document structure. Models like EAST or CascadeTabNet can flag structural discrepancies within tables and text blocks that don’t align with expected formats.

Human-in-the-loop verification: Use a web-based interface to route flagged data for manual validation. The interface can display confidence scores from OCR or machine vision models, guiding human reviewers on areas needing closer inspection. Machine vision assists by flagging elements that don’t meet structural expectations, improving the quality control process.

Data storage and API integration

Database selection: Store structured data in cloud databases (for example, MongoDB, Google BigQuery, or Azure Cosmos DB). BigQuery and Cosmos DB can scale with high availability, making them ideal for handling large datasets reliably.

API development: Build RESTFul APIs using cloud-managed services like AWS API Gateway or Google Cloud Endpoints to facilitate data transfer into downstream systems.

Workflow orchestration

Containerization and microservices: Containerize workflow components (for example, ingestion, OCR, parsing) with Docker for modularity, enabling isolated updates and streamlined deployment.

Orchestration with Kubernetes: Use managed Kubernetes services like AWS EKS, GCP GKE, or Azure AKS to manage containerized services and enable horizontal scaling.

Parallel processing: Implement parallel processing using task queues like Celery with RabbitMQ or Redis to process multiple documents simultaneously, which is crucial for handling large volumes efficiently. Redis can also serve as a message broker for Celery, helping to manage task queues.

Scheduled processing and monitoring

Scheduling tasks: Use schedulers like AWS EventBridge or Google Cloud Scheduler to automate document ingestion and processing at regular intervals.

Monitoring and logging: Set up monitoring with tools like Prometheus and Grafana to keep track of system health and processing times. Use logging services like AWS CloudWatch or GC Operations for real-time troubleshooting and analysis.

Security and compliance

Data encryption: Encrypt data at rest using cloud-native key management services like AWS KMS or Google Cloud KMS. Implement SSL/TLS for data in transit to protect sensitive information.

Access control and audit logs: Implement role-based access control (RBAC) using IAM policies (for example, AWS IAM or Google IAM) and enable audit logging to ensure compliance with standards such as HIPAA or GDPR.

Feedback loop for improved models

Continuous model updates: Store feedback and error data in a cloud database to retrain models automatically using scheduled machine learning workflows (for example, AWS SageMaker or Google AI Platform).

EAST and Detectron2 for layout updates: Machine vision models can be retrained on feedback if new document layouts or structures emerge, allowing the system to adapt to layout changes over time.

Challenges and Solutions

Challenge #1: Resource-intensive document processing

Challenge: Processing large and complex PDF documents can be very resource-intensive, even if the file size is relatively small. The challenge arises from the power-hungry processes of converting PDFs to images, using APIs for cleaning and data extraction, and handling the sub-processes involved, as Tobalo explains:

In the data world, you usually measure by big data – terabytes of data – but with PDFs, you’re dealing with something different. A 10 MB or 30 MB PDF may seem small, but processing it can be much more time-consuming than you’d expect. The challenge comes from the need to convert PDFs to images, make API calls for cleaning and data extraction, and handle all the sub-processes involved. So, it’s not just about processing megabytes – there are a lot of network traversals and the need for infrastructure to handle the vision models that extract the data. It’s far more complex than just measuring the data size in megabytes.”

-- Tobalo Torres

Solution: Lucidis relies on cloud solutions for GPU-intensive tasks, particularly AWS. This allows them to access powerful GPUs over the internet without needing physical hardware on- site. Graph RAG

One rising area we’re focusing on is knowledge graphs for enterprise knowledge bases. It’s not just about enabling chat with enterprise data but improving how we handle investigation, analysis, or assessment of specific documents.”

--Tobalo Torres

Graph RAG represents an evolution of retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), where a generative model like GPT retrieves relevant external information to support its responses. Unlike traditional RAG, which relies solely on text-based document retrieval, Graph RAG incorporates knowledge graphs into the retrieval process.

Knowledge graphs structure information as nodes and edges, representing entities and their relationships. This structured approach enables models to grasp not only isolated pieces of data but also the contextual relationships between them. For example, in legal or compliance settings, a knowledge graph can map connections between various laws, precedents, regulations, and involved entities (companies, individuals), creating a richly interconnected data landscape.

Figure 6 illustrates this in the context of the fictional law firm, ACME Corp. When a lawyer initiates a query — such as “Environmental case involving ACME Corp” — the system leverages a generative model to interpret keywords and identify relevant contexts, such as environmental regulations and entities like the EPA. This initial processing directs the search toward pertinent documents and relationships within the knowledge graph, aligning the retrieval process with the context of the query.

The dual-retrieval approach of Graph RAG combines text-based document retrieval with knowledge graph retrieval. While document retrieval captures contextual details, the knowledge graph pulls structured data to map relationships, such as connections between:

  • ACME Corp
  • Relevant compliance frameworks
  • Prior case law

The system extracts and merges entities and relationships from both sources, creating a well-rounded view of the case.

This synthesized information enables Graph RAG to generate a contextually enriched and precise response, providing nuanced insights that would otherwise require extensive manual research.

Agentic tooling

Lucidis plans to move beyond data cleaning, embedding, and vectorization by incorporating agentic tooling.

We’re currently exploring agentic tooling, applying AI beyond just cleaning, embedding, and vectorizing data. We’ve seen success with Property Preservation Wizard as a tool and are now aiming to extend these agentic methodologies in our solutions.”

-- Tobalo Torres

Agentic tooling refers to systems capable of acting independently, taking proactive actions based on their understanding of data and context. This approach goes beyond basic automation, where AI performs tasks like cleaning, embedding, and vectorizing data, and moves towards AI agents autonomously performing more complex tasks with pre- defined goals and learning from interactions.

Using the Black Dome case as an example, agentic tooling could enable the following:

Proactive data handling: Currently, Lucidis processes unstructured data and cleans it for downstream use. With agentic AI, the system could take the next step, autonomously deciding where and how to route cleaned data based on task requirements. For example, if a work order involves a common maintenance request, the agent could automatically assign it to the appropriate contractor based on past performance.

Autonomous task management: In the Property Preservation Wizard, the AI could go beyond generating work orders and invoices by scheduling repairs, updating timelines, and sending status updates to clients autonomously. This would transform the system into a dynamic task manager, reducing the workload for employees and increasing overall efficiency.

Enhanced decision-making: Lucidis could leverage agentic AI to analyze historical data and optimize workflows. For instance, by learning from past work orders, the AI could recommend or even initiate cost-saving measures, such as adjusting vendor assignments based on efficiency or historical performance data.

Integration with external tools: Extending agentic methodologies may also involve connecting Lucidis to third-party tools via APIs, where the AI autonomously initiates interactions with external systems. For instance, Lucidis could trigger automatic supply orders or notify contractors when a property task requires urgent attention based on live data from its workflows.

Cross-Industry Use Cases and the Future of Data Governance

While Lucidis’ primary focus has been on mortgage field services, its technology holds significant promise for any industry grappling with “data chaos”. Their long-term vision includes expanding into sectors with complex documentation and regulatory requirements, leveraging AI-powered solutions to streamline workflows and enhance data accuracy.

Data chaos is everywhere

Despite advances in digital transformation, many sectors continue to operate on legacy systems or handle data in inconsistent formats, making efficient data management challenging. Binni and Tobalo note that any organization managing large volumes of documents can benefit from AI-driven document cleaning and structuring.

Legal industry: Legal professionals face the daunting task of reviewing large quantities of contracts, case files, and compliance documents, often in unstructured formats like PDFs and scanned images. Machine vision and advanced OCR technologies can automate ingestion and extraction of essential data, such as clauses, dates, and terms, reducing manual review time and minimizing errors

  • Use case: Document review. Law firms can use these technologies to automatically flag relevant clauses or extract key dates and terms from contracts. Natural language querying allows professionals to quickly search across thousands of documents, boosting review efficiency and ensuring no critical details are overlooked.

As Tobalo explains, these solutions have broader applications that go beyond single firms:

There are groups like the Legal Defense Fund and other tertiary organizations that need to navigate a wide variety of legal codes, not to mention potential new legal codes integrated during legislative sessions. Having an enterprise knowledge base solution, like what we’re building, would allow them to access and manage all that information more efficiently.”

--Tobalo Torres

Finance: Financial institutions handle documents such as loan applications, financial statements, and regulatory filings, often arriving in various formats. Traditional processing requires manual data extraction and validation, which is time-consuming and prone to error. Using OCR and NLP, financial institutions can automate document processing, accelerating loan approvals, audits, and compliance checks while reducing human error.

  • Use case: Loan processing and auditing. An AI system could scan loan applications, extract pertinent information (credit scores, income data, debt obligations), and feed it into internal systems. API integrations with existing workflows allow financial institutions to increase operational efficiency without extensive system modifications.

Healthcare: Healthcare organizations manage vast amounts of unstructured data, including patient records, insurance claims, and lab reports. Manual entry into electronic health record (EHR) systems slows operations and introduces errors, impacting patient care. By using machine vision, OCR and NLP, healthcare providers can automate data extraction, making critical information readily accessible.

  • Use case: Patient record management and insurance claims. OCR-enabled systems can pull data from patient records, lab results, and insurance claims, structuring it for seamless integration into EHR systems. This accelerates record handling and improves patient outcomes by providing quicker access to medical information. In insurance, claims are processed faster, reducing turnaround times for patients and providers.

Public sector: Government agencies manage large quantities of public records, legal documents, and regulatory filings, often in unstructured or paper formats. Manual processing is labor- intensive, delays service delivery, and raises error risks. AI-powered document digitization and natural language search capabilities make public records more accessible and manageable.

  • Use case: Public records and compliance. AI tools would enable government agencies to automatically ingest and organize public records, allowing for quick, reliable access through natural language searches. This expedites decision- making, improves transparency, and enhances public service efficiency by allowing officials to locate relevant information across legislative documents, compliance reports, and public records.

Energy and utilities: Energy and utility companies are responsible for extensive documentation, including inspection reports, safety audits, and compliance filings. These documents, often unstructured and in various formats, require significant time and effort to process. Using OCR and machine vision, energy companies can automate data extraction, improving compliance and asset tracking.

  • Use case: Inspection and compliance. AI tools can scan inspection reports, extract compliance data, and feed it into asset management systems, enabling quick response to maintenance needs and adherence to safety standards. For example, an automated system can flag safety violations and prioritize urgent maintenance actions.

Manufacturing: Manufacturers, particularly in regulated fields like automotive and aerospace, face complex documentation for quality control and regulatory compliance. AI-powered document processing enables them to streamline audits and ensure each document contains the necessary compliance details.

  • Use case: Quality control documentation. Machine vision and OCR can check inspection records for critical information, flagging any inconsistencies. This enables quality managers to proactively address compliance issues before they impact production, ensuring smooth operations and minimizing regulatory risks.

Transportation and logistics: Transportation and logistics require substantial document management, such as bills of lading, freight invoices, and customs declarations. Manual processing can slow down shipments and increase regulatory risks.

  • Use case: Freight and customs processing. AI tools can scan and extract essential shipment details, like cargo content, origin, and destination, from bills of lading and customs forms. The extracted data can be integrated with logistics systems, allowing for real-time shipment tracking and streamlined customs processing, reducing transit times.

Retail and e-commerce: Retailers handle a vast array of supplier contracts, inventory lists, and customer returns, often in various formats. Processing these documents manually is labor- intensive and slows inventory management.

  • Use case: Supplier contract and inventory management. OCR and NLP can automate data extraction from supplier contracts, capturing product specs, terms, and renewal dates. The structured data can be used to optimize inventory levels and streamline supplier coordination, reducing stockouts and building customer satisfaction.

DIY versus All-in-One Solutions

From previous sections, you should now have a solid understanding of the core concepts and technologies involved in automating data processing pipelines to clean data for downstream applications and AI initiatives.

A key decision for companies is whether to build and maintain their solution in-house or to use an all-in-one solution, as Black Dome did with Lucidis. This choice involves a cost-benefit analysis and will depend on the resources and expertise within your business. Here are some key considerations.

Drawbacks of a DIY approach

Technical complexity: Developing a robust AI system for data governance requires significant expertise in areas like machine vision, NLP, and cloud infrastructure. Building and maintaining such a system in-house would require a skilled engineering team and ongoing investment in infrastructure.

Staying up-to-date: AI is a fast-evolving field, with new models and techniques emerging constantly. A DIY approach requires continuous research, development, and integration of advancements to remain competitive and effective.

Security and compliance: Ensuring data privacy and security, especially when handling sensitive information, is paramount. Building a secure and compliant system involves adhering to industry standards, conducting penetration testing, vulnerability assessments, and ongoing monitoring—all of which can be resource- intensive for organizations without specialized expertise.

Advantages of all-in-one solutions

Expertise and experience: All-in-one solutions come with a team of experts in AI and data management. These providers understand the nuances of handling unstructured data for specific business needs, cleaning and extracting information, and integrating with various systems and APIs, effectively addressing challenges that can overwhelm in-house teams.

Focus on core business: Outsourcing data governance allows organizations to focus on their core business operations rather than diverting resources to build and manage a complex AI system. This frees up internal teams to concentrate on their primary responsibilities, potentially leading to net increased efficiency and productivity.

Potential cost savings: Building an in-house solution can involve significant upfront investment in infrastructure, talent acquisition, and ongoing maintenance. All-in-one solutions offer a potentially more cost-effective alternative, particularly for organizations with limited resources or those seeking to avoid long-term capital expenditures.

Faster time-to-value: All-in-one solutions provide a pre-built, customizable solution that can be deployed relatively quickly. This allows organizations to realize the benefits of AI- driven data governance much sooner than if they were to embark on a lengthy in-house development process.

Advantages of a DIY approach

Full customization: With a DIY solution, organizations have complete control over the system, from data processing workflows to specific features. This enables a highly customized solution that aligns perfectly with unique business needs, particularly for companies with specialized processes requiring tailored configurations.

In-house expertise development: Building in-house cultivates deep technical expertise within the organization. Over time, this internal knowledge can lead to innovations and efficiencies that may not be achievable through external solutions.

Greater control over data and security: For handling highly sensitive data, a DIY approach allows data to stay entirely in- house. This can facilitate specific security protocols, data privacy requirements, and direct oversight of compliance with industry standards.

Potential for long-term cost efficiency: While requiring significant upfront investment, a DIY approach can be more cost-effective in the long run for organizations with stable data processing needs. This approach can eliminate recurring fees associated with subscription- based platforms and give the organization control over budget allocation for updates and expansions.

Flexibility in vendor and tool selection: A DIY approach allows organizations to choose the best individual tools, vendors, and technology components for their needs, rather than being limited to the tech stack of an all-in- one solution.

Who should consider each approach?

All-in-one solution: Ideal for organizations that are:

  • Prioritizing speed to market.
  • Lacking extensive AI and data processing expertise.
  • Preferring to focus resources on core business functions rather than in-house development.

An all-in- one solution is particularly beneficial for companies that need scalable, secure automation quickly without committing significant capital or time to development.

DIY solution: Suitable for organizations with:

  • Specialized requirements that pre-built solutions may not meet.
  • Robust internal AI and data engineering teams capable of managing continuous system updates.

Companies seeking full customization and control over their data governance infrastructure may find a DIY solution preferable, despite the associated resource demands.

A point for tech leaders to think carefully about is whether to manage their own infrastructure or rely on SaaS solutions. Many realize that SaaS is just fine as long as it meets compliance and security standards. It’s also about thinking strategically. How do you invest your resources in resolving technical debt? It ties back to data governance — how is data shared across departments like finance, sales, or HR?

Often, this is handled manually, but thinking more programmatically and having APIs in place, supported by a strong data pipeline and ontology, is crucial.”

-- Tobalo Torres

Key takeaway

While building an AI data governance solution in-house can be appealing to some organizations, the technical complexities, resource requirements, and need for constant adaptation to evolving technology can present significant challenges. Each organization should conduct a cost-benefit analysis to determine which path better suits its needs, keeping in mind that all-in-one solutions offer a streamlined, expert-led alternative for rapid implementation and growth.
