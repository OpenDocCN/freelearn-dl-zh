PowerPoint Live and Interactive Features in Microsoft Teams

If you present virtually, you might run into challenges while trying to figure out the best way to share your PowerPoint presentation. Yes, you could simply share your whole screen and use Presenter View , as discussed in the previous chapter. But then you might have a hard time seeing your meeting controls, especially if you are working with only one monitor.

With virtual presentations now part of our business lives, I wanted to include a chapter on leveraging Microsoft Teams’ PowerPoint Live and other features to create more interactions and engagement. Why Teams and not another virtual meeting tool? Simply because it is the application I have been using and have seen evolve since its introduction in 2017, and its ability to leverage many more features included in Microsoft 365.

To help you take your presentations to a new level using PowerPoint Live within Teams, here are the topics discussed in this chapter:

  • Getting started with and using PowerPoint Live
  • Creating and using breakout rooms
  • Using Q&A and Polls to create more interactions
  • Creating and using an avatar
  • Improving the camera, lighting, and microphone setup

Technical requirements

You need access to Microsoft Teams with a Microsoft 365 work or school license to have access to PowerPoint Live. Be aware that since Microsoft Teams, just like the subscription version of PowerPoint, is updated on an ongoing basis, it is possible that screenshots shown in this chapter might differ from your version of the application.

The Creating and using an avatar section has additional requirements:

  • A Teams Essentials or M365 Business / Enterprise license
  • The Teams desktop application
  • A decently powerful computer using Windows or macOS
  • The Avatars app to be allowed in the Teams admin center by IT

Getting started with and using PowerPoint Live

Trying to manage your PowerPoint presentation, participants, reactions, and the chat pane in Microsoft Teams can be quite challenging, especially if you only have one monitor connected to your computer. Even when you do have two monitors, handling the various overlapping windows can be overwhelming.

That is when the PowerPoint Live feature can help you. You will find it when clicking on the Share button ( 1 ) in your Teams meeting window ( Figure 14.1 ):

Figure 14.1 – Starting PowerPoint Live from the share screen feature in Teams

Let’s start by defining what sharing options are available:

  • Include sound ( 2 ): Make sure you toggle this option on if your presentation includes sound. This is the only way attendees will hear it.
  • The Presenter mode section ( 3 ):
    • Content only ( 4 ) will only display shared content.
    • Standout mode ( 5 ) overlaps your video feed on top of your slides.
    • Side-by-Side ( 6 ) displays your video feed next to your slides or content.
    • Reporter ( 7 ) displays your slides at an angle to the left of your video feed, just as we see for news reporters.

You cannot use the Standout , Side-by-Side , or Reporter mode when sharing a presentation through PowerPoint Live.

  • The Add background button ( 8 ) gives you access to either blur or choose a background image.
  • The Screen ( 9 ) section allows you to share your whole screen so you can easily switch from one window to another while sharing the screen. If you only have one monitor, only one thumbnail view will be available.

    Warning : this type of screen share shows everything on your screen to you r attendees. Make sure you have closed any confidential content before you start your meeting.

  • The Window ( 10 ) section allows you to choose a specific window to share. The number in parentheses shows you how many single windows are available to choose from. Clicking on the window illustration displays previews of all your windows so you can choose the one you need.
  • Microsoft Whiteboard ( 11 ) allows sharing an existing or new virtual board so everyone can sketch and brainstorm on it.
  • Content from camera ( 12 ) allows you to share external content with your camera or from a more complex equipment setting.
  • The PowerPoint Live section ( 13 ) is where a list of your recent PowerPoint files will be displayed if they are stored in your OneDrive for Business or SharePoint sites. Clicking on one of the files starts sharing your content with PowerPoint Live, which we’ll describe soon. You might need to scroll down the list to view the Show more option ( 14 ).
  • The Excel Live ( 15 ) section allows sharing and collaborating in an Excel file during your meeting. There are some restrictions, such as a maximum of 25 attendees and people outside your organization not being granted access. Please see Further reading for the full support article.
  • If you did not have any files in the PowerPoint Live list, or the one you need is not there, you can use Browse OneDrive or Browse my computer ( 16 ) to load your presentation and use it.

If you browse your computer or a network drive to upload a presentation file, it gets added to the meeting files. If you create a personal meeting, the file is made available to meeting participants when they open the Teams meeting details in their calendar, and it is saved in your OneDrive space. If you create a channel meeting, the file is available to all team members in the team files.

After selecti ng a PowerPoint file from the PowerPoint Live list, a loading screen will appear until the application is ready. Your Teams meeting screen will be adjusted to include PowerPoint Live features ( 1 ), which have a similar layout to Presenter View, seen in the previous chapter, while keeping your meeting management tools available ( Figure 14.2 ) :

Figure 14.2 – PowerPoint Live features integrated into your Teams meeting window

I also opened the Participants pane ( 2 ), labeled as People ( 3 ) in the meeting tools, to show you how easy it is to manage the presentation and your attendees.

A new category of tools ( 4 ) becomes available in the meeting toolbar, above the meeting window:

  • Stop sharing ( 5 ) is where you must click when you want to stop sharing your presentation.
  • Layout ( 6 ): This button toggles the focus from Cameo to Content only when a Cameo object is detected on your slide.
  • Private view ( 7 ) is a must-know feature! By default, attendees can navigate your slides while you are presenting, even the ones that have not yet been covered. To disable browsing through your upcoming slides, click the eye icon.
  • Pop out ( 8 ) allows having PowerPoint Live in a separate window. I suggest you avoid using it if you have only one monitor, as it will make your meeting management more complex.

When using the PowerPoint Live sharing feature, there are a few things going on in the background that explain the loading icon. Your file, if it was not already stored in cloud storage, is first uploaded to your OneDrive if it’s a personal meeting, or SharePoint if it is a channel meeting. Then the file is processed and optimized so it can run as a web-based experience inside Teams. This step allows Teams to stream the same cloud-hosted version to each participant while making it possible to use live captions and translations. PowerPoint Live uses less bandwidth than screen sharing.

PowerPoint Live makes it easy to navigate through your slides because most tools are like the ones we have described in Presenter View in the previous chapter. Let’s review what you have available ( Figure 14.3 ):

Figure 14.3 – Using PowerPoint Live tools during your presentation

  • You have arrows, the current slide number, and the total number of slides for slide navigation ( 1 ).
  • The slide thumbnail strip ( 2 ) to quickly access another slide.
  • The list of all your slides in Grid view ( 3 ) to easily show one on demand.
  • The notes pane ( 4 ) displays your notes but does not allow adding or changing them during your presentation, like in Presenter View, at least at the time this chapter was reviewed. But you do have tools to increase or decrease the font size ( 5 ).
  • In More actions (…) ( 6 ):
    • You can remove your notes and the strip of slide thumbnails with Hide presenter view ( 7 ) while keeping the line of tools below the slide preview.
    • You have access to the Magnify slide for all tool ( 8 ), which requires clicking on the + sign. You don’t need it if you are comfortable using the Ctrl + mouse wheel tip to zoom in and out, which was discussed for Presenter View.
    • View slides in high contrast ( 9 ) is an accessibility feature on the attendees’ side. It helps people with visual contrast or color issues.
    • Translate slides ( 10 ) lets attendees choose the language to machine-translate the text on your slides. Be aware that machine translation is not perfect and that it will introduce formatting variations.
  • The annotation tools ( 11 ) are like the ones discussed for Presenter View. Click on one of them to change your mouse cursor into a laser pointer, pen, or highlighter. Clicking on the icon a second time brings up settings such as color or thickness, when applicable. Use the eraser to remove an annotation or click a second time to access the setting to erase all the annotations on the slide.

Using PowerPoint Live during your virtual meetings is a great way to stay in control of your presentation and your participants’ interactions. You could also use it as a practice tool for yourself, recording your sessions and then reviewing them for timing and how you are looking at your camera. Practicing your virtual presentations ahead of time is a great way to keep improving your delivery.

Adjusting your meeting view

When using PowerPoint Live in Teams, you automatically see attendees, and your own camera feed or tile, displayed above the slide preview ( 1 ). You can change this view to fit your needs ( Figure 14 .4 ):

Figure 14.4 – Changing how video cameras are displayed while using PowerPoint Live

We will discuss some interesting settings to consider from the View menu ( 2 ):

  • Focus on content ( 3 ): Activating this feature removes camera feeds and tiles above your slide preview. If no one is using their camera, it might be worth using it so your content can fill the whole space.
  • Prioritize video ( 4 ): Aligns video feeds first in the attendee list. People with their camera off will be listed at the end.
  • More options | Show gallery on the right ( 5 ): When you have at least four participants with their video turned on, this feature is active and allows moving the gallery vertically on the right of your PowerPoint Live view.

You must be in Gallery view in Teams desktop with the application window wide enough to support that side-by-side layout.

There are many View options you can explore. The ones discussed were the most relevant to our topic, covering PowerPoint Live in Teams.

If you are using subtitles when presenting in PowerPoint to make your presentations more accessible, there are features in Teams you must know about to make presenting with PowerPoint Live more accessible, which is the topic of the next section.

Making PowerPoint Live presentations more accessible

When using PowerPoint Live in Teams, it does n’t show subtitles configured in PowerPoint; therefore, you will need to turn them on in Teams ( Figure 14.5 ):

Figure 14.5 – Turning on subtitles in Teams

  • Go to the More ( … ) menu ( 1 ) and open the Language and speech features ( 2 ). Click on Show live captions .
  • At the bottom of your PowerPoint Live view, a Captions panel will display ( 3 ), mentioning translation settings if they were previously used.
  • Click on the Open caption settings icon ( 4 ) and select Language settings to open the Language settings pane ( 5 ). You will be able to select the spoken language, activate captions translation, and select the translation language. The translation option will work with M365 Business Premium, E3, and E5 licenses.
  • To change the caption styles, go back to the Open caption settings icon ( 4 ), then select Caption styles ( 6 ) – a new pane will open. You can change the font and background colors, the font size, the panel height, and its position, at the bottom or at the top.
  • The other option you should consider for more accessible and inclusive presentations is Start transcription ( 7 ), available in Record and transcribe in the More ( … ) ( 1 ) menu. Starting the feature opens a pane on the right of your meeting screen and captures what each participant is saying, either with their name or anonymously, depending on the Teams settings they chose. It can be a productive feature for meetings, capturing your discussions as you go. Transcriptions can also help you repurpose your virtual presentations because you can download the transcript as a Word file or .vtt file to add captions to your videos. The feature is limited to the desktop version of Teams and to a select set of licenses. See the support article in Further reading for details.

Creating and using breakout rooms

There are many scenarios in which you can use breakout rooms during a Teams meeting. And there are lots of ways to create and use those rooms too! Since I want to keep the focus on how using this feature can be helpful to keep your presentation attendees engaged , I will discuss only one scenario in the following example. If you want to learn more about the various ways to create and use breakout rooms, have a look at the support article listed in Further reading .

Let’s sa y you are delivering a workshop using Teams meetings. If you were in a room with your attendees, you would probably ask them to work in small groups to discuss a specific topic that each team would present to the whole group.

To reproduce this team exercise from your meeting window, you need to click on the Rooms icon ( 1 ) to access the Create breakout rooms pane ( 2 ) ( Figur e 14.6 ):

Figure 14.6 – Creating and using breakout rooms in Teams

  • The first section, Choose the number of rooms you want to create ( 3 ), has a drop-down list to choose the number of rooms you want to create; up to 50 breakout rooms can be created. For our example, I will keep 2 as the number of rooms to create.
  • In the Assign participants section ( 4 ), the default option is Automatically assign people to each room ( 5 ), where you will see the number of people already in your meeting. The Let people choose their rooms ( 6 ) option can be very interesting when you are creating various exercises led by facilitators, allowing participants to select the topic of their choice.
  • The only thing left to do is to click on the Create rooms button ( 7 ) to change the right-hand pane to Breakout rooms ( 8 ) with its own set of tools and an Open rooms ( 9 ) button.

When you need breakout rooms on the fly, automatically assigning people is quick and e asy to do. If you need to assign specific people t o certain groups, consult the Microsoft Support article in Further reading to see how you can create your breakout rooms before your event. That is a more efficient way of doing it when structure is key and you have many participants.

After opening your rooms, you can manage them from the Breakout rooms pane. In my example, one room remained closed ( 1 ) because there were no participants to add to it ( Figur e 14.7 ):

Figure 14.7 – Manage breakout rooms and settings

  • Clicking on the small arrow on the left of an opened room opens the list of participants ( 2 ).
  • Hovering over the room displays options ( … ) ( 3 ) allowing you to join, rename, or close the room.
  • The Close rooms button ( 4 ) closes them all at once and brings participants back into your main Teams meeting.
  • When your rooms are closed, you have access to Settings ( 5 ), which allows assigning people to manage the rooms ( 6 ), toggling the Set a time limit ( 7 ) feature to active and selecting the duration in the hours and minutes lists, and deciding whether people can choose their rooms or return to the main meeting on their own ( 8 ). Always click on the Save button after you have made any changes.

Using breakout rooms can help change the pace during your presentation while helping attendees interact more with one another, energizing them before you continue with your presentation. Get familiar with the various breakout room features and settings so you can try a scenario that works for your type of content.

Let’s now move on to two other interactive features you can use in a Teams meeting to help increase interactions with your attendees while getting their feedback to help you adapt your talk.

Using Q&A and Polls to create more interactions

Virtual presentations require more interaction to keep your audience engaged. The reason is simple: you are competing with emails, news feeds, text messages, and social media, so you absolutely need to find ways to grab everyone’s attention.

When using Microsoft Teams, you have nice tools that can help you, and we will discuss two of them in the following sections.

Using Q&A

Q&A is a nice a ddition to Teams meetings, allowing you to separate questions and important discussions from the regular chat pane. Unfortunately, it is not active by default and first needs to be activated in the meeting options. It can be done when you are creating your meeting invite, but also during your meeting ( Fig ure 14.8 ):

Figure 14.8 – Enabling Q&A during a meeting

  • Click on More ( … ) ( 1 ), go to Settings ( 2 ), and click on Meeting options .
  • In the new Meeting options window ( 3 ), go to the Participation section ( 4 ) and toggle the Q&A switch to active ( 5 ).
  • Click the Apply button ( 6 ) so your Teams meeting gets updated with the Q&A application ( 7 ).

The pre vious steps are only available if you are the Teams meeting organizer or if you were made a co-organizer for the meeting. If the meeting was scheduled by someone else for you and they did not make you a co-organizer, you need to ask them to make the changes. They can go back to their meeting in their calendar and make changes to the meeting options, or they can start the meeting and change the options by following the previous steps. If you are not familiar with the different roles in Teams meetings, have a look at the Microsoft Support article in Further reading .

When you do have Q&A available in your meeting window toolbar, click on its icon ( 1 ) so the Q&A pane ( 2 ) shows on the right ( Fig ure 14.9 ):

Figure 14.9 – Opening Q&A and managing its settings

  • The firs t thing you should do if you have never used this feature is to click on the gear icon ( 3 ) to access the Q&A settings pane.
  • You can then decide what permissions you give your attendees, such as asking questions, replying, upvoting, or reacting to questions ( 4 ). Since our goal is to improve interactions with attendees, allow them to ask questions and reply instead of using Q&A solely for your own questions.
  • The next setting is for allowing attendees to use anonymous posts or replies ( 5 ) – there is an important note mentioning that replies and reactions won’t be anonymous.
  • Finally, you can activate the Moderate questions toggle ( 6 ), making them available only after reviewing them. If you are running your meeting alone, I would advise not using it to avoid overwhelming you with extra tasks.
  • Any change you make will activate the Save button ( 7 ).

You or your attendees can quickly enter a question by clicking in the Start a discussion field ( 1 ) to access the Post as dialog box ( 2 ) ( Figu re 14.10 ):

Figure 14.10 – Adding and managing questions in Q&A

  • Two types of posts are available ( 3 ): Start a discussion and Ask a question . There are not many differences between the two besides the fact that a question is shaded with a colored rectangle, and that it has three types of interactions instead of two ( 4 ): Upvote , Reaction , and Comment .
  • If you want to manage the Q&A list of messages, you can click on the ellipses ( … ) ( 5 ) to access Edit , Archive , Delete , Close conversation , or Pin conversation features.
  • When you have a new activity in Q&A during your meeting, you will see a small circle over the Q&A icon ( 6 ) to notify you.

The best way to get comfortable with this feature is to plan a practice meeting with a few people and decide what would be useful for you. Again, Q&A can be planned before your event when you create your meeting invite.

The advantage of using Q&A for any formal question is to avoid having to search through the chat pane when it is used for everything. To make this work, you do need to tell your attendees about your expectations at the beginning of your meeting. If you prefer Q&A for questions and specific topics, and chat for more informal conversations, show them how to find those panes and use them.

Even though Q&A could be a nice place to add your own questions for attendees, you might want to consider Polls instead, the topic of the n ext section.

Using Polls in a meeting

Polls is a n application that can quickly be added to your Teams meetings so you can ask various questions and get attendees to participate ( Fi gure 14.11 ):

Next, you should have a look at the poll settings ( 1 ) available from the link at the bottom of the Polls creation window ( Fi gure 14.13 ):

  • By default, the poll’s aggregated results are shared with your attendees ( 2 ), but you could decide to record their names as an organizer or for presenters or allow your co-presenters to edit polls that have been saved as drafts.
  • When you click on the Save as draft button ( 3 ), your poll is li sted as DRAFT in the Polls pane ( 4 ).
  • You can click the Launch button ( 5 ) when you are ready to make it live, or the arrow beside it ( 6 ) if you want to edit or delete your poll.

When you launch a poll directly from its creation window, it overlays the meeting window ( 1 ) for everyone in the meeting and becomes available in the Polls pane labeled as LIVE ( 2 ) ( Fi gure 14.14 ):

Figure 14.14 – Launching a poll and retrieving it in the meeting chat

  • People can answer and submit their reply from the overlaid window and see the overall results ( 3 ). Clicking the X or Done button closes the window.
  • Polls also show in the Meeting chat pane feed ( 4 ), where anyone can click the View poll button ( 5 ) to answer if they have not done so before, or view overall results for the question.

Quick polls can be helpful, but it would be much better for you to plan those polls or interactions before your presentation date. In the first chapter of this book, we discussed how important planning is for your presentation content. For your delivery, it is also important to plan the types of interactions you will want to use.

To plan your polls and save them as drafts before your event, go to your Teams calendar and click on your planned meeting ( 1 ) and then on the Chat button ( 2 ) ( F igure 14.15 ):

Figure 14.15 – Planning your polls from the Polls tab in your meeting details

  • You now have access to all your meeting tabs and applications. If you do not see the Polls tab ( 3 ), use the + sign ( 4 ) to add the application, as previously explained in the Teams meeting window.
  • Use the + New poll button ( 5 ) to create all your polls in advance. A Suggestions pane has a list of quick polls you can use ( 6 ).
  • Just leave them in draft mode ( 7 ) until you launch during your meeting.

If you want more information on Polls, have a look at the Microsoft Support article in Further reading .

Creating and using an avatar

Even though avatars are not an interactive feature per se, they do have a way of spicing up meetings and reducing virtual fatigue. I also think it could be a way to encourage participation for camera-shy people.

If you need more details, please refer to the Microsoft Learn article listed in Further reading .

The easiest way to start creating your first avatar, and validate that you have access to the app at the same time, is by opening the application in your Teams interface ( Figure 14.16 ):

Figure 14.16 – Opening the Avatars application in Teams

  • Click on the View more apps ellipsis ( … ) ( 1 ) and search for the application with the Avatars keyword.
  • It will open the Avatars app ( 2 ), where you can start creating your avatar or see the ones you have already created ( 3 ) – up to three versions can be created.
  • Clicking on the + sign ( 4 ) lets you create your avatar from a photo, from a list ( Create from base ), or duplicate one you already created.
  • Existing avatars can be customized or deleted easily ( 5 ).

During the creation or customization process, you can adjust a list of features to make your avatar more aligned with your own look ( Figure 14.17 ):

Figure 14.17 – Creating or customizing your avatar

  • Skin Tone ( 1 )
  • Face ( 2 )
  • Hair ( 3 )
  • Makeup ( 4 )
  • Body ( 5 )
  • Wardrobe ( 6 )

As you can see in the Face category ( 2 ), all parts of the face can be adjusted separately ( 7 ), sometimes adding another selection pane for colors, such as for the eyes ( 8 ).

The best way to get familiar with the application is by trying it. Depending on the level of likeness you want to achieve, it might take you a while to create your first avatar. But once you have one created, duplicate it so you have a starting point.

Selecting your avatar for a meeting can be done in the Join meeting window ( Figure 14.18 ):

Figure 14.18 – Using your avatar from the Join meeting window

  • Turn your camera off ( 1 ) and click on Effects and avatars ( 2 ).
  • Select the avatar you want to use and add a background if you want ( 3 ).

You can select your avatar after you have joined a meeting by using the arrow beside the camera icon and selecting the Avatars effects category.

What I really like about avatars is that they move and express themselves. We see eye and lip movements when we talk, as well as hand movements aligned to the Raise hand or React features in the meeting.

Don’t get me wrong. I am not saying all meetings should use avatars! But do consider them on certain occasions, such as when people are not feeling well but attending a meeting anyway, or for specific presentation types such as virtual games or relaxed meetings. Establish what is allowed for your team and when. Making it clear when avatars can be used can add variety and create a different type of audience engagement.

Improving the camera, lighting, and microphone setup

My goal with this section is not to cover all the greatest and latest equipment available on the market, but to share some features and tips that can help you even when you don’t have acces s to fancy gear.

Checking Microsoft Teams device settings

When Microsoft introduced the improved and lighter version of Teams, they finally made it easier to access camera and microphone settings. They can be accessed in the Join meeting window, but we will see how to access them during a Teams meeting.

Let’s start with camera settings ( Figure 14.19 ):

Figure 14.19 – Accessing camera settings in a Teams meeting

  • Click on the arrow beside the Camera icon ( 1 ) to open the Effects and settings window ( 2 ) and click on More video effects and settings ( 3 ).
  • The Video effects and settings pane will display on the right ( 4 ).
  • Click on the Settings tab ( 5 ):
  • The Brightness tool ( 6 ) will enhance video quality in low light conditions.
  • The Soft focus tool ( 7 ) will smooth your appearance by adjusting it with the slider when it is turned on. Just be careful not to overdo it, because it can make you look unnatural.

Even though these settings will not fix everything, they can help improve the quality of your video.

Audio settings can also be accessed easily by clicking on the arrow beside the microphone icon ( 1 ) ( Figure 14.20 ):

Figure 14.20 – Accessing audio settings in a Teams meeting

  • In the settings window ( 2 ), you have access to select your speaker or microphone if more than one device is detected on your computer ( 3 ).
  • Spatial audio ( 4 ) is a feature that will change your audio experience. When in a meeting, you will hear the audio from where the person speaking is positioned on your screen. Wireless headphones are not supported yet.
  • Noise suppression ( 5 ) is on by default, greatly reducing ambient noise. It is an AI-based feature, analyzing background noise. Teams monitors your background noise and turns suppression up or down to remove noise not recognized as your voice. Two secondary settings are available:
    • Background noise only , which enhances your audio quality by removing background noise only.
    • Voice isolation , which isolates your voice from the background noise and other voices. If you decide to use this feature, follow the instructions in Teams to add a voice sample in the Recognition section of the application settings.
  • Clicking More audio settings ( 6 ) opens the Audio settings pane, where the only new setting option is High fidelity music mode ( 7 ), useful if you play music during some meetings.

If you are wondering how efficient the noise suppression feature is, I can share that I once had to deliver virtual training from a room in my basement while workers were drilling our house foundation in front of me – participants confirmed they could not hear anything. Lucky for them, as I had problems concentrating on what I had to say! But I would add that the reliability of the feature might depend on which type of microphone you are using. Test it with your own equipment.

Now that we have seen what Teams settings can help you improve how you look and sound during a meeting, let’s see some best practice tips in the next section.

Tips to help you improve how you look and sound

Even though I will not discuss any specific equipment, it is clear that if you want to look more professional for your virtual presentations, you need to avoid using the built-in camera and microphone of your laptop. You simply cannot control them well.

Try looking for a separate webcam, and ideally for quality headphones with a microphone. When you add that equipment, small applications are usually supplied by their manufacturer that can help you adjust the settings.

To help you look your best, make sure your webcam is in front of you at eye level. This also helps you look toward your attendees, which is much more engagi ng ( Figure 14.21 ):

Figure 14.21 – Position your webcam in front of you at eye level

For lighting, you should ideally try to present in a room where you have natural light in front of you or where you can use LED or ring lights if you don’t have a window. Never present with lighting coming from behind you because you will look like a silhouette with no fac e ( Figure 14.22 ):

Figure 14.22 – Position yourself so the light is in front of you

During your virtual presentation, if the room you are using is rather dark, try adding light sources at an angle on each side of your face to avoid shadow s ( Figure 14.23 ):

Figure 14.23 – Adding light sources on each side when you are in a dark environment

Of course, improving how you look when delivering your virtual presentations means cleaning up what you have in your background. There is also the possibility of using features such as blurring your background or adding a virtual background. Be aware that if people walk behind you during your presentation, it will look as if you have human body parts floating behind you if they walk close enough.

If you have curly hair, like me, virtual backgrounds will sometimes create deceptive results. I often end up having part of my face disappear when I move. I decided to purchase meeting backdrops that I clip to a pole behind me or just use a plain curtain if I’m delivering content for another organization as a contractor. Now my face stays intact for the duration of my classes or meetings!

The tips I shared in this section are what I would call quick fixes. If you are going to present virtually on a regular basis, you should invest in good equipment and lighting. Your best bet in terms of knowing what to choose is to ask other presenters what they use and what the y would recommend.

Your goal should not be to change everything for your next presentation. Just decide on a few elements that can be improved and work on them. After that, you can focus on other improvements for future presentations. One step at a time is better than no improvement at all.

Wishing you much success in your upcom ing presentations!
