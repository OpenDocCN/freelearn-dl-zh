Starting Your Presentation with M365 Copilot

During the fall of 2023, Microsoft introduced Copilot for M365 as a collaboration and productivity tool integrated within its Microsoft 365 business environment. So, even if you don’t personally use Copilot for M365, you have probably heard about Microsoft’s Artificial Intelligence ( AI ) assistant. It’s also important to mention that Microsoft has re-branded the feature as Microsoft 365 Copilot since its introduction and now simply refers to it as Copilot when used within an application – we will use this new name from now on.

Within PowerPoint, Copilot still needs to be considered as an assistant because it is still not able to produce content aligned with the best presentation practices that you have learned so far. I often refer to it as a smart start .

Writing about an AI assistant that will continue improving through time seemed like a daunting task at first. But the topics chosen for this chapter will help you learn the basics of Copilot in PowerPoint no matter how it evolves.

Defining Copilot and its basic functions

Copilot is an AI technology based on GPT-4/GPT-5 , which stands for Generative Pre-trained Transformer . In plain language, it means it is a model that was pre-trained using huge sets of text data from the internet. Then it learned from more specific data, such as conversations between users and the assistant, and it uses what it has learned to generate answers for new conversations.

It’s important to note that while Copilot’s AI can generate informative and helpful responses, it doesn’t understand the meaning of words and phrases in the same way that a human would. Answers are based on patterns learned during training, not on true understanding or awareness. In addition, Copilot’s AI does not have access to personal or organizational information unless that information is explicitly provided during the conversation. For business use, Copilot will primarily use your business data and files to help you with your daily tasks, although it can also search the web to help you while still protecting your organization’s data. Depending on your organization’s IT admins, it is possible that the web search might be limited or unavailable.

Overall, your Copilot assistant can accomplish a nice list of tasks:

  • Multilingual assistance : It can help you understand or communicate in another language.
  • Content generation : It can help you generate presentation drafts, improve your content, and quickly produce summaries of existing content.
  • Search information : It can help you find multiple types of information in your organization, such as files and conversations, or search the web to add statistics or elaborate on a topic. It can also help you think about potential questions an audience might ask.
  • Create graphic elements : It can help you create new images or graphic elements from your organization’s data.

We will see PowerPoint-specific examples in upcoming sections, but before we get to that, let’s learn how to engage in conversation with Copilot with the next section on prompting.

Understanding the basics of good prompts

Being productive with Copilot means becoming familiar with the art of asking your assistant what you want with precise requests, known as prompts . Prompts are created using natural language, just as you would when having a conversation with someone. These prompts must be clear, precise, and relevant to your goal.

As mentioned in Microsoft’s documentation titled Learn about Copilot prompts (see Further reading for the link), there are four types of information in a prompt that will improve Copilot’s results:

  • Goal : Tell Copilot what your desired outcome is, such as a document, a list, a summary, or a presentation
  • Context : Explain why you need the information (e.g., a meeting, a report, etc.) and who it is for
  • Tone : Explain the tone to use in its reply (e.g., for stakeholders, for trainees, for colleagues, etc.)
  • Source : Ask precisely from which source you want the information, whether it is the web, documents in your organization, discussions in Teams, and so on

It will take you time to adapt to this new way of asking for something, as Copilot will not give you good results if you keep using the keyword approach used in browser search engines. And after you have sent your first prompt, keep the conversation going! If Copilot’s reply was incomplete, say so. If the reply was inaccurate, say so. The more you craft your prompts as if you are asking a colleague to help you, the better your results will be.

For help with prompting and getting the most out of Copilot for Microsoft, please see the Further reading section.

Try it yourself

The best way to learn about Copilot is to try it. Start a new blank presentation and save it to OneDrive or SharePoint so you can easily share it for collaboration or go back to the previous version. Then, use this prompt:

Create a presentation about the benefits of physical activity on employee mental health. This presentation will be made to my co-workers at a short friendly lunchtime meeting. Ideally, we should have a maximum of 30 minutes of content to encourage conversation. We must use relevant images and a friendly, even humorous tone, so that they understand the benefits while being encouraged to try new experiences to move. Don't hesitate to offer me new ideas or ideas that are out of the usual framework.

After Copilot is done generating your presentation outline, the Narrative Builder window will display a series of tools allowing you to change numerous elements before you generate the slides ( Figure 5.4 ):

Figure 5.4 – Using the Narrative Builder tools to work on an outline

  1. Edit prompt : After reading through the first proposed outline, you can simply edit your prompt to generate a new outline.
  2. Like/dislike : These icons allow you to send Microsoft feedback about content generated by Copilot. This feature might not be available if your organization’s IT department has disabled sending this type of feedback or if Microsoft removes it.
  3. Reorder topic : A topic created by Copilot becomes a section in your presentation. The slide icons show us the number of slides in a section with their titles. Creating a new presentation in the Narrative Builder from a file

Even though it will still need some adjustments, my favorite way to start a new presentation with the help of Copilot is by using an existing Word file. I find that creating a presentation’s structure and content draft with Copilot in Word gives me features such as referencing more than one existing file as a starting point and rewriting paragraphs. Even if Copilot in PowerPoint keeps improving and might eventually have features like the ones in Word, I still think it is a good idea to create your narrative and story without opening PowerPoint.

Just as we saw in Figure 5.3 , when creating a new presentation with the Narrative Builder, we start by clicking the Narrative Builder icon to open its prompt window. Adding a reference file as a starting point can be done in two ways ( Figure 5.9 ):

Figure 5.9 – Referencing a file in the Narrative Builder

  • Type in a forward slash ( / ), then select the name of the file in the list of recent files or start typing the file name to help Copilot search for it in your work environment storage (OneDrive or SharePoint) ( 1 ).
  • Click on the Reference a file icon ( ) to get a list of recent files or start typing the file name ( 2 ).

In both cases, you also get access to the Attach cloud files option ( 3 ), which opens your full list of recent files. It also allows you to browse your OneDrive files ( 4 ) and the SharePoint sites you have access to ( 5 ).

At the time this edition was written, only Word and PDF file formats were supported, and only one file could be referenced in a prompt. Be aware that the maximum file size is 10 MB as of May 2025.

The slide-generation process is the same, but I find that slide decks created in this way tend to have a bit less text, especially when the referenced Word file was created with heading styles. But, then again, I might just have been lucky that Copilot generated slides with less text when I used this feature. You will need to test it for yourself a few times and see how it behaves and always read everything Copilot suggests to make sure it is true and aligned with the message you want to convey.

You could also simply ask Copilot to create your presentation from a reference file without using a long and detailed prompt. The results you will get will always depend on the referenced file structure and how Copilot behaves that day. While testing various prompts for this chapter, I obtained various results, generating an outline with as few as 3 slides to as many as 23 slides.

If you try creating a presentation from a file that contains a lot of content and many headers, making it impossible to fit in the 40-slide Copilot limit, it gives you the View more topics option ( 1 ) ( Figure 5.10 ):

Figure 5.10 – Viewing and choosing topics for Copilot slide generation

If the estimated number of slides is close to the maximum but you don’t see View more topics , scroll down to the bottom of the suggested list of topics. When clicking on the link, a new window will open with the Customize your presentation by adding or removing topics option and allowing you to include the following elements:

  • A column for Included topics ( 2 ): Hovering over a topic will reveal an arrow ( 3 ) allowing you to view a bullet list of the content when clicking on it, and a minus icon ( 4 ) to send the topic into the Available topics column.
  • A column for Available topics ( 5 ): Again, when hovering over a topic, you can click on the arrow to view a bullet list of the content ( 6 ) and a plus sign icon ( 7 ) to add the topic to the Included topics column. When it is grayed out, it means you must remove a topic before including a new one.
  • Looking at the bottom of the window will let you know if you have reached the maximum number of slides that can be generated ( 8 ).
  • When you are done adding and removing topics, click on Save topics ( 9 ) and then Generate slides ( 10 ) to get your draft presentation file.

Whatever the method you use, you will need to try it on your own and see what works best with the type of content you are trying to create. If you don’t get the results you want the first time, try it again because Copilot seldom creates the exact same content every time you try a prompt.

In Figure 5.24 , the text on the first line of the content placeholder ( Challenges Affecting Productivity ) would make a much better title than the generic one Copilot first created ( Cognitive Performance Issues ). If you get similar results, just cut the text out of the placeholder and paste it into the title placeholder.

If your slide master and layouts have been configured appropriately, this step will work well. I am not saying that all these changes will be made quickly, especially if you have many slides in your presentation, but taking the time to do it is the only way to produce more impactful presentations.

Let’s now discuss the second element I find irritating: having most/all title placeholders, and any text used as a subtitle in other placeholders, in title case instead of sentence case. If you want to improve readability, that must be changed, and we know Copilot cannot accomplish this task at the time this chapter was written. But Copilot is a great assistant to help us with code! You can use Visual Basic for Applications ( VBA ) code within PowerPoint to automate certain repetitive tasks.

So, even though this book is not intended to teach you how to use VBA (I consider myself a code dummy!), since we are discussing Copilot and how it can help you, here are the prompts and steps I used to remove title case throughout the presentation. If you want to learn more about macros, please refer to the Microsoft Learn article listed in Further reading .

In the Copilot chat pane, I first tried the following prompt:

I want all the title placeholders to use "sentence case" instead of "title case", help me write a macro that will accomplish this task.

With the latest Copilot improvements, the chat pane returned a warning message that VBA is not fully supported and that syntax highlighting is based on Python. Re-testing the prompts and macro did work.

Copilot was able to help and returned the lines of code and steps to accomplish the task. If you try it and Copilot replies that it cannot do that, try it again later ( Figure 5.25 ):

Figure 5.25 – Copilot’s answer to a macro creation prompt

One of the suggested follow-up questions made me think I could automate this task even more, so I decided to follow up with this question:

Can this type of macro be used for all the placeholder types in the file that has text?

Copilot understood and gave me a completely revised macro ( 1 ) and the steps to run it ( 2 ) ( Figure 5.26 ):

Figure 5.26 – Copilot returning a revised macro and the steps to run it

To use the macro, you first need to select the lines of code in the Copilot chat pane and copy them ( Ctrl + C ). Note : A new Copy code icon is available before the lines of code. Then you can proceed with opening the VBA editor. You will probably run into the same problem I had with the Alt + F11 keyboard shortcut: it only displayed letter shortcuts to be used with the Alt key to open functions.

The macro can be created and run from the Developer tab ( 1 ) ( Figure 5.27 ):

Figure 5.27 – Using the Developer tab to create and run the macro

If you don’t see the tab, go to File | Options | Customize Ribbon and add a check mark beside Developer in the column on the right of the window. You can then click on the Visual Basic button ( 2 ). When the Microsoft Visual Basic for Applications window opens, click on the Insert tab ( 3 ), then Module ( 4 ). Paste the code in the module window that opens ( 5 ). The last step is pressing F5 on your keyboard or using the Run Sub/UserForm button in the toolbar ( 6 ). Then, close the VBA window.

If you get any error messages when running the macro, note what the error is and go back to the Copilot chat pane. Tell Copilot the macro has an error and ask it to review the code. Depending on the file I was testing, I got errors when running the suggested macro, but it worked after copying and pasting the corrected macro into the editor.

The macro does a good job, even if in some instances you might encounter an uppercase letter missing at the beginning of a sentence. If that happens, just go back and ask a macro to change all the text placeholders to sentence case. After running the macro you will be able to view the changes on your slides ( 1 ) ( Figure 5.28 ):

Figure 5.28 – Running the macro results and removing the macro from the file

But, as you can see in Figure 5.28 , adding a macro to your file turns off autosave (files saved to OneDrive or SharePoint) ( 2 ), asking you to save it as a macro-enabled file. But depending on the security settings for macro-enabled presentations in organizations, the file might not even open in the future. You can keep a copy of the macro-enabled file if you want, but I strongly suggest removing the macro from the file you will be using to continue your presentation creation process. Go through the following steps:

  1. On the Developer tab, click on Macros ( 3 ).
  2. In the Macro window, select the macro ( 4 ), click on the Delete button ( 5 ), and confirm you want to delete it.
  3. Go to File | Save as or use the Save as button in ( 2 ). Use the filename you want and make sure you are using the .pptx file format. PowerPoint will warn you that VBA cannot be saved in macro-free presentations. Click on the Yes button to continue.
  4. Close your file and re-open it so you see that AutoSave is back on.

With the previous example, my goal was to help you understand that any repetitive cleaning task has the potential to be accomplished with VBA if you leverage Copilot’s knowledge to create the lines of code for you. So, until Copilot can do it on its own, you have a tool to avoid a lot of manual clean-up work.

Summary

In this chapter, we learned about tasks Copilot can accomplish and discussed the basics of good prompts. We also learned how to create a new presentation with the help of Copilot, how to summarize a presentation, and which tasks it can understand to modify the contents of a file.

As much as I love my Copilot assistant in many other applications in my M365 environment, I do think it is still a work-in-progress within PowerPoint. I encourage you to try it and test its limits, always keeping in mind that a Copilot-created presentation is far from ready for delivery. Make sure you always review the visuals with the best practices that should be applied to impactful presentations.

In the next chapter, you will be learning about using other AI tools available in PowerPoint that are included with your regular M365 license to help you improve your visuals. By creating image layouts, visual lists, and timelines, or creating presentations without a tem plate, you will see how the designer can become your next best friend.
