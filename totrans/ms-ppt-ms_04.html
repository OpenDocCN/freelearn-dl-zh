Leveraging PowerPoint’s Slide Master for Design

PowerPoint’s s lide master is a design feature that is still overlooked by so many presentation creators and presenters. This is the main reason why I have seen so many presentation files where content has been created by hand on each slide . No wonder people have been complaining all this time that slide creation takes so much time!

Therefore, the use of slide master is one of the first topics I go through in my training sessions. If you are still creating your slides by deleting the placeholders you see on the blank slides and get busy adding text boxes and other types of content manually, this chapter will help you gain hours of your life back. I usually even make this bold statement to users: if you use the Slide Master feature, you’ll automate up to 90% of your presentation design tasks. You’ll stop re-creating basic designs on each slide to focus on adding your content.

The goal here is not to show you how to build a robust corporate template. If that is what you need, you will find a resource in the Further reading section from my friends Echo Swinford and Julie Terberg – they are the template gurus in our industry, and you should read their book if you need to create functional templates for your organization. Instead, I will focus on the main functions in the Slide Master feature that will help you to create your presentations quickly when you don’t have a corporate template or if you need to adapt an existing one.

In this chapter, we will focus on the following topics:

  • Understanding layouts and placeholders
  • Choosing fonts and colors
  • Configuring standard layouts
  • Adding custom layouts
  • Making your slide master compatible for AI services

Technical requirements

PowerPoint’s Slide Master feature can be found in all desktop versions of the application. Except for the last section in the chapter, the topics discussed can be applied to whichever version of PowerPoint you are using, although you might encounter some differences if you are not using PowerPoint in its Microsoft 365 subscription model. The topic Making your slide master compatible for AI services applies to users subscribing to Copilot for M365.

Understanding layouts and placeholders

Through the years, I have seen many people start a new presentation and tell me they did not like the text boxes on the first slide, so they just deleted them to start from scratch. Unfortunately for them, they did not know that doing so would make them work much harder than necessary to create their slides. This also makes it even harder if they decide to change the overall look of their presentation. This huge mistake usually happens because users don’t know about two fundamental features in PowerPoint: layouts and placeholders . So, let’s show you what they are and how they will help you create your content faster.

Placeholders

Layouts wou ld not be useful without placeholders ; these are the default boxes that you see on slides when you have applied a layout. At first glance, they might look like regular text boxes, but they are not. Placeholders are coded and managed differently than other slide elements by PowerPoint. The first way to differentiate them is the prompt text we see before adding any content ( Figure 3.3 ):

Choosing fonts an d colors

Since the introduction of Office 2007 , users have started to see the presence of themes in their applications. Just to keep the description short, we can say that themes help to define the overall look of a document in terms of colors, fonts, effects applied to shapes, and so on. As you might have noticed, I put emphasis on the word document . That’s because a theme can be applied throughout your Office applications, making your Word , Excel , and PowerPoint documents look more consistent. Microsoft supplies some free themes that can be found in the Themes list in the Slide Master view. If you want to learn more about themes, you should check out Echo Swinford and Julie Terberg’s book Building PowerPoint Templates v2 , listed in Further reading .

Using one of Microsoft’s themes to create your presentations can help speed up the design process, if one suits your needs. But I would still advise you to make some changes so that your presentation will be more aligned with your organization’s look. One thing you might want to do when using one of Microsoft’s themes is change the colors and the pair of fonts. This process is the same if you decide to start with a template of your own or a new b lank presentation.

Since my goal is to help business users create their presentations more quickly, I will give you the basic elements you need to be aware of to make colors work in your presentations. For detailed information, I will again refer you to Building PowerPoint Templates v2 , listed in Further reading .

For the first four colors labeled as Text/Background - Dark 1 and Dark 2 , and Light 1 and Light 2 , you need to keep in mind that they are used to automatically change the text color to a background style allowing optimal contrast. When a template or theme is built properly, you will usually have Text/Background - Dark 1 as black, and Text/Background - Light 1 as white, giving the best possible contrast between text and background. For the Dark 2 / Light 2 colors, you need to make sure their contrast level looks good in the Sample preview, as can be seen in the previous figure ( Figure 3.11 ).

For the accent and hyperlink colors, make the necessary changes and h ave a look at the sample preview to make sure your colors have enough contrast with both the dark and light backgrounds. You should also look at the side-by-side effect of your contrast colors, especially if you will be adding charts and using a colorful style in your presentation. If you want to know how your color palette may look for your audience, create a few content slides, open the slide show view, and move back from your computer screen to look at them.

You can do the same for your virtual presentations by starting a virtual meeting with your preferred tool and having a look at how a few content slides look when sharing your screen. Configuring standard layouts

We already mentioned that in the Slide Master view, the slid e master is the larger thumbnail and the smaller ones that follow are the layouts. Another element you need to be aware of is the highlighted thumbnail ( 1 ), which shows you the layout of the slide you were on when you opened the Slide Master feature ( Figure 3.14 ). You can hover over any thumbnail to show a tooltip ( 2 ) that tells you on what sl ide(s) the layout is being used.

Figure 3.14 – The Slide Master view showing the layout applied to your current slide

Before making changes to any of the layouts, you should make formatting changes and position adjustments to the title and content placeholders that you want to see applied in all of the layouts in Slide Master ( 1 ) ( Figure 3.15 ). Doing so repli cates the changes to the layouts.

Figure 3.15 – Formatting changes made in Slide Master replicate in layouts

I have created an example where I changed the font color and size and modified the vertical alignment of the text to the top in the title placeholder of my slide master ( 2 ). I also changed the font size of three levels of bullets and modified the bullet for the first level ( 3 ). In the title and content layout, my changes were reproduce d automatically ( 4 ).

Some layouts will not get the exact same changes because of their use. For example, the title slide’s layout will get the same font style and color but won’t get the same font size as the regular titles since it is common practice to use bigger fonts on the title slide. Adjustments can be made to other placeholders after you are done with the slide master. Just keep in mind that doing so breaks the placeholder’s association with your slide master, meaning the next time you make a change in the slide master it won’t replicate in pl aceholders modified on th e layouts.

Try it yourself

The best way to understand the previous explanation is to try it yourself. Follow these steps:

  1. Open a new blank presentation and go to the Slide Master view.
  2. Change the font size and color of the title placeholder on your slide master.
  3. Select a layout and change the color of the font in the title placeholder.
  4. Go back to Slide Master and change the title font color again.
  5. Now, have a look at your modified layout. The new color change did not replicate.

You need to keep in mind that you should review all your layouts every time you decide to change something in the slide master to make sure the chang es look like what you have in mind.

Changes you should avoid

Whether in the slide master or layouts, we already mentioned that we can adjust font sizes, font colors, and text alignment within t he placeholders. Make sure you always keep in mind the best practices we discussed in the previous chapter. You can also resize and move placeholders to create the look that suits your organization’s needs, and you can add any shape or graphic element that you want to see on all slides, or just on specific layouts.

But there is a list of things that could create more problems than you bargained for depending on your situation:

  • Deleting placeholders on the slide master : Doing so breaks the link to the layouts and prevents the possibility of making font size and color changes in one place. Just don’t do it!
  • Deleting placeholders on standard layouts : Doing so can cause a lot of problems when bringing together some slides with the same layout from another presentation, or when applying the template to another presentation. PowerPoint won’t be able to map the placeholders thus making you work harder to put everything in its place.
  • Deleting standard layouts you are not using : This might cause similar problems to the ones mentioned previously, especially when handling content from files that don’t use the same template.
  • Renaming standard layouts : Doing so will create problems when PowerPoint is trying to map content coming from a layout with a standard name with a template that does not have such a layout anymore.

When you decide to do a bit of cleaning in your layouts, you will also realize that some elements canno t be deleted, such as layouts already used by some slides, or placeholders on a layout that are used by a slide in the file; that is by design.

So, if you are not satisfied with some of the standard layouts provided in PowerPoint, what should you do next? Simply add some custom layouts, which is the topic of the next section, including tips to help you create them.

Using the lock feature for placeholders or objects

Any graphic element or regular text box added to a layout in the Slide Master view won’t be editable when you go back to the Normal view. This is to protect any content that needs to be displayed on slides without being editable. But for many years, users have asked for a feature that allo ws them to lock placeholders in their templates to avoid having them moved by users. A locking feature was introduced in May 2021 to help users lock slide objects. It does help protect any elements added to your layouts from being moved around in the Slide Master view, but that does not mean they are also locked in the Normal view. Let’s have a look at how to use the feature so you get the desired result.

While in the slide master view, you can access the locking feature by right clicking any object on the slide master or layouts and then clicking Lock or Unlock in the contextual menu. However, I think it is more interesting to do it from the Selection pane , a feature unknown to many users that offers a few other tricks you should know. To display the Selection pane while in the slide master view, you can select the Home tab ( 1 ) | Drawing group, A rrange ( 2 ) | Selection Pane... ( 4 ) options ( Figure 3.31 ):

Figure 3.31 – Steps to display Selection pane

Once it is displayed, you will have acces s to the list of objects on your slide labeled with their generic name created by PowerPoint. In the example in Figure 3. 32 , we can see that the title and content layout has five placeholders ( 1 ):

Figure 3.32 – Locking feature available in Selection pane

To lock an object, you simply need to click the lock icon to close it ( 2 ). As you can see in the figure, the title placeholder ( 3 ) has been locked. If we try to select it on the slide, its border will be full without any handles allowing resizing or movement.

This is where you should use the locking feature if you want to make sure placeholders, or any other objects added to the slide master and layouts are not moved accidentally in your template. That being said, it will not lock slide objects when you go back to the Normal view.

If you leave the Selection pane open and go into the Normal view, it will display the same locking possibility but, as we can see in Figure 3.33 ( 1 ), the title placeholder we locked in the Slide Master view is now unlocked, and the placeholder on the slide can be moved or resized ( 2 ) with the selection and rotation handles. You need to use the Lock feature once again in the Normal view while on slides to prevent any elements from being moved there.

Figure 3.33 – Unlocked placeholder in the Normal view

So, if you want to protect placeholders from being moved or resized when editing the template, lock them in the Slide Master view. If you want to protect objects from being accidentally moved on slides while addi ng content, then lock them from the Normal view.

Sin ce we are talking about the Selection pane, let’s explore other features there that will become useful later when we discuss animations in Chapter 9 . If you look back at F igure 3.33 , you see a small eye icon ( 3 ) that allows you to hide or display objects on your slide. When we want to cre ate more complex animation effects, it is common to overlay more than one object, which makes it very difficult to select only one – this is when this hiding feature will help. If you double-click on the name of an object ( 4 ), you will be able to rename it. Renaming objects should become a standard procedure if you want to keep your sanity with complex animations – it is the quickest way to find objects that need special animations.

A word on the Quick Access Toolbar

The Quick Access Toolbar ( QAT ) feature can be found in all Microsoft Office applications. It is a great way to customize a list of tools you use often. In PowerPoint, the Selection pane is a great tool to add to your QAT. To do that, simply right-click the feature and click on Add to Quick Access Toolbar . If you are not familiar with the QAT, type quick access toolbar in the Search box in the title bar of any Office application to get help.

Making your slide master compatible for AI services

If you plan on using your PowerPoint template with Microsoft’s Copilot for M365 AI assistant, everything that has been discussed in this chapter still holds true. But you must take extra steps so Copilot can use branded layouts to create new presentations and slides. This section applies if you have purchased an add-on Copilot for M365 license to your regular M365 subscription.

In Chapter 5 , we will discuss how to leverage this AI assistant’s features. For now, we’ll focus on how the slide master needs to be updated to optimize your results.

Microsoft’s recommendations

Even though Copilot for M365 in PowerPoint was officially launched by Microsoft in March 2023, supporting branded corporate templates only became available in November 2024. That is also when they made recommendations on how corporate templates should be updated so users would get AI results more aligned with their brand.

Since those recommendations are still quite new at the time I am writing this book, I’ll focus on giving you the high-level information and referring to Microsoft’s documentation in the Further reading section, so you have access to any updates on their part in the future.

When creating presentation content with Copilot, it will look at the layouts in your template. It will try using the best fit for the content it wants to create from your prompts. The following are three categories of elements to consider with a short explanation for each.

Placeholder size variety

Since your Copilot assistant will be looking for placeholders to add its suggested content, if your template’s layouts only have placeholders of the same size, text content will likely look cluttered.

This is caused by my first pain-point with AI-created slides: there is usually too much text . My suggestion is to plan various placeholder sizes, as recommended by Microsoft, so your corporate template will be usable by Copilot, but plan on having some text-reduction work to do after AI slides are created.

Slide layout variety

To accommodate the size variety recommendation, you inevitably have to create more layouts. If you only use standard layouts, it will also be difficult for Copilot to create varied content. You should plan on creating more than one version for each layout you have. As an example, if you customized the Two Content standard layout, duplicate it and change one of the content placeholders to a picture placeholder. The same thing for your Title Slide layout: add versions with or without images or graphic elements.

Naming layouts

The last recommendation category is to use a clear naming convention for your layouts. This means indicating clearly why a layout is created. Copilot will understand words such as Title , Agenda , Section , Content and Conclusion , so make sure to use them.

You can find the link to Microsoft’s blog post, Keep your presentations on brand with Copilot in PowerPoint , in the Further reading section. They also have some examples illustrating each recommendation.

Using the Copilot PowerPoint Starter Template

At the bottom of Microsoft’s blog post, you will also find a download link for a PowerPoint file named Copilot PowerPoint Starter Template Nov 2024 . I should add that you must scroll down past the Feedback section of the post to find the gray box allowing download.

The starter template includes nine slides that explain the size recommendation and give you the steps you need to copy and paste starter layouts in your own files. You also have links to additional resources on the last slide.

Be warned that meeting Copilot’s need for various sizes and layouts will add 43 layouts to your actual template. It’s a starting point to quickly add layouts with a naming convention that will help Copilot, but you will need to customize them with your own branding, too.

As you probably realize now, making your template compatible with Copilot is not just a matter of a few extra clicks of the mouse; however, the time invested here will help you get better results when using Copilot to create content.
