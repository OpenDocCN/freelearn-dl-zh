The Cumbersome Government Contracting Process

Government contracting is the process by which government entities — federal, state, or local — procure goods, services, or projects from private companies. It presents businesses with significant opportunities to secure large, stable contracts that can drive growth and establish credibility. However, the process is complex, requiring contractors to navigate:

  • Intricate requirements
  • Lengthy timelines
  • Rigorous compliance standards

A clear breakdown of the intricacies of the process means businesses can better assess the resources they need, and the value of innovative technologies that aim to reduce these inefficiencies.

Step 1: Identifying opportunities

The process begins with identifying opportunities in government solicitations. Contractors must sift through databases to filter Requests for Information (RFIs) and Requests for Proposal (RFPs).

FIGURE 1: The three types of procurement request issued by government agencies. Sometimes, Requests for Quotes (RFQs) are used to supplement or replace RFIs.

RFIs, as exploratory documents, seek industry feedback on potential projects. Businesses evaluate these to determine alignment with their capabilities, requiring research into agency needs, market conditions, and competitors.

RFIs are often stuck behind very cumbersome interfaces. You’ve got to apply all the filters to a table, click through multiple pages of a web application, and hope you find the data you need. This process alone can consume significant time and resources.”

– Anthony Brown

According to Order.co, another organization dedicated to procurement automation, an RFI generally includes:

  • A project overview
  • The time frame for performance
  • Specific project details
  • Guidance for interested suppliers
  • Room for more information

Step 2: Responding to RFIs

Responding to an RFI requires demonstrating industry expertise by drafting thoughtful responses that outline potential approaches, technologies, and pricing models — often without a guaranteed contract. This involves:

  • Gathering input from experts
  • Analyzing market trends
  • Crafting a narrative to position the company as a credible partner

Step 3: Reviewing and analyzing RFPs

Once the RFP is issued, the stakes increase significantly. The RFP details the exact requirements for the project, including deliverables, timelines, and compliance expectations.

Contractors must carefully review these documents, often hundreds of pages long, to ensure they understand every nuance. This step may involve creating compliance matrices, where every requirement in the RFP is mapped to how the company intends to address it. Missing a single detail can result in disqualification.

The compliance matrix

In the Association of Proposal Manager Professionals (APMP) compliance matrix template, the columns that are described as “Generally used” are:

  • Section
  • Section/subsection title
  • Page
  • Requirement (stated with an active verb)
  • F (fully comply)
  • P (partially comply)
  • N (does not comply)
  • Response Reference (references name and page number of documentation that you supply as part of the response)
  • Comments

The APMP is an “internationally accepted authority” whose mission is “to be the trusted leader that serves an international community of bid and proposal development professionals”. It has over 14,100 members across 79 countries.

Step 4: Assembling the proposal

Crafting the proposal is one of the most labor-intensive stages of the process. Businesses bring together teams of writers, subject matter experts, graphic designers, and legal advisors to create a compelling and compliant response. This involves:

  • Content creation: Writing detailed explanations of how the company will meet the agency’s needs, including technical plans, timelines, and cost structures.
  • Compliance documentation: Ensuring all necessary certifications, financial records, and regulatory requirements are included.
  • Visual artifacts: Creating supporting materials like Gantt charts, diagrams, and architectural designs to enhance the proposal.

It’s a very long process. There are a lot of companies trying to prove that they’re the right one to actually perform the work. In some cases, these solicitations can take a year or more to actually win.”

– Anthony Brown

Step 5: Internal reviews and revisions

Before submission, proposals undergo multiple rounds of internal review to ensure accuracy and compliance. Teams scrutinize every word, number, and citation to ensure alignment with the RFP’s requirements. This iterative process often involves significant back-and-forth between departments, adding weeks to the timeline.

Step 6: Submission and waiting

After submission, contractors face a long waiting period as government agencies evaluate the proposals. During this time, businesses must be prepared to respond to follow-up questions or requests for additional documentation. This stage can take months, creating uncertainty for the company’s pipeline and resource planning.

Step 7: Post-award requirements

Winning the contract is only the beginning. Contractors must prepare for rigorous oversight and reporting requirements throughout the project’s lifecycle. From ensuring compliance with cybersecurity standards like CMMC and FedRAMP to managing ongoing audits, the administrative burden continues even after the award is secured. Each step represents a significant investment of time, effort, and resources for private businesses.

Without efficient tools or streamlined processes, contractors often find themselves overwhelmed by the sheer volume of work. This prolonged timeline often delays revenue generation and stretches resources thin, making government contracting a high-stakes, high-effort endeavor.

FIGURE 2: Diagram showing the time-consuming steps in the process of securing government contracts.

Many companies today rely on a patchwork of tools to manage the complexities of government solicitations and contracts. They might use Salesforce to track processes, separate platforms to store and draft documents, and bring in multiple subject matter experts to piece everything together. This fragmented approach creates inefficiencies and silos, making it difficult to assemble, analyze, and act on critical information. By integrating AI into a single workflow, the process can be streamlined – allowing teams to generate artifacts like Gantt charts, architectural designs, and detailed analyses with ease. It’s about replacing scattered workflows with an intelligent, seamless system.”

– Anthony Brown

Why Good Procurement is Imperative and Where the Funding Goes

In the fiscal year 2023, the US federal government “obligated $759 billion through contracts”. 60% of this total went to defense agencies and the other 40% to civilian agencies.

Besides the standard procurement contract, there are legal instruments called Other Transactions (OTs). They represent more flexible business arrangements deployed for R&D or prototyping activities, accounting for a further $15.7 billion in spending in the same year.

The US Government Accountability Office (GAO) website highlights a government-wide goal to award roughly a quarter of contract dollars to small businesses — 23% by the Small Business Act of 1953 — with the intention of promoting participation. This quota suggests a good opportunity for SMBs willing to take steps to optimize their processes, though there is some uncertainty about how the 2025 administration will look upon small businesses going ahead.

Fraud and bias in procurement

Procurement fraud typically accounts for less than 10% of total civil fraud recoveries under the 1863 False Claims Act (FCA). On the defense side, roughly 20% of the DoD’s ongoing Inspector General investigations are related to procurement fraud. While the scale of fraud is an order of magnitude smaller than that seen in healthcare — measured by number of FY23 cases brought by qui tam whistleblowers — it nonetheless constitutes misuse of taxpayer money and undermines both military readiness and public safety.

Historical cases of defense fraud in the Navy include the 2015 Fat Leonard bribery scandal, and the FBI’s Operation III Wind in the 1980s that saw the conviction of over 50 people — including 9 government officials and multiple defense company executives — for exchanging inside information and illicit favors. Operation III Wind led Congress to enact a broad series of reforms, including the Competition in Contracting Act of 1984 and the Procurement Integrity Act of 1988, in an attempt to level the playing field. The Federal Acquisition Regulation of 1984 formalized many procurement processes, including the use of RFIs.

AI solutions have a natural place in these contexts. For example:

  • Detecting fraud: Using historical procurement data to identify patterns associated with legitimate transactions, and flagging anomalies in bidding behavior, pricing, and contract alterations.
  • Reducing bias: Anonymizing applications and scoring vendors based on objective criteria, minimizing subjective judgment.
  • Improving transparency: Simplifying or summarizing complex legal or technical documents, making procurement more accessible to all stakeholders.

How to think about compliance

Procurement deals are struck for a wide range of products and services. Fixed wing aircraft, guided missiles, and professional engineering support comprised some of the top FY23 defense spends, while drugs, surgical equipment, precious metals, and general healthcare services were among the top civilian spends. It is clear that businesses must maintain a strong regulatory focus if they wish to fulfil these contracts competently.

Anthony Brown, Former Head of Data and AI at Procurement Sciences, looks at compliance as the coming together of two different areas:

  • Data sensitivity: Personal information and sensitive classified information.
  • Proprietary information: Customer data, and how to store and process it without violating federal requirements.

Most of our customer base in the federal market are working with the Department of Defense, so it’s even higher sensitivity to those requirements than just the general government. For government agencies like the Veterans Association, as an example, there’s an even higher sensitivity.”

– Anthony Brown

Procurement in the EU

In the EU, public authorities spend around €2 trillion per year on procurement. The European Commission (EC) website states that the public sector comprises the majority of buyers in transport and education, among other areas. The front page also spotlights the EC’s ability to completely exclude countries like Russia from participation in procurement contracts in the EU, as a method of sanction.

The Most Advantageous Tender (MAT) is the framework used for reward evaluation in the EU, considering factors like innovation, pricing, quality, and environmental impact. EU businesses have the right to compete for contracts in any EU country.

High-level workflow overview

Before exploring the platform’s technologies, we’ll break down the core workflow steps it supports.

  1. Priming the AI: During onboarding, users input details like their industry and expertise, which are tied to taxonomies and a knowledge base. This primes the AI to deliver tailored recommendations and actions.
  2. Opportunity identification: The platform’s AI-powered Bid Search taps into a centralized, daily-updated data warehouse that aggregates information from sources such as SAM.gov. Opportunities are displayed in a structured format, and semantic search enables users to query naturally, avoiding complex navigation and saving significant time.
  3. Qualification: Users qualify identified opportunities by assessing their alignment with company capabilities and strategic goals. Uploaded documents, such as competitive intelligence and capability statements, are analyzed by AI to provide deeper insights and actionable suggestions.
  4. Pipeline integration: Once users identify a relevant solicitation, they can add it to their pipeline. This centralized space helps organize tasks, store related documents, and maintain a logical sequence of actions for each solicitation.
  5. Planning: Procurement Sciences facilitates planning by assisting users in organizing timelines, allocating resources, and defining proposal steps. AI generates artifacts like Gantt charts and diagrams while analyzing uploaded documents to optimize strategy and assess risks.

Proposal generation and fine-tuning: Using AI within Procurement Sciences’ content builder, users create proposals with streamlined processes for compliance analysis, content drafting, diagram creation, research integration, and rapid RFI generation. The platform also supports iterative fine-tuning, enabling users to refine and align proposals with their strategy and goals through collaborative AI tools.
  7. Submission and post-submission: Finalized proposals can be exported in multiple formats for submission. Post-submission tools include AI-driven debrief analysis, protest evaluation, and pattern tracking to inform future improvements.

Data vault modeling

The Awarded AI platform uses data vault modeling to structure its data, supporting scalability and traceability.

This enterprise design pattern, or schema, consists of three types of entities, hubs, links, and satellites.

FIGURE 7: Data vault modeling.

  • Hubs represent business entities and contain only their unique identifiers. For example, agencies, vendors, and contracts.
  • Links define relationships between hubs. For example, links between vendors and solicitations, or between agencies and contracts.
  • Satellites store descriptive attributes and historical changes related to hubs or links, enabling a complete audit trail. For example, vendor or agency attributes, or vendor bid modifications over time.

Data vaults are scalable, flexible to refactoring, and reduce data redundancy compared to star or snowflake schemas.

Neo4j is used to build knowledge graphs. These graphs represent relationships between entities such as companies, agencies, and solicitations, providing valuable insights into industry dynamics.

LLMs in the Context of the Solution

Inference as strength

The strength of the LLM in the Procurement Sciences use case comes from its ability to infer the unsaid. Anthony compares the RFI to a legal document, in which things are written in a very specific way, using statements like shall, must, and we. “These are a legal language that is also a part of a solicitation, because it is a contract that [businesses] are signing up to fulfill.”

FIGURE 8: Contract language that might be absent in a solicitation.

The difference is that an RFI “might not say must, shall, or we…but it actually does”. The RFI will imply that something is expected to be a requirement, and the onus is on businesses to infer it correctly.

Prompt engineering in Awarded AI

There is already much discussion about how to approach prompt engineering, including in vendor documentation and some of TechLeader’s other reports. Below are the broad strokes of the Procurement Sciences approach, which Anthony calls “basic but effective”.

Few-shot prompts: “If I’m saying I want an RFP or an RFI or a particular section of one, I would provide in the prompt an example.”

Not giving the model too many instructions: The memory limitations of the model are relevant here. Anthony gives an example:

  • Bad prompt type: “Create me an RFI and here’s the solicitation you need to reference.”

This prompt could reasonably create a decent outline of an RFI, but each individual section wouldn’t have the desired quality.

  • Good prompt type: “You’re going to create this RFI. This is the type of persona that you’re taking on. Here’s some of the initial information that you’re looking for. Here’s an example of what that information will look like. Now ask the user these particular questions. Start with this one first.”

Now, the model begins a conversation with the user and only outputs sections as it is given the relevant context. The output is specific to one particular task, instead of the model trying to complete three or four tasks at the same time.

We often contradict ourselves. We’ll ask somebody to do a task, and we’ll give [them] so much information that the first part of the task might contradict the last part. We try to scope it down to what is achievable, so we don’t have those contradictions and we’re not trying to do too much inside one part of the document — especially because the output context is limited on these language models. As you start to reach those token limits on the output, that’s where you get hallucinations.”

– Anthony Brown

Evolution of model reasoning

The Procurement Sciences team continues to update how their application interacts with the latest LLMs. “Something that we did notice is, as these innovations happen with the models and their reasoning capabilities improve, the prompting cannot necessarily stay the same.”

Defining reasoning

Anthony expresses skepticism in our interview about whether or not a model is actually “reasoning”, by our current understanding of the word. Here are some relevant directions to explore for answers:

  • Chain of thought: This is similar to how human reasoning happens. Chains of thought can be monitored for compliance or alignment purposes.
  • The intentionality argument: LLMs don’t “reason” about problems, they predict answers based on probabilities.
  • Other “reasoning” strategies: Self-consistency (running multiple solutions and selecting the most frequent answer), Tree of Thoughts (ToTs), program-aided language models (PALs), fine-tuning on reasoning tasks.

Known LLM weaknesses: Hallucinations, abstract and counterfactual reasoning (cannot imagine alternate realities reliably), long-horizon reasoning (break down on multi-step or complex logic chains), lack of internal consistency (can contradict themselves within the same conversation).

The million-dollar question: If it looks like reasoning and acts like reasoning, is it reasoning?

The same types of prompts that you used with a 3.5 and a 4, or even a 4o in some cases, might generate different outputs when using o1 or o1 preview.”

– Anthony Brown

The more advanced “reasoning” capabilities of some of the newer models means that prompts can become lost in translation, according to Anthony. “It might actually play the game of telephone; take what you first said, somehow convert that in the [reasoning] process, then end up with an output that you totally didn’t want or expect.”

Indeed, tools like Claude 3.7 Sonnet and DeepSeek can expose model reasoning explicitly, laying out their plan for responding to a given user query. Future improvements could allow the user to stop model generation so they can correct parts of the plan, if they feel like the wrong course of action is being pursued.

These reasoning models are coming up with their own plan, and no longer is the human in each one of those steps. So, if those initial prompts somehow directed the model to think, “I’m going to make a plan this way”, when your original prompt actually didn’t focus on a plan being generated that way, you could end up with different outputs.”

– Anthony Brown

Business Challenges

Challenge #1: Security-conscious clients

Procurement Sciences found that risk-aversion and restriction are embedded in government contractor culture. Employees may explore LLMs in their own time, but on work time tools like ChatGPT are often blocked wholesale, in Anthony’s experience, including at his previous organization. He cites the data spillage concerns raised by clients:

  • “Where does the data go when I sync my documents with your application?”
  • “How do I know that the output is factual?”
  • “How do I know that the information is accurate enough that it can be used in an RFP, since government contracts require clarity and accuracy?”

The fear is not directed towards adoption of AI in general, Anthony claims, but emerges as a result of the stringent requirements that organizations must comply with.

If your whole company is based on working with the government on contracts, and you haven’t complied with some regulation, and that’s found to be real, you could face penalties, fines, things like that. You could be barred from applying to those contracts. So, there’s real monetary loss, right? There’s real fear about the business actually not being able to operate.”

– Anthony Brown

Solution #1: A large customer success team

Beyond being able to reassure clients with their choice of secure hosting provider, which this report has already covered, Procurement Sciences emphasises a heavy support presence as one of its key differentiators. “We’re going to help you along with how to actually interact with the technology with guides, videos, training, events.”

This support includes daily webinars that are recorded and distributed to clients. There are also guides built into the application itself, in the form of direct walkthroughs for particular tasks, and pre-generated prompts presented as assistants. “They can just select the actual assistant for the task that they want to do and not have to learn how to become a prompt engineer.”

Solution #1: Baking in traceability

Procurement Sciences took two steps to allow clients to manually verify the accuracy of their application’s outputs:

  • The RFI/RFP is addressed by the compliance matrix output on a section-by-section basis: This makes it convenient for users to check their eventual proposal against the relevant part of the RFI or RFP manually, to see if anything was missed by the AI.
  • Citations are displayed on outputs: When an output is generated, the application cites the specific documents and contexts that were used to achieve it. This is a basic expectation that is already built into research tools like Google’s NotebookLM.

Internally, the Procurement Sciences team also uses the open-source LLM engineering platform, Langfuse, to conduct evaluations on accuracy, completeness, and hallucinations using a given set of criteria. The platform uses conversation traces to assess, for example, the quality of diagrams produced by the application (“did they get a diagram, or did they get an image?”). This helps the team to understand where the gaps are and where to do better, “whether it be through prompting that we can provide to improve the outputs, or the tool calling that we’re doing based on the intent of what a user types in the chat and within interactions with the models.”

Key insights for tech leaders

Take time to understand steps in complex workflows: Centralized platforms can reduce reliance on disparate tools that aim to only address parts of processes.

Scope tasks sensibly: Pre-built, domain-specific AI prompts can be mapped to each step of a workflow, with agents handing off between steps. By keeping the scope focused, token limits do not become an issue, and accuracy and reliability are maintained.

Prioritize compliance and traceability for risk-conscious clients: Automated compliance matrices trace document requirements back to source sections, ensuring traceability and trust. Advanced language models detect nuanced requirements, reducing risks of disqualification.

Plan to integrate with existing client platforms: Using secure cloud environments like Azure Government Cloud ensures adherence to compliance mandates like FedRAMP and CMMC. In Procurement Sciences’ case, Azure Gov Cloud was also already being used by many of its clients.

Play to the strengths of LLMs: The inference abilities of LLMs — their ability to detect nuanced requirements — are crucial to the success of the Procurement Sciences solution, as well as the adoption of different LLM models for different use cases.

Think about how to unlock useful insight from data: AI solutions can uncover unique contract opportunities through semantic analysis and dynamic knowledge graphs. Emerging capabilities, such as text-to-SQL tools, have the potential to further enhance market intelligence and visualization.

Knowledge graphs in procurement

A knowledge graph, or semantic network, is a diagram illustrating the relationship between entities, defined by IBM as “objects, events, situations or concepts”.

FIGURE 11: A very simple knowledge graph, consisting of nodes as entities and edges as relationships between entities.

Procurement Sciences already uses Neo4j to create graph representations of government procurement entities, which provide valuable context for their models. These entities include agencies, companies, solicitations, and contract locations.

To develop these capabilities, they are looking into other Graph RAG technologies like LangChain and Microsoft’s recent open source GraphRAG.

Use case: If a company is pursuing a contract, Procurement Sciences could recommend potential partners based on:

  • Past performance
  • Proximity
  • Shared employee history, such as identifying former employees who now work at a relevant organization

Anthony notes that these types of hidden relationships aren’t always obvious in traditional relational databases but can be surfaced through graph-based network analysis.

More generally, graph-based models may prove crucial in mitigating procurement bias. Mapping relationships between vendors, officials, and contract outcomes could bring to light suspicious collusion, hidden connections, or repeated irregularities.

Conclusion

Procurement Sciences chose to focus on a small part of the complex workflow of procurement, delivering it securely and with due emphasis on customer support. AI is a core part of their offering; they have taken seriously the benefits and drawbacks of using LLMs in an industry where compliance is non-negotiable, and baked them into their approach. Their proof of success is not only in the efficiency gains they have been able to demonstrate, but also in the profile of their clients.

For other leaders in technology, consider:

  • Deep vertical expertise as a competitive differentiator: Use insider knowledge to build solutions that directly address industry-specific pain points. There is strategic advantage to combining technical expertise with profound industry understanding.
  • Well-targeted AI as a core enabler for significant efficiency gains: Leaders should consider how well-targeted AI applications can yield similar step-change improvements within their own organizations.
  • Compliance and security in regulated industries: For companies operating in or serving highly regulated sectors like government, a strong focus on compliance and data security is paramount. Leaders must be aware of potential trade-offs, such as limited access to the latest technology.
  • The importance of user-centric AI implementation: Recognize that end-users may be unfamiliar or risk-averse towards AI, so you can make sure technology delivers tangible value without requiring users to become AI experts.

Breakthroughs in government procurement processes have global impact, influencing millions directly and shaping policies that may affect billions worldwide. Targeted efforts in such vital parts of our ecosystem (healthcare, supply chain, finance, energy) will be similarly wide-ranging.
