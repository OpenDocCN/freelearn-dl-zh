Using Presenter View

I often hear from presenters that they want to have all their text on slides because they fear forgetting what to say. Lack of practice is often a problem, but some presenters fear public speaking so much that they need more support when using mostly visual slides.

That is why speakers need to learn how to leverage technology to help them ease their fears. PowerPoint has been providing Presenter View for some time now, helping presenters see their notes while the audience sees only their slides. Now is the time to learn how to leverage all its power through the topics discussed in this chapter:

  • Defining and starting Presenter View
  • Using the display and navigation tools
  • Using the annotation tools
  • Using other tools
  • Making your presentation more accessible with live subtitles

Technical requirements

The Presenter View feature is available in all versions of PowerPoint, but there could be differences in the tools available depending on the version you are using. Be aware that since the subscription version of PowerPoint is updated on an ongoing basis, it is possible that screenshots shown in this chapter might differ from your version of the application.

Defining and starting Presenter View

Presenter View i s a PowerPoin t feature that allows presenters to see their speaking notes and various tools on their computer while the audience only sees the slides in their presentation. We’ll see how easy it is to set up and describe the various parts of the Presenter View window.

If you are using any recent version of PowerPoint, as soon as you connect your computer to a second monitor, a projector, or a TV screen, PowerPoint should automatically set up Presenter View for you. When you are working with two monitors permanently connected to your computer, there might be times when you don’t want to use it, so let’s see where Presenter View can be turned on or off.

From the Slide Show tab ( 1 ), go to the Monitors group ( 2 ) and check or uncheck the Use Presenter View box ( 3 ) according to your needs ( Figure 13.1 ) :

Figure 13.1 – Activating or deactivating Presenter View in the Slide Show tab

The Monitor drop-dow n list ( 4 ) allows you to choose on which display y ou want to see Presenter View. The Automatic setting is usually the default one, allowing PowerPoint to choose the display automatically. We will see an option in Presenter View to change the display if it is not appropriate.

Starting the slide show can be done directly in the Slide Show tab by using the From Beginning button ( 5 ), starting your presentation on the first slide, or using the From Current Slide button ( 6 ). You can also use the Slide Show button on the far right of the status bar ( 7 ) to start the slide show from the selected slide. You can also use the following keyboard shortcuts to start your slide show:

  • From the first slide: F5
  • From the current slide: Shift + F5

    You may need to press the fn (function) key + F5 on some external keyboards.

Once you start the slide show, you will see Presenter View on your main monitor and the slide displayed on the second monitor. If you want to practice your presentation with Presenter View but don’t have access to a second monitor, projector, or TV, you will need to first start your slide show and then right-click your mouse to get the contextual me nu wi th the Show Presenter View option ( 1 ) ( Figure 13.2 ) :

Figure 13.2 – Accessing Presenter View with only one monitor

Now that we have seen how to start Presenter View, let’s go over the main sections of the window ( Figure 13.3 ) :

Figure 13.3 – Exploring Presenter View’s sections

  • The first section shows your actual slide and the various delivery tools you can access ( 1 ).
  • The seco nd secti on shows a preview of your next animation or next slide ( 2 ), allowing you to see what is coming next.
  • In the third section, you will see your speaker notes ( 3 ) if you added any during your content creation phase. It now allows adding notes in this section during your presentation. There’ll be more on that later in the chapter.
  • You also have navigation buttons ( 4 ) underneath the slide preview, showing you the slide number of your current slide and the total number of slides in your presentation.
  • The main sections in the window can be resized by clicking and dragging the line between the actual slide and Next slide /notes, or the one between Next slide and notes ( 5 ).
  • At the top of the window, you have a section to manage your displays ( 6 ).

The previous example shows my own resized sections’ display. The default display usually shows a much bigger Next slide thumbnail, which can be confusing for most presenters because it is right above the speaker notes for the current slide.

Many times, when I’ve helped speakers rehearse with Presenter View, I found most were confused because their notes did not match the slide right above them – I must say it still happens to me, so I always make the next slide smaller. I suggest that you resize the Next slide section and make it much smaller, so your eyes can make sense of the upcoming visual, and you have even more space for your notes.

Using the display and navigation tools

When you start your s lide show with Presenter View, you might need to change how it is displayed, so we will first have a look at the display tools ( Figure 13 .4 ):

Figure 13.4 – Using the display tools in Presenter View

If you need to look for something in another window on your computer while in a slide show, you will quickly realize you don’t see your taskbar when using Presenter View, so using the SHOW TASKBAR tool ( 1 ) could be helpful. It works like a toggle that you click to show or hide your taskbar. Be aware that it displays the taskbar on the primary monitor by default. If you do not want to display the taskbar below the slide seen by the audience, you will need to use the DISPLAY SETTINGS tool.

The DISPLAY SETTINGS tool ( 2 ) allows you to change the device on which you want Presenter View displayed by using the Swap Presenter View and Slide Show setting ( 3 ).

If you start your presentation and Presenter View opens on the wrong screen, don’t panic. Simply swap it over by clicking on Swap Presenter View and Slide Show . I have used it many times during training sessions so that people would see Presenter View on a big screen to help them learn about the feature.

The Duplicate Slide Show setting ( 4 ) will show only the sl ide o n your computer and the audi ence’s screen.

You can select END SLIDE SHOW ( 5 ) from the display tools or simply use Esc on your keyboard.

To help you deliver your presentation, let’s now have a closer look at the actual slide section with its tools to help you keep on track and navigate through your slides ( Figure 13.5 ):

Figure 13.5 – Presenter View tools to keep you on track and navigate your slides

Right a bove t he slide preview, you can see a timer ( 1 ) dis playing the duration of your presentation. It starts counting as soon as you open Presenter View, meaning that it will keep counting while you are getting ready to start your presentation with the audience.

If you want to rely on the timer to know how long you have been presenting, you might appreciate the Pause the timer and Restart the timer tools ( 2 ) right next to it. But you do have the time of day displayed ( 3 ) in the upper-right corner of the slide preview. For example, during training sessions, I find the time of day convenient to keep track of when I need to give people a break.

To navigate through your slides, you can use the Advance to next animation or slide button ( 4 ) or Return to previous animation or slide button ( 5 ), although I think a much more efficient way is to use keyboard keys or a presentation remote. If you need a refresher on keyboard shortcuts, click on the More slide show options button ( 6 ) and select Help to access the whole list.

The final navigation tool you should know about is the See all slides button ( 1 ), allowing you to access all your slides in a new window ( 2 ) ( Fig ure 13.6 ):

Figure 13.6 – Using the See all slides tool in Presenter View

The new window will completely overlap your original window, so the first thing to know is that clicking on the Back arrow ( 3 ) brings you back to Presenter View. In my ex ample, t he sections created i n the file are shown in the left pane of the window ( 4 ), which can be helpful to find specific content more quickly than just using the scroll bar on the right ( 5 ). Clicking on any slide thumbnail brings you back to Presenter View. If you decide you don’t want to change to another slide, simply click on the Back arrow ( 3 ).

As you can see, using Presenter View helps you manage your displays more easily and improves your flexibility with audience questions or comments by being able to navigate your whole presentation quickly, even if you have not planned any navigation elements in your presentation. Let’s now move on to another set of tools that can help you deliver more engaging presentations – the annot ation tools.

Using the annotation t ools

When you click on the Pen and Laser Pointer tools bu tton ( 1 ) below the slide preview in Presenter View, you get access to nice annotation tools that can be easily used directly on the slide preview thumbnail ( F igure 13.7 ):

Figure 13.7 – Accessing the annotation tools in Presenter View

Selecting either the Laser Pointer , Pen , or Highlighter tool options ( 2 ) changes your mouse cursor to the selected type, such as Laser Pointer in my example ( 3 ), making it much easier for the audience to follow your cursor. You can draw over an element on your slide with the laser pointer to briefly grab attention. If you are using the pen or highlighter, your drawings remain on your slide.

After changing your cursor to any of the tools, you can change its color ( 4 ) before using it on your slide. You can use the Eraser and Erase All Ink on Slide ( 5 ) tools to remove part of your annotations or all of them at once – the tools are active only when you have annotations on your slide.

Arrow Options ( 6 ) is helpful only if you plan on moving your mouse cursor over what is displayed to the audience. When you are in slide show mode using Presenter View, what it does in the background is extend the view of your screen as if you had a very large monitor, on which the left part is used to display your speaker view, while the right part is used for the full-screen slide for the audience. I suggest that you leave the setting as Automatic and use the annotation tools if you need to highlight something on your slide for the audience. It will make things easier to follow than just having a regular mouse pointer moving over the slide.

If you used any of the annotation tools, you need to go back to the list and click on the one you activated to turn it off. When you finish your presentation, any annotations left on your slides will trigger a dialog box, asking you whether you want to keep or discard them. If you need to refer back to them after your presentation is over, keep them, and they will show as editable ink objects on your slides.

Using other too ls

We will not necessarily go through all the remaining tools and settings available in Presentation View. Instead, we will focus on Zoom into the slide ( 1 ), Black or unblack slide show ( 2 ), and Click to add notes ( 3 ) while you are presenting ( Figure 13.8 ):

Figure 13.8 – Extra useful tools in Presenter View

I f you want the audience to have a closer look at a specific element on your slide, using the Zoom into the slide feature ( 1 ) is an excellent way to do it ( Figure 13.9 ):

Figure 13.9 – The Zooming into the slide feature in Presenter View

  • After clicking on the magnifying glass icon ( 1 ), moving your cursor over the slide will show you a rectangle with a magnifying glass icon, helping you decide the area to zoom in ( 2 ).
  • When you click, the slide gets zoomed in ( 3 ), showing a hand instead of the regular mouse cursor. If you left-click your mouse and maintain it, you can drag the image to move the zooming effect around your slide. You can change the zoom level using the Ctrl key + scrolling up or down with your mouse wheel.
  • When you want to go back to your whole slide view, simply click on the magnifying glass icon again ( 2 ) or the Esc key on your keyboard.

This feature is easy to use and creates a simple but effective zooming effect to help improve your audience’s understanding.

Sometimes, helping our audience understand wh at we are saying means helping them focus on us without distractions, and this is when you can leverage the Black or unblack slide show feature ( 1 ) ( Figure 13.10 ):

Figure 13.10 – Using the Black or unblack slide show feature to improve audience focus

When you click on the monitor icon ( 1 ), it becomes highlighted, and the slide preview becomes black. I would suggest using this feature when you need to talk and the slide visual is not relevant to what you have to say. You will see people’s eyes focus back on you, helping them concentrate on what you say.

Here are some ways to change the black screen to white:

  • Right-click the black slide ( 2 ), go to Screen ( 3 ), then select White Screen ( 4 ).
  • Click on More slide show options ( 5 ), then select Screen and White Screen .
  • Use the W key twice on your keyboard. It will first remove the black screen, then apply the white screen.

Making your slide preview white can make a great canvas to use annotation tools to sketch something to help the audience understand. If you try this trick, just be aware that your annotations cannot be saved, as you are drawing over the hidden slide.

When you want to resume your presentation, click again on the Black or unblack slide show icon ( 1 ) – you can also press the B key or the Esc key on your keyboard. If you have used the white screen, it will not stay for the next time. You will need to go back to the options settings to change it again.

If you are presenting in a room with an overhead projector and use the white screen, you may have a white light shining on your face. Before your presentation, go over your presentation once so you can test settings and equipment, making sure everything is going as planned.

If you need to take some notes while you are presenting, Presenter View has received a nice upgrade, allowing M365 users to add their notes while presenting. The first clue that lets you know this feature is available in your version is seeing the Click to add notes text ( 1 ) when you don’t already have speaker notes for your slide ( Figure 13.11 ):

Figure 13.11 – Adding notes to your slide in Presenter View

To add notes, simply click in the notes section and type in your text ( 2 ). You will also see a white outline ( 3 ) for the section. When you finish your presentation, your notes will be in the notes pane available under your slides.

Now that we have covered many of the tools that can help you with your delivery, let’s see one last feature that will help you deliver presentations that are more accessible.

Making your presentation more accessible with live subt itles

If you are an M365 user and want to use this feature, make su re you use a good microphone connected to your computer, and make sure to have access to an internet connection to deliver your presentation. Using live subtitles is not only a way to make your presentation more accessible, but also a way to make your presentation easier to understand for everyone in your audience.

If you want to use subtitles during all your presentations, you can simply change the setting in the Slide Show tab ( 1 ) by checking the Always Use Subtitles box ( 2 ) ( Figure 13.12 ):

Figure 13.12 – Using subtitles for all your presentations

Accessing Subtitle Settings ( 3 ) will allow changing the settings of the subtitles:

  • Spoken Language and Subtitle Language ( 4 ): You need to select the language you will speak from the list supplied by Microsoft, and in what language you want the subtitles displayed.

Having subtitles in another language is supported only for M365 Business, Enterprise, and Education licenses. Some features may be limited in Business Basic and Education A1.

  • Microphone ( 5 ): If you plan on using the built-in microphone from your laptop, make sure to test it first. I have seen my share of laptop microphones not having the quality level needed to render quality live subtitles. If you are using an external microphone, make sure to select your device before starting your presentation.
  • Choose the position of your subtitles:
    • The first two options, Bottom (Overlaid) and Top (Overlaid) ( 6 ), will add captions at the bottom or top of your slides on a semi-transparent shape. I suggest you avoid those options because they require extra planning when you are creating your content to avoid covering important information; it also means less space on the slide for your content.
    • The following two options, Below Slide and Above Slide ( 7 ), are the best choices. Yes, it reduces the size of your projected slide, but your content will never be hidden by subtitles. If you are presenting in a large room and the bottom of the projection screen could be hidden by people, whether sitting at the front of the room or on a stage, you might want to consider using the Above Slide setting instead of the default Below Slide setting.

While in Presenter View, you can click on the Toggle Subtitles ( 1 ) icon to stop or start the feature. In the slide preview area, your words will be captured and then added in the position you chose. In the following example, it’s under the slide ( 2 ) ( Figure 13.13 ):

Figure 13.13 – Using subtitles during your presentation

  • If you need to make changes to your subtitle settings while in Presenter View, click on More slide show options ( 3 ) icon and then on Subtitle Settings ( 4 ). This is a quick way to change the position of the subtitles while presenting.
  • Clicking on More Settings… ( 5 ) opens the Captions & Subtitles dialog box ( 6 ). While presenting, it would be useful if you wanted to make Language changes ( 7 ).

Take note that if your spoken language is listed in the Preview section, it means the AI will not be as precise as it is for the fully supported languages.

When using subtitles during your presentation, you will need to be mindful of your speech pace and pronunciation to get more precise subtitle results.

Using subtitles might require extra equipment, depending on whether you are presenting online or in a venue. Consider getting assistance from a technology expert if you need help. It is worth the time and investment and will greatly improve the impact you will have on your audience.

Summary

In this chapter, you have seen how to use Presenter View and leverage many of its great tools to improve your presentation delivery, and how to make your presentations more accessible and inclusive by using subtitles.

Using Presenter View to help you reduce your fear of forgetting what to say is a great way to improve your presentation delivery. Just make sure that you don’t put very long scripts in the notes. Doing so might increase your stress level because you have to scroll through the notes, or it may even make your eyes focus on your computer instead of on your audience. Also, if you have a long script, reading it might make you sound unprepared and unnatural. The best advice I can give you is to aim to reduce your notes to very short sentences or keywords by the end of your delivery practices. That way, your notes become a reminder of the points you want to discuss.

As wonderful as I think Presenter View can be, if you think it will solve all your delivery problems, you are wrong! No tool can ever replace quality practice time; it can only help improve your performance because you have thoroughly practiced your presentation.
