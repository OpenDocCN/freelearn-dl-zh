Semantic Caches

Semantic caches represent an evolution of RAG concepts, forming a critical component in modern production-grade generative AI applications. They are particularly useful in agent-based RAG, to help offset the extra reasoning steps an agent typically takes. By leveraging vector search and intelligent query matching, semantic caches ensure users receive correct, consistent information while dramatically reducing latency and computational costs. This chapter explores how to build, optimize, and deploy semantic caches as an advanced control flow mechanism in your AI systems.

Understanding semantic caches with the long-tail pattern

Consider all of the queries that come into your application. If your query data is like the majority of RAG-based application query data out there, you will likely see a distinct pattern emerge. A small percentage of your queries will likely have significantly higher frequency than all the others. Often, there is a single query that comprises more than 20% of all traffic! On the other side of this spectrum, you will have a very significant number of queries, probably 60–70% of them, that are only requested a handful of times. This is called the long-tail pattern.

Figure 15.1 – Example of long-tail pattern applied to queries

The long-tail pattern (or the related Pareto index) is a statistical concept that is widely studied and applied across numerous fields. Still, in this case, we want to focus on the “head” of the data, the queries that represent the bulk of the queries passed to your RAG application. Ideally, you want to handle 100% of the queries gracefully, right? But at the very least, aim for the “head” of this chart, the top 20%, which gives you 80% coverage in terms of query usage in your application. That is already better than the best applications out there, where the top-performing agent companies (I won’t name names!) brag in their advertising about the successful processing of 68% of queries! Given that, even aiming for complete coverage of the top 20% is daunting in all but the smallest applications.

But wait, if we have a well-designed agent, can’t it just determine the right response path for each query dynamically? Isn’t the whole point of an agent to be able to instantly handle 100% of the queries? Yes, you could say that, but let’s speak plainly. Beyond the extra latency and cost, designing an agent to be that effective is very difficult and just not very likely. The reality is that the more effective your agent is at processing the most random query, the trade-off is always in the form of higher latency and inference costs. The agent literally has to “reason” its way through a new query every time. That means multiple LLM calls. The typical response time for a single LLM call, especially in complex situations such as uncommon queries, is already 2–20+ seconds, which is often unacceptable for most consumer-facing applications. But we are likely looking at several of these calls to have a well-designed agent that handles all scenarios gracefully. There are all kinds of techniques that we talk about in this book and that you can read about in more detail elsewhere to reduce the cost and latency of these individual calls, reduce the number of them required for a final response, or reduce the amount of data retrieved and passed to the generation stage to improve inference times. There are numerous techniques that you will want to exhaust in addition to implementing a semantic cache. But ultimately, you are going to find that in attempting to have an agent handle the large variation in queries you will experience, while also keeping the latency and costs to an acceptable level on a production scale, you will run out of solutions. Maybe you are lucky and you have the budget to put all of this on a billion-dollar array of quantum computers that can process all of this in a fraction of a second (this scenario is likely only possible for those of you reading 5–10 years after this book’s publication, but I digress!), but for the rest of us, there are semantic caches.

Let’s get into the details of what a semantic cache really is, so you can better understand why it is so effective in an AI agent environment.

Fun fact

We mentioned that the long-tail pattern is a statistical concept. If you want to dive into the statistical mathematics around this, then you will want to learn more about the Pareto index. The Pareto index comes from the Pareto distribution, which is a mathematical model used to describe data with a “power-law” relationship, where a small number of items account for most of the effect. The Pareto index basically measures how “heavy” or “fat” the long tail is. The lower the Pareto index, the more pronounced the long-tail pattern. A statistician would use the Pareto index to quantify the long-tail pattern. A closely related concept is the Pareto principle (80/20 rule), which states that 20% of causes lead to 80% of effects, which is considered a special case of Pareto distributions. The Pareto index directly informs resource allocation decisions. For instance, with α = 1.0, optimizing the top 20% of query types covers ~80% of volume, while α = 2.0 might require handling 40% of query types for the same coverage. This mathematical relationship helps set realistic targets for chatbot performance metrics and development priorities. Additionally, tracking changes in the Pareto index over time reveals whether your chatbot’s usage is consolidating around core functions or diversifying into new areas, which is critical intelligence for product roadmap decisions and capability planning.

Semantic cache – the interceptor layer

When I say “interceptor” layer, I am talking about using the same vector search we spent multiple chapters on earlier in this book and aiming it at intercepting queries to replace the reasoning steps of the agent with super-fast, LLM inference-free semantic matches. Consider that the first step for most agents is not to respond to the user query but to come up with a plan for responding, typically involving a selection of tools that will enable them to answer the query effectively. We will call this tool selection a solution path. The most basic implementation involves intercepting an incoming query and using vector similarity search to match it to queries you have cached with existing solution paths, basically skipping the planning step of the agent and saving all the time and inference costs that that involves!

For the top 20% of queries at the very least, and as far down the long tail as we can go, we want to intercept them and make sure they get the right data. The statistics on semantic caches change daily, so I encourage you to look up the freshest numbers. Also, as I’ve preached here many times, the most important numbers are what you get from your own system, so set up trials to determine the best approach and measure the potential impact of this. But, generally, recent reports from AWS, OpenAI, and Intercom indicate that the most successful implementations maintained 600-millisecond-to-2-second responses with semantic cache-based “full-scale reasoning agents,” compared to 5–6 seconds for responses that were not handled by the semantic cache.

It is important to clarify that a semantic cache does not typically remove inference completely. It just replaces the planning stage of the agent. The proportion of the full response that this replaces varies significantly across different applications, but in a simple scenario, where you call the LLM once to reason (ultimately determining what data needs to get pulled for the RAG process), then that would be half of the processing. The other half is sending that data retrieved to the LLM in the traditional RAG sense.

So, in that scenario, you are reducing latency and inference costs for that first stage. The second stage still exists, with whatever latency and costs it incurs. The reason I said “typically” in a previous statement is that there are scenarios where all you need is the semantic cache, or where the semantic cache can improve the generation stage. For example, with “static” content, such as with a FAQ-type response, if you know exactly what you want the response to be, you can store that in the semantic cache, and it can just return that response with no inference needed at all. You can also store database-optimized SQL (or whatever you use for retrieval) that is specific to a query in the semantic cache, which reduces the speed of retrieval and the amount of data passed to the LLM in the generation stage, which helps improve that stage as well. But generally, it is important to understand that the primary benefit of the semantic cache is in improving the reasoning stage of the response process.

With a semantic cache, we are anticipating the queries the user is going to ask, and we are pre-determining the data we want to provide for that query. We are intercepting these queries with this “interceptor” layer. Next, we will discuss why we want to do this interception.

Core components of semantic caches

At their foundation, semantic caches consist of several essential components that work together to provide intelligent query matching. Query embeddings form the heart of the system, capturing semantic meaning in a high-dimensional vector space. These dense vector representations encode not just the words but the underlying concepts and relationships within each query.

Similarity thresholds serve as the decision boundaries for determining matches. Unlike binary exact-match systems, semantic caches operate on a continuum of similarity, requiring careful calibration to balance catching valid paraphrases and avoiding false matches.

Metadata acts like a detailed label on each cached item, providing crucial context that helps the system make smarter decisions. Think of it as attaching a comprehensive information card to every cached query-response pair. This metadata typically includes three key types of information:

  • Provenance information tracks where the cached answer came from, which data sources were consulted, which version of the model generated it, and what specific retrieval methods were used. This helps determine whether a cached answer is still trustworthy or whether it came from outdated sources.
  • Timestamps record when the cache entry was created and last accessed. For example, a cached answer about “current stock prices” from last week would be flagged as potentially stale, while an answer about “the capital of France” from months ago remains valid. These timestamps enable automatic cache invalidation for time-sensitive information.
  • Relevance scoring assigns quality metrics to each cache entry based on factors such as how well it answered the original query, user feedback, or how often it’s been successfully reused. A cache entry that consistently satisfies users might have a score of 0.95, while a partially relevant answer might score 0.70.

This rich metadata enables sophisticated filtering and management strategies. For instance, the system could prioritize recent, high-scoring cache entries from authoritative sources, or automatically purge entries older than a certain threshold for specific topic areas. It transforms the cache from a simple storage system into an intelligent knowledge repository that understands the quality, freshness, and reliability of its contents.

Perhaps most importantly, semantic caches store solution paths that map queries to appropriate agent actions. Rather than simply caching responses, modern implementations cache the route to generate correct answers, ensuring freshness while maintaining efficiency.

The intelligence layer between the query and the response

Semantic caches represent more than just a performance optimization. They constitute an intelligent decision layer that understands user intent. This intelligence manifests in several ways. The cache must distinguish between queries that appear similar but have different meanings, such as “What are Roth IRA tax benefits?” versus “What are Roth IRA withdrawal rules?” Both queries concern Roth IRAs, but they seek fundamentally different information.

The system must also handle the nuanced variations in how users express themselves. A financial advisor and a young investor might ask about the same concept using entirely different vocabulary, levels of formality, and assumed knowledge. The semantic cache must recognize these as equivalent while maintaining boundaries between distinct concepts.

This intelligence extends to understanding context and managing the temporal aspects of information. Some cached responses remain valid indefinitely (such as explanations of financial concepts), while others require real-time data (such as current stock prices). Modern semantic caches incorporate this understanding, routing appropriate queries to cached responses while ensuring time-sensitive requests receive fresh information.

Why use a semantic cache?

Let’s start with an example to illustrate the power of the semantic cache. This assumes you have set up automated population, which we will cover in step 7 of Code lab 15.1 later in this chapter. Without a semantic cache, every time an agent reasons through what to do with a query, that inference is conducted and then lost. Think about the value of that inference; let’s say it is $1. So, you spend $1 to get that inference, and then the next time a similar query comes in, you spend $1 again. Let’s say this is a popular query on a popular website, and you see that query 1,000 times a day. That’s $1,000 spent for all of that inference. Now, let’s introduce a semantic cache with that query and its solution path already stored. Here are the benefits you are getting:

  • Inference utilization: You only have to run the inference once, the first time you see the unique query. Compare this to the 1,000 times you have to run it without the semantic cache. Your utilization of the LLM inference in your system just went through the roof! This “utilization” is important as it reduces the strain on your entire AI infrastructure, which has numerous benefits, particularly in larger-scale implementations where you have to start balancing server loads.
  • Inference cost reduction: You still spend that first $1, but after that, you are reusing the same solution path. So, that 1,000 times a day still only costs $1 in inference with a semantic cache. With each agentic RAG query potentially costing significant compute resources for embedding, retrieval, and generation, serving repeated conceptual queries becomes prohibitively expensive at scale. Semantic caches can reduce these costs dramatically by recognizing when a new query is essentially asking something that already has a known solution path from an agent perspective. The more popular the query, the more you save!
  • Latency reduction: Now, add in the fact that the planning inference without a semantic cache might take 2–20 seconds, whereas the retrieval from the semantic cache takes maybe 0.03–0.3 seconds. This speed improvement transforms the user experience, making AI assistants feel more responsive and natural in conversation. This is a huge reduction in latency!
  • Broader semantic coverage: It isn’t just the one query that you are saving money and time on; anything that is semantically above the similarity threshold you use with your semantic cache is covered too! This means that one inference you did for query “A,” where queries “B,” “C,” “D,” and “E” all match 85% or higher to query “A,” only has to be carried out once for all of those queries.
  • More consistent experience: Consistency is a crucial requirement, particularly in domains such as finance or healthcare, where conflicting answers to similar questions could have serious consequences. By mapping semantically equivalent queries to the same solution path, these systems ensure users receive consistent, vetted information regardless of how they phrase their questions. Consistency is also a key aspect of quality, supporting the effort to deliver the same high-quality response every time. Let’s not forget, these LLMs are not deterministic and there is always a chance that a response might be different in a future inference iteration. That sort of surprise can wreak havoc in a professional agent-based application and can be avoided with a semantic cache!
  • More granular control: Semantic caches are often curated beyond just what the agent originally comes up with in their solution path planning. It is not uncommon for a company to look at certain queries in their semantic cache, determine some new tools that will answer the query even better, and then add that tool to the semantic cache solution path without having to change anything else. This gives you increased control that allows you to curate your user experience and how you want to enhance query handling.

So, semantic caches give you better inference utilization, reduce latency, decrease costs, improve consistency, and give you more control over your user experience. Is there anything a semantic cache can’t do? Not really, they are pretty amazing! But the biggest challenge of a semantic cache is setting it up properly so that it intercepts the right queries and doesn’t intercept the wrong ones. We will talk about that next as we discuss how to plan out your semantic cache implementation.

Proper semantic cache planning

What happens when you don’t find a match with your semantic cache? It will not take long for a query to come into your system that does not match anything in the semantic cache (above the threshold you set). At some level, you might consider this a failure, as you did not correctly anticipate every possible query that could come in. Don’t be too hard on yourself! This is a natural part of the semantic cache process. What happens next is just as important as the semantic cache itself, and there is a wide variety of approaches that companies take to handle what happens next. Here are a few of the options that are implemented.

Orchestrator/planner systems

Implement hierarchical decision frameworks that evaluate multiple response pathways simultaneously:

  • Key-value stores (50–60 milliseconds): Carry out exact-match lookups in key-value stores, which are essentially direct/exact matches, but with some important nuances. Consider that in many systems, the most popular queries (the “head” of the long tail described earlier) are often the same or very similar. It is common for applications to artificially ensure certain queries are exactly the same. For example, you might see a company send out an email with a link that triggers a query once it enters the application. This query has to go through the same process as every other query, but you know exactly what it is, so why use a process that will take significantly longer than a key-value match? We won’t cover this in the code lab, but it is common to add a key-valuestore layer in front of the semantic cache, similar to what we build in the code lab.
  • Semantic vector searches fallback (100 milliseconds to 2 seconds): If there are no key-value matches, we fall back to the most popular type of semantic cache implementation, where we use vector embeddings to represent cached queries, vectorize incoming queries, and use vector similarity search to find matches. This is a very common pattern that we have discussed in depth in previous chapters, only in this case, it is applied specifically to the queries so that we can match them to the solution path that would normally be provided by the planning agent. This step will be the primary focus of what we build in the code lab.
  • Full agent planning fallback (2–10 seconds): If all else fails, we are probably dealing with a new query that is not captured yet in our semantic cache. Our planning agent is the final fallback here. This is essentially where we are before we add a semantic cache, with a planning agent simply determining a path, and we follow it to gather the data and answer the user query. But now that we know how valuable a semantic cache is, we want to capture this plan for the next time we get that query, rather than just throwing it out. So, we still want to add this memory. We will review this type of mechanism in the code lab as well!

Before we get into the code lab, let’s talk about a few other approaches you can try on your own, such as the multi-tier fallback architecture.

Rule-based fallback systems

Provide deterministic fallback paths through natural language understanding models that detect intent and route to decision trees. Production implementations such as the Rasa framework’s Two-Stage Fallback combine intent classification with confidence thresholds to determine escalation paths. Dialogflow CX extends this pattern with generative fallback using custom prompt templates, effectively bridging rule-based and generative approaches.

Intent classification pathways

Create predefined response protocols by categorizing queries into distinct handling paths. FAQ queries route to structured templates, technical support triggers diagnostic trees, general conversation maintains personality consistency, and escalation protocols determine human handoff triggers based on complexity scores. This systematic approach ensures consistent handling across query types.

But handling cache misses is only half the equation. You also need smart policies for managing the cache itself.

Retrieval, population, and eviction policies

The effectiveness of a semantic cache depends not just on its architecture but on the intelligent strategies used to retrieve matches efficiently, populate it with queries, and maintain cache quality over time. These three interconnected policies, namely retrieval, population, and eviction, form the operational heart of any semantic caching system, determining both its coverage and precision.

In the code lab, we will primarily address the retrieval step. Then, after the code lab, we will talk more about how to populate and evict from your semantic cache. Let’s start with the retrieval-oriented code lab.

Code lab 15.1 – using semantic cache for retrieval

In this code lab, we’ll implement a semantic cache with vector search that intelligently catches similar queries even when phrased differently. We’ll start with a basic ChromaDB-backed cache using sentence transformers, then progressively enhance it with entity masking to generalize across specific values (such as dates or tickers), cross-encoder verification to reduce false positives, adaptive thresholds for different match types, and auto-population that learns from cache misses.

This means if your agent takes five seconds and costs $0.10 to answer the first query, the second query returns in milliseconds at virtually no cost, even though the user used completely different words.

Step 2 – setting up

Now we’ll import our libraries and create a single shared ChromaDB collection that every step in the notebook will reuse:

import re
import uuid
import chromadb
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer, CrossEncoder
COLLECTION_NAME = "semantic_cache"
client = chromadb.Client()
# Clean start when running all cells
try:
    client.delete_collection(COLLECTION_NAME)
    print("✓ Cleared existing semantic_cache collection")
except Exception:
    print("✓ No existing collection to clear")
collection = client.get_or_create_collection(
    name=COLLECTION_NAME,
    metadata={"hnsw:space": "cosine"}
)
def _new_id():
    return str(uuid.uuid4())

Here’s what’s happening in the preceding code block:

  • uuid is imported so we can generate unique IDs for cache entries instead of manually incrementing counters.
  • We define COLLECTION_NAME once ("semantic_cache") and create one global client and collection that all later cache classes will reuse.
  • On rerun, the code first tries to delete the collection so we start fresh, which is useful during iterative development. But be careful as this deletes any previous caches!
  • Then, using get_or_create_collection, we ensure the collection exists in a safe, idempotent way, no accidental duplicate creation or errors if it already exists.
  • The _new_id() helper function gives each cache entry a unique, collision-free identifier.

This way, each subsequent step (masked cache, verified cache, adaptive cache, etc.) builds on the same underlying collection, rather than trying to create and delete new ones.

Let’s now build our first semantic cache class that can store and retrieve similar queries.

Step 3 – basic semantic cache

Let’s build our foundation with a simple semantic cache that uses embeddings to find similar queries. This cache will convert text into vectors using a lightweight model (all-MiniLM-L6-v2), store them in ChromaDB with cosine similarity search, and retrieve cached responses when similar queries are detected. This cache response has a solution path associated with it, which is used when there is a match. The magic happens because semantically similar text produces similar vectors, even with different wording:

# Step 3: Cache Entry Structure
class SemanticCache:
    def __init__(
        self, embedder_model='all-MiniLM-L6-v2', collection_ref=None
    ):
        """Initialize embedding model and reuse the shared ChromaDB collection."""
        self.embedder = SentenceTransformer(embedder_model)
        self.collection = collection_ref if collection_ref is not None 
            else collection
    def add(self, query, soln_path):
        """Add query-solution path pair to cache"""
        embedding = self.embedder.encode(query).tolist()
        self.collection.add(
            embeddings=[embedding],
            documents=[query],
            metadatas=[{'query': query, 'soln_path': soln_path}],
            ids=[_new_id()]
        )
    def search(self, query, threshold=0.75):
        """Search for similar cached query"""
        embedding = self.embedder.encode(query).tolist()
        results = self.collection.query(
            query_embeddings=[embedding], n_results=1)
       
        if results.get('distances') and results['distances'][0]:
            score = 1 - results['distances'][0][0]
            if score >= threshold:
                metadata = results['metadatas'][0][0]
                return {
                    'soln_path': metadata['soln_path'],
                    'score': score,
                    'cached_query': metadata.get('query')
                }
        return None

This code creates a semantic cache that stores query-solution path pairs and retrieves them based on semantic similarity using the following steps:

  1. Initialization: The cache uses SentenceTransformer to convert text into embeddings and stores them in the ChromaDB collection we created earlier.
  2. Adding entries: The add method encodes queries into vectors and stores them with their corresponding solution paths (such as lookup_fact() or get_help_article()) rather than direct answers.
  3. Searching: The search method finds the most similar cached query and returns its solution path only if the similarity score exceeds the threshold (default 0.75).

Here is the code to run tests and see how this works:

    "lookup_fact('France', 'capital')")
cache.add("How do I reset my password?",

test_queries = [
    "What's the capital of France?",
    "Password reset instructions",
    "When are you open?",
    "What's the weather today?"
]
for q in test_queries:
    # Get the raw similarity score even if below threshold
    embedding = cache.embedder.encode(q).tolist()
    results = cache.collection.query(
        query_embeddings=[embedding], n_results=1)
   
    if results.get('distances') and results['distances'][0]:
        score = 1 - results['distances'][0][0]
       
        # Now check against threshold
        result = cache.search(q)
        if result:
            print(f" '{q}' → '{result['soln_path']}' (score: 
                {result['score']:.2f})")
        else:
            print(f" '{q}' → Match below threshold (score: {score:.2f})")
    else:
        print(f" '{q}' → No match (no cached queries)")

This basic implementation already saves us from redundant API calls for semantically similar questions. However, it still struggles with queries that contain different specific values (such as different years, tickers, or amounts). We don’t want to treat each of those as separate cache entries, so in the next step, we’ll introduce entity masking to generalize them.

Step 4 – entity masking for better generalization

Now we’ll tackle a common limitation of the basic semantic cache, which relate to queries that are semantically identical except for specific values, such as years, stock tickers, dollar amounts, or percentages. For example, “What was AAPL stock price in 2023?” and “What was TSLA stock price in 2024?” should route to the same tool; the only difference is which ticker and year get passed along. Without normalization, these would generate separate embeddings and fragment the cache.

Entity masking fixes this by replacing variable values with generic placeholders before embedding. For instance, AAPL and TSLA both become [TICKER], while 2023 and 2024 both become [YEAR]. This way, all variations collapse into the same masked query, “What was [TICKER] stock price in [YEAR]?”, and map to a single cached solution path.

Here’s the updated implementation:

# Step 4: Semantic Boundaries
class MaskedSemanticCache(SemanticCache):
    def mask_entities(self, text):
        """Replace specific entities with placeholders"""
        text = re.sub(r'\$[\d,]+', '[AMOUNT]', text)       # Money amounts
        text = re.sub(r'\b[A-Z]{2,5}\b', '[TICKER]', text) # Tickers
        text = re.sub(r'\b20\d{2}\b', '[YEAR]', text)      # Years
        text = re.sub(r'\d+(\.\d+)?%', '[PERCENT]', text)  # Percentages
        text = re.sub(r'\S+@\S+', '[EMAIL]', text)         # Emails
        return text
    def add(self, query, soln_path):
        """Add with entity masking"""
        masked_query = self.mask_entities(query)
        embedding = self.embedder.encode(masked_query).tolist()
        self.collection.add(
            embeddings=[embedding],
            documents=[masked_query],
            metadatas=[{
                'original_query': query,
                'masked_query': masked_query,
                'soln_path': soln_path
            }],
            ids=[_new_id()]
        )
    def search(self, query, threshold=0.75):
        """Search using masked query"""
        masked_query = self.mask_entities(query)
        embedding = self.embedder.encode(masked_query).tolist()
        results = self.collection.query(
            query_embeddings=[embedding],
            n_results=1
        )
        if results.get('distances') and results['distances'][0]:
            score = 1 - results['distances'][0][0]
            if score >= threshold:
                metadata = results['metadatas'][0][0]
                return {
                    'soln_path': metadata['soln_path'],
                    'score': score,
                    'cached_query': metadata.get('original_query'),
                    'masked_query': metadata.get('masked_query')
                }
        return None

While entity masking improves generalization, it also increases the risk of false positives, queries that look similar once masked but don’t actually share the same intent. To mitigate this, the next step adds a cross-encoder verification layer to confirm semantic matches before returning cached results.

Step 5 – cross-encoder verification

Up until now, our semantic cache has been good at recall, that is, catching queries that look different but mean the same thing. However, this comes at a cost: sometimes two queries may be close in vector space but do not actually have the same intent. For example, “What’s my balance?” and “What’s my account number?” could be considered “similar” by embeddings, but clearly require different responses.

This is where cross-encoder verification comes in. Unlike sentence embeddings (which independently encode each query), a cross-encoder takes both the candidate query and the cached query together and scores their similarity. It essentially re-reads the pair as if answering “Are these two queries asking the same thing?” This allows us to dramatically cut down false positives by applying a second, stricter filter after the vector search has narrowed down candidates.

The two-stage process looks like this:

  1. Fast retrieval (stage 1): Use the embedding-based vector search to quickly find the top-k similar queries.
  2. Careful verification (stage 2): Pass each candidate through a cross-encoder to re-rank them based on intent-level similarity and only keep matches above a higher confidence threshold.

By combining these methods, we get the best of both worlds: speed from embeddings and precision from cross-encoders. This ensures our cache doesn’t just retrieve similar-looking queries but truly equivalent-intent ones. Here is one example of how to do this in code:

class CrossEncoderSemanticCache(MaskedSemanticCache):
    def __init__(
        self, embedder_model='all-MiniLM-L6-v2', collection_ref=None
    ):
        super().__init__(embedder_model=embedder_model,
            collection_ref=collection_ref)
        self.verifier = CrossEncoder(
            'cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2')
    def search_with_verification(
        self, query, vector_threshold=0.7, verify_threshold=3.5
    ):
        """Two-stage search: vector similarity + verification"""
        masked_query = self.mask_entities(query)
        embedding = self.embedder.encode(masked_query).tolist()
        results = self.collection.query(
            query_embeddings=[embedding], n_results=3)
        if not (results.get('distances') and results['distances'][0]):
            return None
        best_match, best_score = None, 0.0
        for i, distance in enumerate(results['distances'][0]):
            vector_score = 1 - distance
            if vector_score < vector_threshold:
                continue
            metadata = results['metadatas'][0][i]
            verify_score = float(self.verifier.predict(
                [[query, metadata.get('original_query',
                    metadata.get('query', ''))]]
            )[0])
            if verify_score > best_score 
                and verify_score >= verify_threshold:
                best_score = verify_score
                best_match = {
                    'soln_path': metadata['soln_path'],
                    'vector_score': vector_score,
                    'verify_score': verify_score,
                    'cached_query': metadata.get('original_query',
                        metadata.get('query'))
                }
        return best_match

Here’s what happens in the preceding code block:

  1. Class: CrossEncoderSemanticCache extends the masked cache with a cross-encoder verifier.
  2. Init: The initialization method loads CrossEncoder('ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2') to score query-candidate pairs.
  3. Stage 1: Vector search quickly finds the top three candidates.
  4. Stage 2: The cross-encoder re-scores them for intent-level similarity.
  5. Result: The best match is returned only if both vector and verify thresholds are met.

With verification in place, we’ve reduced the chances of pulling the wrong response. But depending on the model and the type of query, we may still need to tune how strict we are when calling something a “match.” That’s where adaptive thresholds come in.

Step 6 – adaptive thresholds

Here, we’re addressing the fact that not all scenarios demand the same strictness. For mission-critical queries (such as financial transactions), we want a high threshold as it is better to miss a match than return the wrong one. For exploratory or “fuzzy” searches, we can lower the bar and allow more lenient matches.

The adaptive threshold system lets us dynamically adjust match criteria depending on the model, query type, or user preference. This prevents us from using a one-size-fits-all threshold that might be too strict in some cases and too loose in others. One way to do this in code is as follows:

class AdaptiveSemanticCache(CrossEncoderSemanticCache):
    def __init__(
        self, model_name='all-MiniLM-L6-v2', collection_ref=None
    ):
        super().__init__(
            embedder_model=model_name, collection_ref=collection_ref)
        self.model_name = model_name
        self.model_thresholds = {
            'all-MiniLM-L6-v2': 0.75,
            'all-mpnet-base-v2': 0.80,
            'all-distilroberta-v1': 0.70
        }
    def get_threshold(self, match_type='normal'):
        base = self.model_thresholds.get(self.model_name, 0.75)
        adjustments = {
            'exact': base + 0.15,
            'normal': base,
            'fuzzy': base - 0.10,
            'exploratory': base - 0.20
        }
        return adjustments.get(match_type, base)
    def adaptive_search(self, query, match_type='normal'):
        threshold = self.get_threshold(match_type)
        verify_threshold = 0.9 if match_type == 'exact' else 0.85
        return self.search_with_verification(
            query,
            vector_threshold=threshold,
            verify_threshold=verify_threshold
        )

Here’s what the preceding code does:

  1. Class: The AdaptiveSemanticCache class extends CrossEncoderSemanticCache with model-specific base thresholds.
  2. Thresholds: The get_threshold() method adjusts the vector cutoff to be stricter for exact match types and looser for fuzzy/exploratory match types. Fuzzy and exploratory matches use lower similarity thresholds to allow more lenient matching, which is useful for broader searches where finding related content is more important than ensuring a precise match.
  3. Adaptive search: The adaptive_search method uses both the adjusted vector threshold and a raised verify cutoff (3.5 on a raw scale).
  4. Result: The same query can be tested under different strictness levels to balance recall versus precision.

So far, our cache retrieves smarter and filters better. But what happens when a query is completely new and not yet in the cache? We don’t want to stop there; we want the cache to learn automatically from its misses. That’s the motivation for auto-population with fallback.

Step 7 – auto-population with fallback

Caching is only powerful if it grows with experience. Instead of relying on manual population, we can hook the cache up to a fallback mechanism, such as calling an agent, an LLM, or a custom function, whenever there’s a cache miss. The result of that fallback call is then immediately added back into the cache, so the next time a similar query comes in, it’s served instantly.

This makes the cache self-learning: every miss today becomes a hit tomorrow. Over time, the hit rate improves dramatically, saving even more calls to expensive backends. The stats tracker we added helps you see this evolution in real time, showing hits, misses, and auto-added entries.

class SmartSemanticCache(AdaptiveSemanticCache):
    def __init__(
        self, model_name='all-MiniLM-L6-v2', collection_ref=None
    ):
        super().__init__(
            model_name=model_name, collection_ref=collection_ref)
        self.stats = {'hits': 0, 'misses': 0, 'auto_added': 0}
    def query_with_fallback(
        self, query, fallback_fn=None, match_type='normal'
    ):
        """Try cache first, fallback to function if miss"""
        result = self.adaptive_search(query, match_type)
        if result:
            self.stats['hits'] += 1
            return result['soln_path'], 'cache'
        self.stats['misses'] += 1
        if fallback_fn:
            soln_path = fallback_fn(query)
            self.add(query, soln_path)
            self.stats['auto_added'] += 1
            return soln_path, 'computed'
        return None, 'miss'
    def print_stats(self):
        total = self.stats['hits'] + self.stats['misses']
        if total > 0:
            hit_rate = self.stats['hits'] / total * 100
            print("Cache Stats:")
            print(f"  Hits: {self.stats['hits']} ({hit_rate:.1f}%)")
            print(f"  Misses: {self.stats['misses']}")
            print(f"  Auto-added: {self.stats['auto_added']}")
# Mock agent function
def mock_agent(query):
    """Simulate an expensive agent call"""
    q = query.lower()

    elif 'savings' in q and 'balance' in q:

    elif 'balance' in q:
        return 'balance_tool'  # Generic balance query
    elif 'transaction' in q:
        return 'transaction_tool'
    else:
        return 'general_tool'

Population – query expansion strategies

Now that we’ve explored how semantic caches retrieve matches, let’s return to the second of our three core policies: population. While retrieval determines how well we match incoming queries to cached entries, population strategies determine what queries enter the cache in the first place, and more critically, how we expand that initial set to achieve comprehensive coverage.

To achieve high cache coverage, semantic caches must anticipate the myriad ways users express the same underlying need. Query expansion transforms a limited set of cached queries into a comprehensive semantic net that can catch most variations users might produce. In the upcoming sections, we discuss some of the more popular techniques for expanding your semantic cache. But watch out! Check out the following pro tip and make sure you take the proper precautions when expanding your semantic cache.

Pro tip!

For all of these techniques, be sure to check your new queries against existing queries in the semantic cache (by running the same vector search you would run for the semantic cache matches). If the query matches above the threshold for an existing query, and (importantly) both queries have the same solution path, then you do not need to add that particular query to the semantic cache, as it is already well semantically represented. It is important to understand that adding a query to the semantic cache could cause issues with your queries eventually, conflicting with queries that have other solution paths, significantly undermining the effectiveness of your semantic cache. If the solution paths are different, though, then you definitely do want to include the query, or your users will end up matching to the wrong solution through that query.

LLM-based paraphrasing

Modern semantic caches leverage LLMs to generate natural paraphrases of queries. This approach produces diverse rewordings while preserving the original intent. For a financial query such as “How do I invest in bonds?”, an LLM might generate variations such as “What’s the process for purchasing bonds?”, “How can I buy bonds?”, and “What steps are involved in bond investment?” Each paraphrase maintains the core intent while varying the linguistic expression.

Back-translation for natural variation

Back-translation remains one of the most effective techniques for generating semantically equivalent queries with natural linguistic variation. By translating a query through one or more intermediate languages and back to the source language, the system produces paraphrases that maintain meaning while varying structure:

  • English → German → English: Produces structural variations due to German’s different word order
  • English → Japanese → English: Generates high semantic diversity due to fundamental linguistic differences
  • English → French → Russian → English: Compound transformations that yield significant rephrasing

Different language families contribute unique transformation patterns. For example, romance languages (Spanish, French, and Italian) excel at stylistic variation, while Sino-Tibetan languages introduce high paraphrase diversity. The key is selecting language pairs that maximize useful variation while maintaining semantic fidelity.

Synonym and lexical expansion

At the word level, semantic caches expand coverage through systematic synonym replacement and abbreviation handling. This is particularly crucial in specialized domains such as finance, where technical terms, acronyms, and colloquialisms abound:

  • “401(k) contribution” “retirement plan contribution”
  • “EPS” “earnings per share”
  • “Treasurys” “US government bonds”
  • “municipal bonds” “munis”

These lexical expansions prove invaluable when users approach the same concept from different angles or expertise levels. For example, a novice investor might search for “retirement savings,” while a financial advisor queries “qualified plan allocations,” yet both seek similar underlying information. By maintaining comprehensive synonym mappings and domain-specific abbreviation dictionaries, semantic caches can recognize these equivalent expressions without requiring separate processing for each variation. This word-level understanding forms the foundation for more sophisticated query matching, but true semantic comprehension requires moving beyond simple substitutions to generating entirely new query formulations that anticipate user needs.

Synthetic query generation

Modern semantic caches don’t simply wait for users to provide query variations, they proactively generate likely questions before they’re asked. This anticipatory approach recognizes that users often struggle to formulate the exact query that will retrieve the information they need, especially in technical domains where multiple phrasings might express the same intent.

These systems employ several generation strategies. Document-based generation analyzes existing content to derive questions that the material could answer. Pattern-based generation identifies query sequences from user behavior; for example, when hundreds of users who search for “IRA contribution limits” subsequently ask about “catch-up contributions,” the system learns to pre-generate and cache that logical follow-up. Some implementations use domain models to generate technically relevant variations, understanding that a query about “portfolio rebalancing” likely relates to “asset allocation” and “risk adjustment.”

Morgan Stanley’s evolution from 7,000 fixed queries to handling “effectively any question” demonstrates the power of this approach. Their system didn’t just memorize more question-answer pairs; it learned to generate thousands of plausible financial queries by understanding the relationships between concepts, common user needs, and the structure of financial inquiries. However, this systematic expansion of query space introduces a critical problem: without domain-specific constraints, these systems risk generating plausible-sounding but fundamentally incorrect equivalences. The next section examines how specialized domains such as finance must carefully restrict which semantic connections their caches are allowed to make.

Domain-specific constraints

The flexibility that makes semantic caches powerful also creates risks in specialized domains. For example, with financial systems, you must recognize critical distinctions that general-purpose models might overlook. While “EBITDA” and “earnings” both relate to company profitability, they represent fundamentally different metrics. EBITDA excludes interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization to show operational performance, while “earnings” might refer to net income, EPS, or other specific measures.

These constraints require semantic caches to implement domain-aware boundaries. The system needs enough intelligence to recognize that “Fed” means “Federal Reserve” and “munis” means “municipal bonds,” and it must also maintain strict distinctions between terms such as “gross” and “net,” or “realized” and “unrealized” gains. Financial semantic caches typically enforce these boundaries through multiple mechanisms: automated validation checks that assess whether substitutions preserve query intent, hardcoded rules that prevent specific dangerous conflations, and expert review processes during training phases.

These are examples from the financial industry, which serves as a good example because it has many unique “rules” that can be applied. But all domains have similar unique aspects that need to be accounted for. Think through your domain and what unique rules apply there.

The challenge lies in finding the right balance. If it is too restrictive, the cache misses valid connections; if it is too permissive, it serves incorrect or misleading results. As caches accumulate these carefully validated entries over time, they face another critical challenge: determining which information remains valuable and which has outlived its usefulness. Even the most accurately cached financial answer becomes a liability when market conditions change or regulations update, making intelligent eviction policies essential for maintaining cache integrity.

Eviction – keeping a clean cache

With our cache now equipped with robust retrieval mechanisms and populated through various expansion strategies, we face the third critical policy challenge introduced at the beginning of this chapter: eviction. Unlike traditional caches, where eviction simply frees up space, semantic cache eviction must balance multiple concerns, maintaining coverage of the semantic space, preserving high-value entries, removing outdated information, and eliminating redundancies that accumulate through aggressive population strategies.

Maintaining cache quality requires intelligent eviction policies that navigate these competing demands. The challenge is particularly acute in semantic caches because the relationships between entries are complex and multidimensional. Removing one query might leave a gap in semantic coverage that affects dozens of related queries. Conversely, keeping everything leads to bloated caches filled with outdated, redundant, or poor-performing entries that degrade overall system performance.

The eviction strategies that follow represent increasingly sophisticated approaches to this challenge, from simple time-based rules to complex semantic analysis that identifies and eliminates redundancy while preserving essential coverage. Each approach offers different trade-offs between computational complexity, cache quality, and coverage maintenance. We will start with time-based eviction.

Time-based eviction

The simplest eviction strategy uses time-to-live (TTL) values, removing entries older than a specified duration. However, semantic caches benefit from more nuanced temporal handling:

  • Evergreen content (definitions and procedures) may have no expiration
  • Periodic content (quarterly reports) expires on a known schedule
  • Dynamic content (market conditions) requires short TTLs or real-time validation

While time-based eviction provides a straightforward baseline, it fails to account for actual usage patterns or the semantic value of cached entries. This limitation drives the need for more intelligent approaches that consider how entries are actually used. The next approach, least recently used (LRU) with semantic decay, addresses this by combining usage statistics with temporal factors and semantic drift measurements.

Least recently used with semantic decay

LRU is a classic cache eviction strategy that removes the entries that haven’t been accessed for the longest time, based on the principle that recently used items are more likely to be needed again soon. In traditional caching, LRU simply tracks when each entry was last accessed and evicts the oldest ones when space is needed. However, for semantic caches, we can enhance this approach by adding “semantic decay”, which is a measure of how the meaning and relevance of cached content degrades over time.

Traditional LRU eviction can be enhanced with semantic decay factors. Entries lose relevance not just through disuse but through semantic drift as language and domain knowledge evolve. This is what a function might look like that handles these various aspects:

def calculate_eviction_score(entry):
    age_factor = time_decay(entry.timestamp)
    usage_factor = 1 / (entry.hit_count + 1)
    semantic_factor = semantic_drift_score(entry.embedding)
    return age_factor * usage_factor * semantic_factor

This combined scoring recognizes that cache entries exist in a changing semantic landscape. Yet even sophisticated scoring may not catch all problems; some entries might be frequently accessed but consistently fail to satisfy user needs. Performance-based pruning tackles this disconnect by tracking not just how often entries are used but how well they actually serve users when retrieved.

Performance-based pruning

Sophisticated eviction policies track not just usage but effectiveness. Entries with high retrieval rates but poor user satisfaction scores may indicate semantic drift or inappropriate matches. These problematic entries represent a particular challenge; they appear successful by traditional metrics (high hit rate and recent usage) but actually degrade the user experience.

Modern implementations collect various performance signals to identify these troubled entries. User feedback mechanisms such as thumbs up/down ratings provide direct quality signals. Indirect metrics such as query reformulation rates (when users immediately rephrase a query after receiving a cached response) or session abandonment patterns also indicate cache quality issues. Some systems even track downstream task completion: Did the cached response actually help the user accomplish their goal?

The system can handle underperforming entries through multiple strategies. Immediate pruning removes clearly problematic entries that consistently fail quality thresholds. Graduated decay accelerates the eviction timeline for borderline performers. Manual review queues flag ambiguous cases for human evaluation, which is particularly valuable in specialized domains where automated metrics might miss nuance. This performance-based approach ensures the cache evolves based on real-world effectiveness rather than theoretical metrics.

While performance-based pruning eliminates poor-quality entries, it doesn’t address another growing problem in semantic caches: redundancy. The final eviction strategy we’ll examine uses semantic clustering to identify and consolidate duplicate entries that waste resources and create inconsistency.

Semantic clustering for redundancy elimination

As the cache grows, redundant entries covering the same semantic space waste resources and can cause inconsistent responses. Periodic clustering identifies groups of highly similar entries:

def remove_redundancy(cache_entries, similarity_threshold=0.95):
    clusters = cluster_embeddings(cache_entries)
    for cluster in clusters:
        if cluster.max_similarity &gt; similarity_threshold:

This consolidation maintains coverage while reducing the cache size and improving consistency. With these eviction strategies working in concert, time-based, usage-aware, performance-driven, and redundancy-eliminating, semantic caches can maintain high quality even as they scale. Let’s now step back and consider what we’ve learned about semantic caching as a whole.

Summary

Semantic caches are no longer just an optimization trick. They are an essential control layer for production-grade AI systems. By intercepting common queries and reusing solution paths, they offset the cost and latency of agent-based reasoning while enforcing consistency in responses. Whether it’s skipping the planning phase for repeated queries, generalizing with entity masking, or ensuring correctness through verification, the cache becomes a force multiplier that transforms how agents operate under real-world scale.

What makes semantic caches particularly powerful is how adaptable they are. From long-tail coverage strategies to adaptive thresholds, from auto-population to redundancy elimination, they evolve with your application’s needs and your users’ behaviors. They aren’t static lookup tables, but dynamic systems that balance speed, precision, and freshness. When designed well, they don’t just reduce compute; in fact, they shape the user experience, delivering answers that are fast, reliable, and consistent, even as query patterns shift.

The real takeaway is that semantic caches are living infrastructure. Their value grows as they learn, refine, and integrate more deeply into your agent pipelines. The most successful implementations are the ones that continuously monitor cache health, tune thresholds, and update eviction strategies to keep responses sharp and trustworthy. In practice, a well-tuned semantic cache fades into the background. It’s invisible to the user but indispensable to the system, ensuring that your AI application is not only efficient but also dependable at scale.

But caching only takes you so far. A semantic cache is about remembering how to answer common queries, not what else the agent has already seen and done. To handle richer conversations, long-term context, and more dynamic reasoning, we need to move beyond caching into memory. That’s where the next chapter takes us, as it deals with agentic memory. It is the system of working, episodic, semantic, and procedural memories that lets agents learn from past interactions, recall the right information at the right time, and ultimately operate more like intelligent collaborators than stateless tools.
